# Buffett's Acquisition & Investment Evaluation: Case Study Compilation

How Warren Buffett actually analyzed businesses before buying or rejecting them -- extracted from shareholder letters (1977-2025).

---

## Part 1: Successful Acquisitions -- The Analysis Process

### Case 1: Nebraska Furniture Mart (NFM) -- Acquired 1983

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Single-store annual sales: over $100 million (the largest home furnishings store in the U.S.)
- Operating expense ratio: 16.5% of sales vs. Levitz's 35.6% -- nearly half the cost structure
- Customer savings: approximately $30 million annually compared to what goods would cost elsewhere
- Growth trajectory: sales grew from $44 million to $132 million over a decade in a slow-growth market with modest goods inflation
- Geographic pull: by the mid-1980s, NFM was the #3 most popular furniture retailer in Des Moines, Iowa -- 130 miles away, while 19 competitors were local

**What comparisons he made:**
- Compared NFM's 16.5% expense ratio directly against Levitz (35.6%) and Circuit City (25%)
- Compared the store's economics to other Berkshire businesses: "Two paper routes in Buffalo had more economic goodwill than a massive factory that once employed 1,000 people"

**The deal-maker:**
- The virtuous circle business model: low costs enabled low prices, low prices drove high volume, high volume funded enormous selection, and broader selection pulled customers from ever-wider geographies
- Mrs. B (Rose Blumkin) herself -- started the business at age 44 with $500, still working at 95. "I'd rather wrestle grizzlies than compete with Mrs. B and her progeny"
- The Blumkin formula: (1) extraordinary enthusiasm and energy, (2) extraordinary realism about competence boundaries, (3) ignore even enticing propositions outside that area, (4) unfailing high-grade integrity
- Trust as due diligence substitute: "We gave Mrs. B a check for $55 million and she gave us her word. That made for an even exchange." No audit, no inventory check, no verification of receivables, no property title checks

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Purchase price: $55 million for 90% of the business
- Sales: $100M+ from a single store (1983), growing to $132M by mid-1980s
- Operating costs: 16.5% of sales (vs. industry 35-40%)
- Customer savings: ~$30 million/year vs. competitors' prices

**Source letters:** 1983, 1984, 1987, 1988, 1989, 1990

### Case 2: See's Candies -- Acquired 1972

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Return on tangible assets: earned approximately $4 million pre-tax on only $8 million of net tangible assets -- a 50% pre-tax return
- Capital requirements: minimal reinvestment needed to grow. From 1972 to 1999, See's earned $857 million pre-tax while requiring very little additional capital
- The full economics by 2014: $25 million purchase generated $1.9 billion in cumulative pre-tax earnings with only $40 million of additional capital investment
- Product quality: refused to add preservatives or freeze products despite production challenges; 40% of annual volume and 75% of profits concentrated in four weeks before Christmas
- Customer loyalty: when See's was about to lose its Albuquerque store, 263 customers voluntarily wrote letters protesting the closure

**What comparisons he made:**
- See's ($8M tangible assets earning 25%+) vs. hypothetical mundane business ($18M tangible assets earning 11%) -- both producing the same dollar earnings but See's far more valuable, especially under inflation
- Under inflation: when prices double, See's needs only $8M more capital while the asset-heavy business needs $18M more
- See's per-pound costs tracked obsessively as a unit economics measure

**The deal-maker:**
- Consumer franchise creating pricing power: customers chose See's over competitors' candy costing two to three times as much
- Asset-light model: high returns on small tangible asset base meant nearly all earnings were free cash
- "One-of-a-kind product personality" combining taste, moderate price, controlled distribution, and exceptional service
- "Cheerful, helpful personnel are as much a trademark of See's as is the logo on the box"

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Purchase price: $25 million (1972)
- Pre-tax earnings at purchase: $4 million on $8 million net tangible assets
- Cumulative pre-tax earnings by 1999: $857 million
- Cumulative pre-tax earnings by 2014: $1.9 billion
- Additional capital invested over 42 years: only $40 million
- Operating margin (1999): record 24%

**Source letters:** 1983, 1984, 1987, 1989, 1993, 1999, 2014, 2020

### Case 3: GEICO -- Full Acquisition 1996

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Cost structure: operating costs at 23.5% of premiums (1986) vs. competitors 15+ percentage points higher. By 2015, underwriting expenses were 14.7% vs. ~25% for State Farm/Allstate and ~40% for agency-based insurers
- Source of cost advantage: direct-to-consumer model eliminating the agent distribution layer -- a structural advantage embedded in the business model, not just operational efficiency
- Market share trajectory: 2.5% (1995) growing steadily to 8.1% (2009), 10.8% (2014), 11.4% (2015)
- Customer acquisition economics: word-of-mouth provided over one million referrals annually; 65% of Executive Jet's new owners came as referrals
- Self-reinforcing flywheel: low costs --> low prices --> more customers --> more referrals --> lower per-unit acquisition costs --> even lower costs

**What comparisons he made:**
- GEICO's expense ratio vs. every major competitor by name (Allstate, State Farm, agency-based insurers)
- Compared GEICO to the cereal industry: "Kellogg, General Mills earn double the return on capital of auto insurers" -- to understand why industry structure matters
- Compared the investment to a "wonderful business at a fair price" framework

**The deal-maker:**
- Structural cost advantage that was "just as potent in 1995 as when Buffett first discovered it in 1951" -- 44 years of durability
- "Others may copy our model, but they will be unable to replicate our economics"
- Competitors could see the advantage but could not abandon their agent-based distribution because it was too deeply ingrained
- The low-cost position created a widening moat: "The difference between GEICO's costs and those of its competitors is a kind of moat that protects a valuable and much-sought-after business castle"

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Price paid for remaining 49%: $2.3 billion (1996)
- Float growth: from $2.7 billion at full acquisition to enormous sums thereafter
- Market share growth: 2.5% to 11.4% over 20 years
- Cost advantage: 14.7% expense ratio vs. 25-40% for competitors
- Marketing spend escalation: $33 million (1995) to $242 million (1999), planned $300-350 million (2000)
- Goodwill at purchase: $2.7 billion book; estimated $14 billion real economic value by 2010

**Source letters:** 1980, 1986, 1988, 1995, 1996, 1997, 1999, 2009, 2010, 2014, 2015, 2020

### Case 4: Coca-Cola -- Stock Investment Beginning 1988

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Global dominance: 44% worldwide soft drink market share (1993)
- Product durability: unchanged core product since 1886. A 1938 Fortune article declared an investor was "looking too late" -- yet from 1938 to 1993, $40 invested grew to $25,000
- Growth trajectory: 207 million cases sold in 1938 grew to 10.7 billion cases by 1993 -- a 50-fold increase after being called "saturated"
- Dividend compounding: purchased for $1.3 billion (completed 1994); dividends grew from $75 million to $704 million annually by 2022
- Management quality: Roberto Goizueta and Don Keough took over in 1981 when Coca-Cola had stagnated for a decade and grew market value from $4.4 billion to $58 billion in under 13 years

**What comparisons he made:**
- Coke as an "Inevitable" -- "companies whose worldwide dominance no sensible observer, not even their competitors, could question for an investment lifetime"
- Compared to holding a 30-year bond: a $1.3 billion bond would represent only 0.3% of Berkshire's net worth by 2022, delivering unchanged $80 million annually, while Coke delivered $704 million and the position was worth $25 billion
- The "moat" comparison: "The might of their brand names, the attributes of their products, and the strength of their distribution systems give them an enormous competitive advantage"

**The deal-maker:**
- Product that "travels" -- became a recognizable name worldwide, addressing the timeless human need for liquid consumption
- Buffett had observed its extraordinary consumer appeal since 1936 -- "Only in the summer of 1988 did my brain finally establish contact with my eyes"
- "Buy commodities, sell brands" formula that produced enormous sustained profits for over a century
- The power of inaction: Berkshire did not buy or sell a single share for over two decades. By 2023, Berkshire's share of annual AMEX and Coke earnings considerably exceeded the entire original purchase costs

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Total investment: $1.3 billion (completed 1994)
- Initial annual dividends: $75 million
- Dividends by 2022: $704 million annually
- Position value by 2022: $25 billion
- Goizueta-era market value growth: $4.4B to $58B in <13 years
- Global market share: 44% of soft drinks (1993)

**Source letters:** 1988, 1989, 1993, 1996, 2010, 2022, 2023

### Case 5: American Express -- Stock Investment

**What Buffett looked at:**
- The 1964 salad oil scandal as the key analytical moment: GEICO's near-bankruptcy (1976) was comparable -- both were "one-of-a-kind franchises temporarily reeling from a fixable problem"
- Core competitive advantage: unquestioned financial trust built over 170+ years, serving a timeless human need
- Products that "travel" globally -- worldwide brand recognition
- Dividend compounding: initial dividends of $41 million grew to $302 million annually

**The deal-maker:**
- Distinguishing temporary problems from permanent ones: the salad oil scandal was "a localized excisable cancer" -- the underlying franchise was unharmed
- The franchise was built on trust -- "unquestioned financial trust" that competitors could not replicate
- Like Coca-Cola, American Express addressed a timeless need and its products traveled globally
- Buffett described the returns as "110% luck, the balance skill"

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Original investment: $1.3 billion
- Initial dividends: $41 million/year
- Dividends by 2022: $302 million/year
- By 2023: annual earnings share exceeded the entire original purchase cost

**Source letters:** 1980, 1993, 1997, 2022, 2023

### Case 6: Washington Post -- Stock Investment 1973

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Purchase price vs. intrinsic value: bought at one-fourth of per-share business value
- Media franchise strength: measured by penetration ratio (readers per household), not just revenue
- The Buffalo News analogy: #1 weekday penetration ratio among the 100 largest U.S. papers; 50% news hole vs. industry average in the high 30s

**The deal-maker:**
- The advantage was "attitudinal, not analytical" -- most professionals could have calculated the Post's value, but institutional investors, "under the spell of efficient market theory academics, considered business value irrelevant"
- Triple dip return: (1) business value soared upward, (2) per-share value increased faster due to stock repurchases, and (3) the discount to intrinsic value narrowed

**Source letters:** 1985

---

### Case 7: Wells Fargo -- Stock Investment 1990

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Valuation: stock fell almost 50% on California real estate fears; purchased at less than 5x after-tax earnings
- Return on equity: 20%+ ROE
- Management quality: Carl Reichardt and Paul Hazen reminded him of Tom Murphy and Dan Burke at Capital Cities/ABC

**What comparisons he made:**
- Reichardt/Hazen shared four traits with Murphy/Burke: (1) partnership stronger than sum of parts, (2) pay able people well but refuse unnecessary headcount, (3) attack costs in good times AND bad, (4) stick with what they understand

**The deal-maker:**
- "We have no interest in purchasing shares of a poorly-managed bank at a 'cheap' price. Instead, our only interest is in buying into well-managed banks at fair prices"
- Pessimism itself was the opportunity: "It's optimism that is the enemy of the rational buyer"
- In leveraged businesses (banks at 20:1), management quality matters more than purchase price

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Purchase price: less than 5x after-tax earnings
- ROE: 20%+
- Stock had fallen ~50% from peak

**Source letters:** 1990

### Case 8: BNSF Railroad -- Acquired 2009

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Structural cost advantage: moves each ton of freight 500 miles on a single gallon of diesel -- three times more fuel-efficient than trucking. This is rooted in physics, not management tricks
- Essential infrastructure: provides a service the economy cannot function without
- Capital intensity: requires massive ongoing investment ($5.8-6 billion annually), but returns were acceptable for Berkshire's cash-generation needs
- Regulatory relationship: operates under a social compact -- company provides first-class service, regulators allow fair return on capital

**The deal-maker:**
- Physics-based cost advantage that is permanently structural and impossible to replicate
- Berkshire had evolved: "Charlie and I historically shunned capital-intensive businesses, but now embrace utilities and railroads because Berkshire generates more cash than asset-light businesses can absorb"
- Payment: approximately 70% cash, 30% stock -- only 6.1% dilution to shares outstanding
- Counter-cyclical timing: acquired during the 2008-2009 crisis when Berkshire deployed $15.5 billion while others were begging for government help

**Specific numbers cited:**
- Annual capital expenditure: $5.8-6 billion (2014-2015)
- Pre-tax income: $6.8 billion (2015 record)
- Share dilution from acquisition: only 6.1%
- Fuel efficiency: 500 miles per gallon per ton vs. ~167 for trucking

**Source letters:** 2009, 2010, 2014, 2015, 2023

### Case 9: Precision Castparts -- Acquired 2015

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Market position: "the world's premier supplier of aerospace components"
- Management quality: CEO Mark Donegan described as "the da Vinci of his craft" who transforms ordinary raw materials into extraordinary products
- Business model: Berkshire's approach was to "buy businesses already run by cost-conscious, efficient managers rather than buying and trying to fix them"

**The deal-maker:**
- World-class operator in Mark Donegan
- Premier market position in a critical, specialized industry
- Fit Berkshire's model of buying excellent businesses and leaving management alone

**What went wrong (see Part 2 -- Mistakes):**
- Buffett later admitted he was "too optimistic about PCC's normalized profit potential" and took an $11 billion write-down in 2020

**Source letters:** 2015, 2017, 2020

---

### Case 10: FlightSafety International -- Acquired 1996

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Management: CEO Al Ueltschi, age 79, had spent decades building the company with personal passion
- Owner-manager mindset: Ueltschi exemplified the principle that "the best business managers are those who run their operations as if they personally own them"
- Capital requirements: $272 million annual spending on flight simulators -- depreciation was a real and significant cost

**The deal-maker:**
- The owner-manager mentality: "We never fool with success"
- Ueltschi would continue running operations exactly as before
- Demonstrated the principle that the person running the operation matters as much as the economics

**Source letters:** 1996, 2000

### Case 11: Dairy Queen -- Acquired 1997 (via American Dairy Queen)

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Brand recognition and customer loyalty
- Improving profits even as sales declined during the 2009 recession -- demonstrating disciplined cost management

**Source letters:** 2009

### Case 12: Japanese Trading Companies -- Stock Investments Beginning 2019

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Capital allocation discipline: each company reduced outstanding shares at attractive prices
- Management compensation: far less aggressive than U.S. norms
- Dividend policy: paid only about one-third of earnings as dividends, retaining the rest for growth and buybacks
- Share issuance: reluctant to issue shares, avoiding dilution
- Values alignment: "impressed not just with cheap stock prices but with how they treat shareholders"

**What comparisons he made:**
- Compared their shareholder-friendly policies favorably to U.S. corporate norms
- The five companies (Itochu, Marubeni, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo) were evaluated as a group with shared cultural traits

**The deal-maker:**
- Culture and governance alignment with Berkshire's own values
- Natural currency hedge: financed mostly with yen-denominated bonds at fixed rates, generating $812 million in annual dividends vs. $135 million in interest costs
- Dividend income far exceeded borrowing costs from day one
- Generated $1.9 billion gain from yen weakening (bonus from the hedge structure)

**Source letters:** 2023, 2024

---

### Case 13: Apple -- Stock Investment Beginning ~2016

**What Buffett looked at:**
- Share repurchase compounding: Apple's continuous buybacks increased Berkshire's ownership from 5.2% to 5.4% at no cost
- Combined with Berkshire's own repurchases, created a double-compounding dynamic
- "The math of repurchases grinds away slowly, but can be powerful over time"

**The deal-maker:**
- Brand power and customer loyalty (a non-essential consumer product where "the customer is the ultimate authority")
- Self-reinforcing buyback dynamic amplifying Berkshire's effective ownership
- Fit the "wonderful business at a fair price" criterion

**Source letters:** 2020, 2022

## Part 2: Failed, Rejected, and Regretted Deals

### Case 14: Berkshire Hathaway (Textiles) -- The Original Sin (1962)

**What happened:**
- Buffett bought shares because they were statistically cheap -- the "cigar butt" approach. The stock was trading below book value and below replacement cost of assets
- He eventually took control out of spite over an eighth of a point when management tried to squeeze him on a tender offer
- "Through Seabury's and my childish behavior -- after all, what was an eighth of a point to either of us? -- he lost his job, and I found myself with more than 25% of BPL's capital invested in a terrible business about which I knew very little"

**What he learned -- the analysis failures:**
- Book value and replacement cost were meaningless: equipment with $13 million original cost, $866,000 book value, and $30-50 million replacement cost sold at auction for only $163,122. Looms purchased for $5,000 each sold for $26 as scrap
- Capital investments that looked like winners individually failed collectively: "When every competitor invests to reduce costs, the savings get competed away through lower prices. Each round of investment puts more money in the game while returns remain anemic -- like everyone at a parade standing on tiptoes"
- Brilliant management (Ken Chace, Garry Morrison) could not overcome bad industry economics: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for poor fundamental economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact"
- He stayed too long due to emotional attachment: "I believed what I preferred to believe" while 250 other textile mills processed the same information more objectively
- Estimated cost of the structural error: approximately $100 billion (2014 estimate of what it cost to put the original NICO insurance purchase under Berkshire rather than his partnership)

**The lesson crystallized:** "Should you find yourself in a chronically-leaking boat, energy devoted to changing vessels is likely to be more productive than energy devoted to patching leaks"

**Source letters:** 1979, 1983, 1985, 1989, 2010, 2014

---

### Case 15: Waumbec Mills -- Acquisition Compounding the Textile Error

**What happened:**
- After already struggling with Berkshire's textile operations, Buffett acquired Waumbec Mills expecting "important synergy"
- His own verdict: "We also made a major acquisition, Waumbec Mills, with the expectation of important synergy (a term widely used in business to explain an acquisition that otherwise makes no sense)"

**The lesson:** Doubling down on a declining business -- using "synergy" as justification -- is the dumbest thing a manager can do

**Source letters:** 1985, 2014

---

### Case 16: Dexter Shoes -- The Worst Acquisition (1993)

**What he evaluated (and got wrong):**
- Dexter and H.H. Brown were both manufacturing shoes domestically when 93% of U.S. shoes came from abroad
- He believed "ingenious management and a skilled workforce can make domestic operations highly competitive even against low-cost foreign competitors"
- He assured founders Harold Alfond and Peter Lunder full operational autonomy: "We do not tell .400 hitters how to swing"

**What went wrong -- the analysis failures:**
- Misjudged the durability of domestic manufacturing in a labor-intensive commodity product: "extremely low-cost labor is the rule" abroad
- The product lacked differentiation to command a premium over imports
- Paid $433 million -- and made the catastrophic error of paying in Berkshire stock
- The Berkshire shares used for payment later became worth $5.7 billion -- "a candidate for the Guinness Book of World Records as a financial disaster"

**The compounded mistake:**
- Bad business judgment (buying a deteriorating competitive position) PLUS bad financial structure (using undervalued Berkshire stock as currency)
- "I was wrong in originally purchasing the stock" -- but compounding it by issuing stock made it orders of magnitude worse
- "No one misled me in any way -- I was simply too optimistic"

**Source letters:** 1993, 1999, 2000, 2014

### Case 17: USAir -- The Investment He Couldn't Get Rid Of

**What he evaluated (and got wrong):**
- Invested in convertible preferred stock -- a defensive structure he used when he "could not forecast an industry's future" (agnostic, not atheist)
- "I was so beguiled by the company's long history of profitable operations, and by the protection that ownership of a senior security seemingly offered me, that I overlooked the crucial point"

**The crucial point he missed:**
- Past performance was earned under regulation: "revenues would face fierce unregulated competition while costs remained bloated from the regulatory era"
- "A company's track record under one set of industry conditions tells you nothing about its prospects when conditions fundamentally change"
- In a commodity business, "it's impossible to be a lot smarter than your dumbest competitor" -- kamikaze pricing by competitors dragged the entire industry down

**Failed exit attempts:**
- "I tried once more to unload our holdings... You're lucky: I again failed in my attempt to snatch defeat from the jaws of victory"
- "I was wrong in originally purchasing the stock, and I was wrong later, in repeatedly trying to unload our holdings at 50 cents on the dollar"
- Humorous self-assessment: "Two changes at the company coincided with its remarkable rebound: (1) Charlie and I left the board of directors and (2) Stephen Wolf became CEO"

**Key insight:** "If history supplied all of the answers, the Forbes 400 would consist of librarians"

**Source letters:** 1989, 1990, 1996, 1997

---

### Case 18: Tesco -- The Cockroach in the Kitchen (2014)

**What happened:**
- Berkshire invested in the UK grocery chain and watched problems worsen month by month
- Market share fell, margins contracted, and accounting problems surfaced
- Buffett soured on management but was too slow to act

**The analysis failure -- delayed action:**
- "You see a cockroach in your kitchen; as the days go by, you meet his relatives"
- Charlie Munger called the delay "thumb-sucking"
- Lost $444 million by being too slow to sell after recognizing the problems
- "The cardinal sin is delaying the correction of mistakes"

**The lesson:** When warning signs appear, act quickly. The cost of delayed action almost always exceeds the cost of acting on incomplete information. The first cockroach is never the last.

**Source letters:** 2014

---

### Case 19: Precision Castparts -- Overpaying for Excellence (2015/2020)

**What happened:**
- Bought "a fine company -- the best in its business" with excellent management (Mark Donegan)
- Took an $11 billion write-down in 2020 -- "almost entirely the quantification of a mistake I made in 2016"

**The analysis failure:**
- "I was too optimistic about normalized profit potential"
- Even buying a wonderful company with wonderful management does not protect against overpaying
- "No one misled me in any way"
- "PCC is far from my first error of that sort. But it's a big one."

**The lesson:** Valuation discipline matters as much as business quality. A wonderful company at the wrong price is not a wonderful investment.

**Source letters:** 2015, 2020

---

### Case 20: Hochschild Kohn (Department Store) -- Early Mistake

**What happened:**
- Purchased a "third-place department store" as a bargain -- part of the "cigar butt" approach
- Had able, honest managers who could not overcome poor economics

**The lesson:** "In a difficult business, no sooner is one problem solved than another surfaces -- never is there just one cockroach in the kitchen"

**Source letters:** 1989

## Part 3: The Evaluation Framework -- Synthesized

From these cases, Buffett's actual evaluation process can be distilled:

### The Five Questions He Always Asks

1. **Is this a wonderful business or a mediocre one?**
   - Return on tangible assets (See's: 50%+ pre-tax return)
   - Capital requirements for growth (See's: $40M additional over 42 years)
   - Pricing power (consumer franchise vs. commodity)
   - Cost position (GEICO: 14.7% vs. 25-40% for competitors)

2. **Is the competitive advantage durable and structural?**
   - Physics-based (BNSF: 3x fuel efficiency vs. trucks)
   - Business-model-based (GEICO: direct vs. agent distribution)
   - Brand-based (Coca-Cola: 44% global share, 100+ year brand)
   - NOT temporary efficiency or clever management

3. **Who is running it, and do I trust them?**
   - Demonstrated track record, not projections ("future projections are of little interest to us")
   - Owner-mentality ("think like owners" is the highest compliment)
   - Character assessment: "I knew I was dealing with a man of integrity and that's what counted"
   - History of honest dealing with stakeholders

4. **What am I paying relative to what I'm getting?**
   - Intrinsic value of future cash flows, not book value or replacement cost
   - "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price"
   - What currency am I using? (Cash preferred; stock issuance can be catastrophic -- see Dexter Shoes)

5. **What can go wrong, and can I survive it?**
   - Has the competitive environment changed? (USAir: regulation to deregulation)
   - Is this a temporary problem or a permanent one? (GEICO 1976 vs. Berkshire textiles)
   - "You see a cockroach in your kitchen; as the days go by, you meet his relatives"
   - Never risk what you have for what you don't need

### The Three Instant Disqualifiers

1. **Turnarounds and projections:** "Future projections are of little interest to us, nor are turnaround situations." Turnarounds seldom turn.
2. **Businesses he cannot understand:** "If there's lots of technology, we won't understand it." Circle of competence boundaries are sacred.
3. **Bad people:** "We've never succeeded in making a good deal with a bad person." Character is non-negotiable.

### The Key Metrics He Consistently Tracks

| Metric | What It Reveals | Example |
|--------|----------------|---------|
| Return on tangible assets | Business quality | See's: 50%+ pre-tax |
| Operating expense ratio | Cost advantage | NFM 16.5% vs. Levitz 35.6% |
| Capital required per $ of earnings growth | Capital efficiency | See's: $40M for $1.9B earnings |
| Market share trajectory | Moat widening or narrowing | GEICO: 2.5% to 11.4% |
| Owner earnings (not GAAP) | True cash generation | Earnings + depreciation - maintenance capex |
| Per-share intrinsic value growth | Shareholder value creation | Not total size |
| Dividend growth rate | Compounding power | Coke: $75M to $704M |

## Key Patterns Across All Cases

**Pattern 1: The best acquisitions required almost no due diligence.**
NFM (no audit, one-page contract), Borsheim's (no audited statements), Star Furniture (two-hour meeting), National Indemnity (15-minute conversation, 1.5-page agreement). When you trust the people and understand the business, elaborate process adds cost without value.

**Pattern 2: The worst mistakes involved buying commodity businesses because they looked cheap.**
Berkshire textiles, Waumbec Mills, Hochschild Kohn, USAir, Dexter Shoes. The "cigar butt" approach -- buying mediocre businesses at bargain prices -- failed consistently. Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre.

**Pattern 3: Errors of commission were less costly than errors of omission.**
Buffett repeatedly cited opportunities he understood but failed to act on ("thumb-sucking") as among his most expensive mistakes. Decisiveness when analysis is favorable matters enormously.

**Pattern 4: Using stock as acquisition currency magnified every mistake.**
Dexter Shoes paid with $433M in Berkshire stock that became worth $5.7B. "Charlie and I enjoy issuing Berkshire stock about as much as we relish prepping for a colonoscopy."

**Pattern 5: The referral chain produced the best deals.**
NFM (1983) led to referrals that produced R.C. Willey, Star Furniture, and Jordan's Furniture over 14+ years. "At Berkshire, our carefully-crafted acquisition strategy is simply to wait for the phone to ring."
