# See's Candies: How Buffett Evaluated It

Extracted from Berkshire Hathaway annual letters (1977-2020). Every number and method below comes directly from Buffett's own words.

---

## 1. Purchase Price and Terms

- **Year**: Beginning of 1972 (purchased by Blue Chip Stamps, later folded into Berkshire)
- **Purchase price**: $25 million
- **Seller's asking price**: $30 million. Buffett was "adamant about not going above $25 million." The seller caved. Buffett later admitted: "I almost blew the See's purchase... that $1.35 billion would have gone to somebody else." (2007 letter)
- **Net tangible assets at purchase**: $8 million
- **Pre-tax earnings at purchase**: $4 million (later cited as $4.2 million in the 1977 letter)
- **Implied goodwill paid**: $17 million ($25M price minus $8M tangible assets)
- **Price-to-pre-tax-earnings**: ~6x
- **Price-to-net-tangible-assets**: ~3x

## 3. Return on Invested Capital Calculations

Buffett used several ROIC frameworks across the years:

### At Purchase (1983 Appendix)
- Earnings: ~$2M after-tax on $8M net tangible assets = **25% after-tax return on tangible assets**
- Compared to a "mundane" business earning 11% on $18M of assets producing the same $2M
- The 25%-on-$8M business was far superior because it needed far less capital to grow

### 20-Year ROIC (1991 Letter)
- Total capital in business: $25M net worth ($7M original + $18M reinvested)
- Pre-tax earnings: $42.4M per year
- Pre-tax return on net worth: **170%**
- Cumulative distributions: $410M pre-tax on $18M of reinvested earnings

### 35-Year ROIC (2007 Letter)
- Purchase price: $25M
- Total reinvestment: $32M
- Total capital deployed: $57M ($25M purchase + $32M reinvestment)
- Cumulative pre-tax earnings: $1.35B
- **Return: $1.35B earned on $57M deployed = 23.7x cumulative return**

### 40-Year ROIC (2011 Letter)
- Pre-tax earnings in 2011: $83M
- Net carrying value: **below zero**
- Return on invested capital: effectively infinite
- Cumulative pre-tax earnings: $1.65B

### 42-Year ROIC (2014 Letter)
- Original purchase: $25M
- Additional capital invested: $40M
- Total invested: $65M
- Cumulative pre-tax earnings: $1.9B
- **Return: $1.9B on $65M = 29x cumulative**
- At-purchase returns: $4M pre-tax on $8M tangible assets = **50% pre-tax ROIC**

## 4. Pricing Power Evidence

### The Core Insight (1991 Letter)
- "See's had untapped pricing power" -- this was the key insight at purchase
- Profits grew from $4.2M to $42.4M over 20 years (10x) while industry had "practically no unit growth"
- The earnings growth came almost entirely from pricing power, not volume growth

### Price vs. Volume Dynamics
- **1977**: Pre-tax earnings tripled ($4.2M to $12.6M) "with little additional capital investment" in an industry with "practically no unit growth" -- meaning virtually all growth came from price increases
- **1983**: Customers preferred See's chocolate "to competitors' candy costing two or three times as much" -- demonstrating pricing was well below perceived value ceiling
- **1984**: 40% of annual volume and 75% of profits concentrated in four weeks before Christmas -- seasonal demand created pricing leverage
- **1989**: Tripled newspaper ad spending, achieved 8% increase in pounds sold including first same-store gains in years -- rare volume growth supplementing pricing power
- **2011**: "Whether the currency a century from now is based on gold, seashells, shark teeth, or a piece of paper, people will be willing to exchange a couple of minutes of their daily labor for... some See's peanut brittle" -- pricing power transcends monetary systems

### The Test
- Buffett's franchise test: "Can this company raise prices tomorrow without losing significant volume?" See's consistently passed this test for 50+ years

## 5. Franchise vs. Commodity Business

Buffett defined the distinction explicitly in the 1991 letter, using See's as the franchise archetype:

### Franchise Characteristics (See's)
1. **Product is needed or desired** -- chocolate/candy is a perennial want
2. **Customers believe it has no close substitute** -- See's was not interchangeable with other candy
3. **Not subject to price regulation** -- free to set prices based on perceived value
4. **Can tolerate mismanagement** -- franchise durability survives management mistakes
5. **Can price aggressively** -- pricing based on value to customer, not cost of production

### Commodity Characteristics (Anti-See's)
1. Earns exceptional profits only as low-cost operator or when supply is tight
2. Can be killed by poor management
3. Price set by market, not by the company
4. Any competitor can drag the whole industry down by pricing irrationally

### The Tax Test (1986 Letter)
- In commodity industries, tax cuts flow through to consumers as lower prices
- In franchise businesses, tax cuts flow to the company and shareholders
- "The strength of a company's franchise determines who captures the economic benefit of tax changes"
- See's would keep the tax benefit; a commodity candy maker would pass it through

### Industry Context
- "Candy stores are fun to visit, but most have not been fun for their owners" (1987)
- "Practically no one besides See's has made significant profits" in the candy shop industry (1987)
- See's succeeded in an industry where competitors "lost money" (1984)

## 6. How Buffett Measured Brand Strength

Buffett never used brand surveys or NPS scores. His measurement methods were entirely economic:

### Method 1: Return on Tangible Assets
- A business earning 25% on $8M of tangible assets has enormous economic goodwill
- The gap between earnings power and tangible asset base IS the brand value
- "Consumer franchises are a prime source of economic Goodwill" (1983 Appendix)

### Method 2: Pricing Power Durability
- Could See's raise prices year after year without losing customers? Yes, for 50+ years
- Pre-tax profit growth from $4.2M to $83M was the proof

### Method 3: Customer Behavior Under Stress
- **The Albuquerque Test (1990)**: When See's was about to lose its Albuquerque store due to an unreasonable landlord, 263 customers voluntarily wrote letters and made calls protesting the closure. The landlord reversed course. Christmas sales subsequently rose substantially
- This was Buffett's real-world brand loyalty metric: customers who fight to keep your store open

### Method 4: Economic Goodwill Growth Over Time
- Unlike physical assets that deteriorate, See's economic goodwill "had grown substantially for 78 years" (as of 1999 letter)
- "Economic goodwill is more like land than machinery: its value fluctuates but is not ordained to decline"
- Accounting mandated goodwill amortization; economic reality showed goodwill increasing

### Method 5: Competitor Failure Rate
- Almost no one else made significant profits in the candy shop industry
- See's thrived where competitors died -- the delta is brand strength

### Method 6: The Customer Verdict Over 100 Years
- "Don't mess with my candy" -- Buffett's summary of See's customers' century-long message (2020)
- The brand's value was proven by "countless pleasant experiences" customers had with both product and personnel (1983)

## 7. The Capital-Light Model Specifics

### Why See's Required Almost No Capital

**Cash Business (No Receivables)**
- Sold candy for cash at retail stores -- no accounts receivable to finance
- Eliminated one of the three major working capital drains

**Short Production Cycle (Minimal Inventory)**
- Fresh candy with no preservatives meant short production-to-sale cycle
- Refused to freeze finished products despite competitors doing so
- Low inventory carrying costs

**Minimal Fixed Assets**
- 207 stores (as of 1983), but stores are small retail shops, not factories
- Low capital intensity per location

### The Numbers That Prove It

| Metric | See's | Average American Business |
|---|---|---|
| Additional capital needed per $1 of additional annual pre-tax earnings | ~$0.63 ($40M for $64M earnings increase) | ~$5.00 (1985 letter) |
| Net worth required to operate | $25M (1991) | Would need multiples more for same earnings |
| Capital reinvested over 42 years | $40M | -- |
| Cumulative earnings over 42 years | $1.9B | -- |
| Ratio of earnings to reinvestment | 47.5:1 | -- |

### The Flywheel Effect (2007 Letter)
- See's generated $1.35B in cumulative pre-tax earnings
- Only $32M was reinvested in the business
- The remaining ~$1.32B was redeployed to buy other businesses
- "Just as Adam and Eve kick-started an activity that led to six billion humans, See's has given birth to multiple new streams of cash for us"
- Those new businesses themselves produced distributable profits -- "envision rabbits breeding" (2014)

### The Three Types of Businesses (2007 Letter)
Buffett used See's as the archetype for "Great":
1. **Great** (See's): Earns extraordinary returns on capital, requires little reinvestment -- like a savings account paying 25% that lets you withdraw the interest
2. **Good** (FlightSafety): Earns attractive returns but must reinvest significantly to grow
3. **Gruesome** (Airlines): Grows rapidly, requires massive capital, earns little or nothing

### Inflation Advantage (1983 Appendix)
- When prices double, See's needs only $8M more in tangible assets
- A mundane business earning the same would need $18M more
- See's gains $3+ of nominal value for every $1 invested during inflation
- The mundane business gains only $1 for $1
- "During inflation, Goodwill is the gift that keeps giving"

## 8. What See's Taught Buffett

The meta-lesson: See's was the investment that changed Buffett's entire philosophy.

- **Before See's**: Buffett favored "cigar butt" investing -- buying mediocre businesses at bargain prices based on tangible asset values
- **After See's**: Shifted to buying wonderful businesses at fair prices, prioritizing intangible competitive advantages over tangible assets
- **Direct downstream impact**: "We made significant money in certain common stocks because of the lessons we learned at See's" (1991) -- specifically Coca-Cola and Gillette
- **Charlie Munger's influence**: Munger pushed Buffett toward this "wonderful business at fair price" approach, and See's was the proof of concept

### The Key Evaluation Framework See's Established
1. Can the business raise prices without losing volume? (Pricing power)
2. Does it require heavy reinvestment to grow? (Capital efficiency)
3. Is the brand built on "countless pleasant experiences"? (Durability)
4. Can competent (not just exceptional) management run it well? (System vs. star)
5. Does it buy commodities and sell brands? (Margin structure)
