# Capital Allocation — The CEO's Most Important Job

> *"The heads of many companies are not combatable with capital allocation. Their inadequacy is not surprising. Most bosses rise to the top because they have excelled in an area such as marketing, production, engineering, administration — or, sometimes, institutional politics."* — [1987](../markdown/buffett-letter-1987.md)

**Checklist mapping**: Stage 1.3 (Financial Profile), Stage 3 C6 (Capital-Light + Any Moat), Gate G5 (Demonstrated Earnings), Gate G6 (Financial Independence)

---

## Core Principle: Every Dollar Retained Must Create More Than a Dollar of Value

Buffett's fundamental capital allocation test: for every dollar of earnings retained, the company must create at least one dollar of market value. If it cannot, the money belongs in shareholders' hands.

---

## ROE — The True Measure

**[1977](../markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md) ROE over EPS:**
> ROE, not EPS, is the real measure of performance. A company can grow EPS every year simply by retaining earnings — even if those retained earnings earn subpar returns. Only ROE reveals whether management is creating genuine value.

Application: When evaluating a business, look at 10-year ROE trends. Consistent ROE above 15% without excessive leverage is a strong signal of business quality.

**[1979](../markdown/buffett-letter-1979.md) Without leverage or gimmickry:**
> "The primary test of managerial economic performance is the achievement of a high earnings rate on equity capital employed (without undue leverage, accounting gimmickry, etc.) and not the achievement of consistent gains in earnings per share."

---

## Owner Earnings — Buffett's Preferred Metric

**[1986](../markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md) The definition:**
> Owner earnings = reported earnings + depreciation/amortization - maintenance capital expenditures. This is what the owner can actually take out of the business while maintaining its competitive position.

Why this matters: GAAP earnings include non-cash charges that may not reflect economic reality, and exclude required reinvestment. Owner earnings capture the true free cash flow available to owners.

**[2018](../markdown/buffett-letter-2018.md) Maintenance capex is the true cost:**
> Maintenance capex — the capital required just to maintain current operations — is the business's true annual cost of staying alive. Growth capex is optional; maintenance capex is not.

**[2023](../markdown/buffett-letter-2023.md) When depreciation understates reality:**
> In capital-intensive industries, reported earnings can be deeply misleading. When a business must consistently spend more than depreciation to maintain operations, actual owner earnings are far below reported earnings. EBITDA is particularly misleading in such contexts (Buffett bans it as a metric at Berkshire).

Application: Always compare maintenance capital expenditures to depreciation charges. If capex consistently exceeds depreciation, the business is less profitable than it appears.

**[1984](../markdown/buffett-letter-1984.md) Restricted vs. unrestricted earnings:**
> Not all earnings are created equal. In capital-intensive businesses, inflation causes some portion of reported earnings to become "ersatz" — these restricted earnings must be reinvested just to maintain the business's economic position. A company that distributes restricted earnings is destined for oblivion.

## The Capital-Light Dream (Checklist C6)

**[2007](../markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) Three tiers of business quality:**
> Great businesses earn extraordinary returns on capital and require little reinvestment (See's Candy — $25M purchase, $1.35B cumulative pre-tax earnings, $32M total reinvestment). Good businesses earn attractive returns but require significant reinvestment to grow (FlightSafety). Gruesome businesses grow rapidly, require massive capital, and earn little or nothing (airlines).

**[1977](../markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md) Capital-light as the ideal:**
> The most valuable businesses are those that can grow earnings without requiring proportional increases in invested capital. Earnings growth that requires constant reinvestment of capital is far less valuable than growth that throws off cash.

**[2011](../markdown/buffett-letter-2011.md) See's vs. utilities:**
> See's Candy earned $83 million on effectively zero capital employed. Contrast this with utilities, where earning more requires investing proportionally more. Capital-light businesses compound owner wealth far faster.

**[2014](../markdown/buffett-letter-2014.md) The rabbits metaphor:**
> See's didn't just generate profits — it funded Berkshire's acquisition of other businesses, which themselves produced distributable profits. "Envision rabbits breeding." A capital-light cash generator is the seed for an entire portfolio.

---

## When Capital-Intensive Works

**[2009](../markdown/buffett-letter-2009.md) Not inherently bad:**
> Capital-intensive businesses are not inherently bad — they are bad only when they fail to earn reasonable returns on incremental investment. For a company generating enormous cash flows, businesses that can absorb large capital deployments at decent returns become attractive.

**[2020](../markdown/buffett-letter-2020.md) BNSF proof:**
> BNSF invested $41 billion in fixed assets since acquisition and paid $41.8 billion in dividends back to Berkshire. Capital-intensive works when the return on incremental investment is adequate.

**[2008](../markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md) Reinvestment builds virtue:**
> In capital-intensive businesses, reinvesting earnings to improve service quality and infrastructure creates a virtuous cycle. Customers get better service, regulators allow fair returns, and the business builds an ever-larger and more valuable asset base.

**[2013](../markdown/buffett-letter-2013.md) Infrastructure as compounding:**
> Capital-intensive businesses that can reinvest all their earnings into growing, essential infrastructure (especially renewables) at reasonable returns create compounding value. Retained earnings + regulatory support + long-lived assets = durable wealth creation.

## What to Do With Free Cash Flow

**[2007](../markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) You don't have to reinvest where you earned it:**
> There is no rule that profits must be reinvested in the same business that generated them. Forcing reinvestment into a mature business with limited growth opportunities is often a mistake. The wise move is to harvest the cash and deploy it into better opportunities elsewhere.

**[1986, 2007] The allocation hierarchy:**
The letters reveal Buffett's implicit ranking for deploying free cash flow:
1. **Reinvest at high returns** — if the business can deploy capital at 15%+ returns, reinvest
2. **Make acquisitions** — buy other great businesses at sensible prices
3. **Repurchase shares** — but only below intrinsic value
4. **Pay dividends** — the last resort, when no better option exists
5. **Hoard cash** — acceptable temporarily while waiting for opportunities

**[2011](../markdown/buffett-letter-2011.md) Share repurchases — the first law:**
> "The first law of capital allocation — whether the money is slated for acquisitions or share repurchases — is that what is smart at one price is dumb at another." Many CEOs "never stop believing their stock is cheap," leading to value-destructive repurchases.

**[1980](../markdown/buffett-letter-1980.md) Cash flow conversion matters:**
> Attractive reported earnings that never convert into free cash flow are a mirage. Track free cash flow conversion relentlessly. Businesses that can grow while generating surplus cash are fundamentally more valuable than those whose earnings are perpetually consumed by maintenance capital requirements.

## The Textile Mill Warning — What Bad Capital Allocation Looks Like

**[1978](../markdown/buffett-letter-1978.md) The commodity trap:**
> Producers of undifferentiated goods in capital-intensive industries are trapped in a structural disadvantage. When excess capacity exists, prices reflect direct operating costs rather than the capital employed, making adequate returns nearly impossible.

**[1983](../markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md) Throwing money at a bad industry:**
> Major additional investment in a terrible industry is "about as rewarding as struggling in quicksand." The projections will be dazzling and the advocates sincere, but industry economics usually prevail over even the best management.

Context: Berkshire's original textile business taught Buffett that no amount of capital investment can overcome industry economics. He kept the textile operation running for years out of loyalty but ultimately recognized it was a capital-destroying business.

## Key Diagnostic Questions (for AI agents)

1. **What is the 10-year average ROE?** Above 15% consistently (without leverage tricks) signals strong economics.
2. **What are owner earnings?** Calculate: reported earnings + depreciation - maintenance capex. Compare to reported earnings — how big is the gap?
3. **What does management do with free cash flow?** Trace the last 5 years of FCF allocation. Smart reinvestment? Value-destructive acquisitions? Buybacks above intrinsic value?
4. **How capital-light is the business?** Revenue per dollar of invested capital. Can earnings grow without proportional capital increases?
5. **Is the company retaining earnings that create value?** For every dollar retained, has at least one dollar of market value been created over 5 years?
