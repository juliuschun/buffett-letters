# Lessons from Buffett's 1990 Shareholder Letter

## Business Lessons for Survival and Thriving

### Lesson 1: Outstanding Managers Matter More Than Industry Economics
- **Context**: Berkshire's non-insurance subsidiaries earned 51% after-tax return on equity -- enough to rank about 20th on the Fortune 500 -- yet operated in prosaic fields like furniture retailing, candy, vacuum cleaners, and steel warehousing.
- **Lesson**: Extraordinary returns can come from ordinary industries when led by exceptional managers. You do not need to be in a glamorous, high-margin industry to produce world-class results. The quality of management is the decisive variable.
- **Application**: When evaluating a business (or building one), prioritize finding and retaining outstanding operators over chasing trendy industries. A great manager in a boring business will outperform a mediocre manager in a spectacular one.

### Lesson 2: Low-Cost Operator Wins -- The Borsheim's / NFM Formula
- **Context**: Borsheim's jewelry ran 18% operating costs vs. 40% for typical competitors. Nebraska Furniture Mart ran 15% operating costs vs. 40% for Levitz and 25% for Circuit City. Both used low costs to offer unbeatable prices, which generated massive volume, which funded extraordinary selection.
- **Lesson**: A structural cost advantage creates a virtuous cycle: low costs enable low prices, low prices drive high volume, high volume funds broader selection, and broader selection pulls customers from ever-wider geographies. This flywheel is nearly impossible for high-cost competitors to replicate.
- **Application**: Relentlessly attack operating costs -- not to hoard margin, but to offer prices competitors cannot match. The goal is to become the low-cost provider and use that advantage to expand your addressable market, just as NFM pulled customers from 130 miles away.

### Lesson 3: Expand Territory Through Magnetism, Not Stores
- **Context**: NFM was the #3 most popular furniture retailer in Des Moines despite being 130 miles away, while 19 competitors were local. Borsheim's mailed 1,500 jewelry selections nationwide from a single Omaha location.
- **Lesson**: A business can dramatically expand its effective territory without opening new locations by creating an irresistible combination of price, selection, and service that functions as a magnet. Geographic distance becomes irrelevant when the value proposition is strong enough.
- **Application**: Before expanding physically, ask whether your existing operation can pull customers from farther away. A single extraordinary location can outperform a chain of mediocre ones.

### Lesson 4: Family Commitment and Ownership Culture Drive Results
- **Context**: At Borsheim's, multiple generations of the Friedman family -- including Ike's 89-year-old mother -- worked the store daily. At NFM, the Blumkin family operated similarly.
- **Lesson**: When a family treats the business as a life's work rather than a job, the level of commitment, attention to detail, and customer care far exceeds what salaried managers typically deliver. Competitors whose managers watch the clock cannot keep up.
- **Application**: Foster an ownership culture where key people have real skin in the game. Whether through actual equity or deep personal identification with the business, the psychology of ownership is a powerful competitive advantage.

### Lesson 5: Never Cut Product Quality in Response to Adversity
- **Context**: The Buffalo News faced declining advertising revenue and rising costs in 1990-91. Despite the profit squeeze, Buffett maintained a minimum 50% "news hole" (portion devoted to news content).
- **Lesson**: When times get tough, the temptation to cut quality is strong but destructive. Customers notice quality cuts, and once trust is lost, it is extremely difficult to regain. Maintaining quality during downturns positions you to emerge stronger.
- **Application**: Identify the core quality commitments that define your product or service. Protect those commitments even when margins are under pressure. Cut elsewhere -- overhead, perks, non-essential spending -- but never the thing that makes customers choose you.

### Lesson 6: Customer Loyalty is a Bankable Asset
- **Context**: When See's Candies was about to lose its Albuquerque store due to an unreasonable landlord, 263 customers voluntarily wrote letters and made calls protesting the closure. The landlord reversed course, and Christmas sales subsequently rose substantially.
- **Lesson**: Deep customer loyalty -- earned through years of consistent quality and service -- is an asset that shows up on no balance sheet but can save a business in a crisis. It also generates organic growth through word of mouth and repeat purchases.
- **Application**: Invest in building genuine customer relationships. "Fanaticism about quality and service" (as Buffett described Chuck Huggins's approach at See's) creates a reservoir of goodwill that pays dividends in unexpected ways.

### Lesson 7: In a Commodity Business, You Cannot Be Smarter Than Your Dumbest Competitor
- **Context**: USAir suffered from "kamikaze pricing tactics" by other airlines. Despite good management and excellent service, the economics of the business deteriorated because competitors engaged in destructive pricing.
- **Lesson**: When selling a commodity-type product, any competitor can drag the entire industry down by pricing irrationally. Superior management and service cannot overcome an industry structure where the most reckless participant sets the price floor.
- **Application**: Avoid commodity businesses if possible. If you are in one, differentiate relentlessly to escape pure commodity status. Understand that in commoditized markets, your returns are partly hostage to the worst decisions of your least rational competitor.

### Lesson 8: Leverage Magnifies Both Genius and Stupidity
- **Context**: Buffett discussed why banking (with 20:1 asset-to-equity leverage) is dangerous for poorly managed institutions, and why junk-bond-laden capital structures of the 1980s guaranteed failure.
- **Lesson**: High leverage amplifies outcomes in both directions. When things go well, returns are spectacular. But even small mistakes -- a tiny pothole on the road -- can be fatal. The roads of business are riddled with potholes, so a plan that requires dodging them all is a plan for disaster.
- **Application**: Use debt conservatively. A business that can survive bad times without leverage will outperform one that relies on leverage to generate returns in good times. Financial strength is itself a competitive advantage -- it lets you act when others are paralyzed.

### Lesson 9: Margin of Safety -- The Three Most Important Words in Investing and Business
- **Context**: Buffett discussed the collapse of junk bonds in 1990, quoting Ben Graham: "Confronted with a challenge to distill the secret of sound investment into three words, we venture the motto, Margin of Safety."
- **Lesson**: Whether in capital structure, pricing insurance, or evaluating investments, always build in a buffer for the unexpected. Optimistic projections and plans that work only under favorable conditions will eventually be destroyed by reality.
- **Application**: In any business decision -- pricing, hiring, capital investment, borrowing -- ask "what happens if conditions are worse than expected?" Build your plans to survive the downside, not just capture the upside.

### Lesson 10: The Institutional Imperative -- Beware Lemming Behavior
- **Context**: Banks made foolish loans because executives mindlessly imitated their peers. "In their lending, many bankers played follow-the-leader with lemming-like zeal; now they are experiencing a lemming-like fate."
- **Lesson**: One of the most destructive forces in business is the tendency of managers to copy what other managers are doing, regardless of whether it makes sense. The institutional imperative overrides independent analysis and leads entire industries off cliffs.
- **Application**: Develop the discipline to think independently. When an industry consensus forms around a particular strategy or valuation, that is precisely when skepticism is most valuable. The crowd is not a source of safety.

### Lesson 11: Buy Well-Managed Businesses at Fair Prices, Not Poorly-Managed Ones at Cheap Prices
- **Context**: Buffett explained his Wells Fargo investment: "We have no interest in purchasing shares of a poorly-managed bank at a 'cheap' price. Instead, our only interest is in buying into well-managed banks at fair prices."
- **Lesson**: A cheap price cannot compensate for bad management, especially in leveraged businesses where small errors compound. Superior managers create value year after year; a one-time discount on a bad business is a false bargain.
- **Application**: Whether acquiring companies or choosing partners, prioritize quality of leadership over apparent bargain pricing. Pay a fair price for excellence rather than a low price for mediocrity.

### Lesson 12: Qualities of Superior Management Teams
- **Context**: Buffett described why Carl Reichardt and Paul Hazen at Wells Fargo reminded him of Tom Murphy and Dan Burke at Capital Cities/ABC.
- **Lesson**: The best management teams share four traits: (1) partners whose combination is stronger than the sum of its parts because of mutual trust and admiration; (2) they pay able people well but refuse unnecessary headcount; (3) they attack costs with equal vigor in good times and bad; (4) they stick with what they understand and let ability, not ego, determine what they attempt.
- **Application**: Evaluate management teams against these four criteria. Especially notable is point three -- most managers cut costs only under pressure. The best managers maintain cost discipline when profits are at record levels.

### Lesson 13: Pessimism Creates Opportunity for the Rational Buyer
- **Context**: Wells Fargo stock fell almost 50% on fears of California real estate losses. Buffett bought aggressively, paying less than 5x after-tax earnings for a bank earning 20%+ ROE.
- **Lesson**: Pessimism -- whether broad or specific to an industry -- is what creates attractive prices. Optimism is the enemy of the rational buyer. Ongoing buyers of businesses should welcome falling prices, just as ongoing buyers of food welcome lower grocery prices.
- **Application**: Train yourself to see price declines as opportunity, not danger. The emotional reaction to falling prices is fear, but the rational response for a long-term buyer is enthusiasm. Build financial reserves so you can act when others are fleeing.

### Lesson 14: Accounting Earnings Are Putty -- Focus on Economic Reality
- **Context**: Buffett introduced "look-through earnings" because GAAP earnings failed to capture Berkshire's share of retained earnings at investee companies. He also attached Ben Graham's 1936 satire on accounting manipulation.
- **Lesson**: Reported earnings can be manipulated or misleading. Smart operators look past the accounting numbers to understand the true economic earnings of a business. "Earnings can be as pliable as putty when a charlatan heads the company reporting them."
- **Application**: Use accounting numbers as a beginning, not an end. Always ask: what are the real cash economics of this business? Be especially suspicious when reported earnings seem too good to be true or when complex accounting treatments obscure simple business realities.

### Lesson 15: The Value of Float -- Understanding the Insurance Business Model
- **Context**: Buffett explained that insurance companies hold "float" -- other people's money -- which can be invested. The key metric is the cost of that float (underwriting loss divided by average float). Berkshire's cost was 1.6% in 1990, far below government bond yields.
- **Lesson**: The insurance business is fundamentally about generating low-cost capital. If float can be obtained at below-market rates, the business creates enormous value. But if underwriting discipline slips, float becomes expensive and the business destroys value.
- **Application**: In any business that collects money before delivering services (subscriptions, deposits, prepayments), understand the economics of that float. The spread between what you earn on held funds and what they cost you is a key driver of profitability.

### Lesson 16: Concentrate on What You Do Best -- Stay Around Your Spots
- **Context**: Buffett quoted Thomas Watson Sr. of IBM: "I'm no genius. I'm smart in spots -- but I stay around those spots." He applied this to Wells Fargo's management philosophy.
- **Lesson**: Self-awareness about your circle of competence is more valuable than raw intelligence. The best managers and investors know what they understand and refuse to be tempted beyond those boundaries by ego or ambition.
- **Application**: Define your circle of competence honestly. Resist the pull to expand into areas you do not deeply understand, no matter how attractive they appear. Depth of knowledge in a narrow domain beats surface knowledge across many.

### Lesson 17: Prefer Lumpy 15% Returns to Smooth 12% Returns
- **Context**: Buffett explained Berkshire's super-cat insurance business, which would produce either ~$100M profit or $200M+ loss in any given year, but excellent returns over a decade.
- **Lesson**: Volatility is not the same as risk. A business or investment strategy that produces irregular but superior long-term results is preferable to one that delivers smooth but mediocre returns. Most managers and investors wrongly conflate volatility with danger.
- **Application**: When evaluating strategies, focus on long-term expected value, not smoothness of returns. Be willing to accept short-term volatility if the math favors you over time. This requires both the financial strength and psychological fortitude to endure bad years.

### Lesson 18: Media Industry Disruption -- Pricing Power Can Erode
- **Context**: Buffett acknowledged that media businesses, once considered economic marvels, were showing unexpected vulnerability. Advertising dollars were growing slowly, non-media retailers were gaining share, and the number of advertising channels was increasing.
- **Lesson**: Even businesses with historically extraordinary economics can see their competitive position erode as industry structure changes. Pricing power -- the most valuable attribute a business can have -- is not permanent if the supply of competing alternatives increases.
- **Application**: Continuously monitor the structural forces that underpin your pricing power. Are new channels or competitors emerging? Are customers finding substitutes? The fact that an industry has been wonderful for decades does not guarantee it will remain so.

### Lesson 19: Capital Discipline -- Send Cash to Headquarters When You Cannot Deploy It Well
- **Context**: Berkshire's operating managers funneled over 80% of their earnings to Buffett and Munger over five years for redeployment, even though their businesses were growing. Net worth grew only $47M on $133M of earnings because capital was being efficiently redeployed.
- **Lesson**: Good managers invest aggressively where returns are high but refuse to deploy capital without good reason. Rather than empire-building or making mediocre investments to "put money to work," they return excess cash to where it can earn the highest returns.
- **Application**: Every dollar of capital should earn its keep. If your business cannot reinvest at attractive returns, distribute the excess rather than destroying value through low-return projects or acquisitions done for growth's sake.

### Lesson 20: Financial Strength as Competitive Advantage in Volatile Markets
- **Context**: Berkshire became the largest U.S. writer of super-cat insurance because its unmatched financial strength was exactly what buyers needed in catastrophe coverage. Other insurers lacked either the ability or willingness to absorb potential massive losses.
- **Lesson**: Financial strength -- large reserves, low debt, ample liquidity -- is not just defensive. It is an offensive weapon that allows you to enter markets and write business that weaker competitors cannot, precisely when demand and pricing are most attractive.
- **Application**: Maintain a fortress balance sheet. The ability to act decisively when others are constrained by fear or financial weakness is one of the most durable competitive advantages in business.

### Lesson 21: When Selling a Business, Choose Your Buyer Wisely
- **Context**: In Appendix B, Buffett wrote a letter to a potential seller explaining why Berkshire was a superior buyer -- they buy to keep, let managers run autonomously, never install parent-company executives, and use no leverage against acquired businesses.
- **Lesson**: For owner-managers, the form of a sale matters as much as the price. An acquirer who promises autonomy but has layers of managers eager to "help" will inevitably meddle. A financial buyer using heavy leverage will restructure and flip the business. The character and structure of the buyer determines the business's future.
- **Application**: If selling a business, evaluate buyers not just on price but on how they will treat your employees, culture, and operations. Check references with their previous acquisitions, especially those that did not go well.

### Lesson 22: Beware of Past-Performance "Proofs" in Finance
- **Context**: Junk bond salesmen used historical default rates of fallen angels to justify new-issue junk bonds, despite the two universes being fundamentally different.
- **Lesson**: Historical data can be dangerously misleading when the underlying population changes. The statistical fallacy of applying past results from one group to a fundamentally different group has destroyed enormous wealth. The conditions that generated historical returns may not persist.
- **Application**: When presented with backtests, historical returns, or "proof" that a strategy works, always ask: is the current situation truly comparable to the historical data? Have the rules, players, or structural conditions changed?

### Lesson 23: Wall Street's Enthusiasm Is Proportional to Revenue, Not Merit
- **Context**: Investment banks promoted junk bonds not because they believed in the product's value, but because of the enormous fees they generated. "Mountains of junk bonds were sold by those who didn't care to those who didn't think."
- **Lesson**: Financial intermediaries are motivated by transaction fees, not by the long-term outcomes for buyers or sellers. The more complex and novel a financial product, the higher the fees, and the louder the promotion -- regardless of underlying merit.
- **Application**: Always consider the incentives of anyone recommending a financial product or strategy. If the recommender earns a fee on the transaction, their enthusiasm tells you nothing about the product's quality.

### Lesson 24: Succession Planning and Institutional Permanence
- **Context**: Buffett detailed his succession plans: his wife Susan would inherit control, key managers were already in place, and neither the Buffett estate plan nor his wife's was designed to preserve family fortune but rather to preserve Berkshire's character and return the fortune to society.
- **Lesson**: A truly durable business must not depend on any single person's health or tenure. The leader's responsibility is to ensure continuity of management philosophy and institutional culture, not just to perform well during their own tenure.
- **Application**: Plan for succession long before it is needed. Ensure that the values, culture, and decision-making frameworks of the business are embedded in the institution, not just in one individual.

## Memorable Quotes

> "Gee, I'm really good at this."
-- Context: Buffett's granddaughter Emily, age 4, marveling at her "magic" during a birthday party trick. Buffett used this as an analogy for his own contribution to Berkshire's operating managers -- he waves the wand while they perform the real magic.

> "Lethargy bordering on sloth remains the cornerstone of our investment style."
-- Context: Describing their approach to the six major stock holdings, five of which they neither bought nor sold a single share of during 1990.

> "Earnings can be as pliable as putty when a charlatan heads the company reporting them."
-- Context: Warning investors that reported earnings are not as precise as they appear, and that accounting manipulation has created important American fortunes.

> "We care not whether the auditors hear a tree fall in the forest; we do care who owns the tree and what's next done with it."
-- Context: Explaining why the accounting treatment of investee earnings is irrelevant -- what matters is ownership and capital allocation.

> "What works with diapers works with diamonds."
-- Context: Comparing Borsheim's low-cost strategy to Wal-Mart's, explaining that the same economic principle -- low costs enabling low prices enabling high volume -- applies across industries.

> "Charlie and I always have preferred a lumpy 15% return to a smooth 12%."
-- Context: Explaining why Berkshire was willing to accept volatile results from super-cat insurance in exchange for superior long-term returns.

> "In a business selling a commodity-type product, it's impossible to be a lot smarter than your dumbest competitor."
-- Context: Discussing USAir's troubles caused by competitors' kamikaze pricing tactics in the airline industry.

> "The most important thing to do when you find yourself in a hole is to stop digging."
-- Context: Describing the insurance industry's failure to raise prices despite mounting underwriting losses.

> "The roads of business are riddled with potholes; a plan that requires dodging them all is a plan for disaster."
-- Context: Criticizing the junk bond thesis that heavy debt would force managers to "focus" -- comparing it to mounting a dagger on a steering wheel to make a driver more careful.

> "Confronted with a challenge to distill the secret of sound investment into three words, we venture the motto, Margin of Safety."
-- Context: Quoting Ben Graham from The Intelligent Investor, applied to the failure of highly leveraged junk bond structures.

> "In their lending, many bankers played follow-the-leader with lemming-like zeal; now they are experiencing a lemming-like fate."
-- Context: Describing the institutional imperative in banking, where executives mindlessly imitated peers' foolish lending decisions.

> "I'm no genius. I'm smart in spots -- but I stay around those spots."
-- Context: Quoting Thomas Watson Sr. of IBM, used to describe the management philosophy of Wells Fargo's Carl Reichardt and Paul Hazen.

> "It's optimism that is the enemy of the rational buyer."
-- Context: Explaining why pessimism creates buying opportunities. Low prices are caused by pessimism, and ongoing buyers should welcome them.

> "Most men would rather die than think. Many do."
-- Context: Quoting Bertrand Russell to criticize investors who follow the crowd rather than thinking independently. Contrarian approaches and follow-the-crowd strategies are equally foolish -- what's required is thinking, not polling.

> "If history books were the key to riches, the Forbes 400 would consist of librarians."
-- Context: Warning against past-performance "proofs" in finance, specifically the misuse of historical default rates to justify junk bond investments.

> "Mountains of junk bonds were sold by those who didn't care to those who didn't think -- and there was no shortage of either."
-- Context: Describing Wall Street's promotion of junk bonds, where the Street's enthusiasm was proportional to revenue generated, not to the product's merit.

> "The only time to buy these is on a day with no 'y' in it."
-- Context: On never buying new issues of junk bonds -- a humorous way of saying "never."

> "We have no interest in purchasing shares of a poorly-managed bank at a 'cheap' price. Instead, our only interest is in buying into well-managed banks at fair prices."
-- Context: Explaining the Wells Fargo investment thesis, emphasizing that in leveraged businesses, management quality matters more than purchase price.

> "Trying to sell something without advertising is like winking at a girl in the dark."
-- Context: Justifying his use of the annual letter to solicit acquisition candidates, noting that advertising does work.

> "When the phone don't ring, you'll know it's me."
-- Context: Quoting a country song to express his lack of interest in turnarounds, new ventures, or auction-like sales processes.

> "We've found that if you advertise an interest in buying collies, a lot of people will call hoping to sell you their cocker spaniels."
-- Context: Describing the stream of unsuitable acquisition proposals Berkshire receives despite clearly stating its criteria.

> "Were I to die tomorrow, you could be sure of three things: (1) None of my stock would have to be sold; (2) Both a controlling shareholder and a manager with philosophies similar to mine would follow me; and (3) Berkshire's earnings would increase by $1 million annually, since Charlie would immediately sell our corporate jet, The Indefensible (ignoring my wish that it be buried with me)."
-- Context: Discussing succession planning with characteristic humor about his love of the corporate jet.

> "Cutting product quality is not a proper response to adversity."
-- Context: Pledging to maintain the Buffalo News's 50% news hole despite declining revenues and rising costs.

> "I plunged into the business at almost the exact moment that it ran into severe problems. (No one pushed me; in tennis parlance, I committed an 'unforced error.')"
-- Context: Candidly admitting his poorly timed investment in USAir, demonstrating the self-honesty he considers essential.

> "In the insurance business, there is no statute of limitations on stupidity."
-- Context: Acknowledging his own past mistakes in insurance, noting that the bills for errors keep arriving years later.

> "I can't understand why more people aren't bi-sexual because it doubles your chances for a date on Saturday night."
-- Context: Quoting Woody Allen to explain Berkshire's willingness to look at any investment category -- stocks, bonds, whole businesses -- as long as they understand it and see a gap between price and value.
