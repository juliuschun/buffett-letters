# Lessons from Buffett's 1992 Shareholder Letter

## Business Lessons for Survival and Thriving

### Lesson 1: Grow Only When It Increases Owner Value
- **Context**: Buffett discussed Berkshire's policy on issuing shares and told a story about a bank acquisition where the acquirer paid twice the value of the target, only for the seller to quip "promise me you'll never again make a deal this dumb."
- **Lesson**: Increasing a company's size is not the same as increasing owner wealth. Issuing shares or making acquisitions should only happen when you receive at least as much value as you give. Many managers confuse growth with progress.
- **Application**: Before any expansion, merger, or capital raise, ask: "Does this make each existing owner richer?" If the answer is no, walk away regardless of how exciting the opportunity looks.

### Lesson 2: Tolerate Short-Term Volatility for Long-Term Results
- **Context**: Buffett explained why Berkshire's annual results swing wildly due to stock market movements and concentrated holdings, and why he welcomes that volatility.
- **Lesson**: A willingness to accept short-term swings improves long-term prospects. Optimizing for smooth quarterly results often means sacrificing bigger opportunities. The right performance yardstick is slugging percentage, not batting average.
- **Application**: Businesses should evaluate strategies on their long-term payoff potential, not on how smooth they make quarterly reports look. Consistency is overrated if it comes at the cost of magnitude.

### Lesson 3: Buy Good Businesses at Fair Prices, Not Fair Businesses at Good Prices
- **Context**: Buffett reflected on his early career of buying cheap, mediocre businesses ("kissing toads") and how he shifted strategy after repeated failures, inspired by a golf pro's advice that "practice makes permanent."
- **Lesson**: Buying a mediocre business cheaply does not transform it into a good business. Quality of the underlying business matters far more than the discount at which you acquire it. Bad habits in acquisition strategy become entrenched over time.
- **Application**: Whether acquiring a company, choosing a vendor, or entering a market, prioritize quality of the asset or opportunity over getting a bargain. A great business at a reasonable price beats a mediocre one at a steal.

### Lesson 4: The Acquisition Discipline -- Patience Over Urgency
- **Context**: Buffett compared the search for acquisitions to looking for a spouse, and described how many CEOs overpay for "corporate toads" hoping for fairy-tale transformations.
- **Lesson**: It pays to be active, interested, and open-minded in seeking acquisitions, but it does not pay to be in a hurry. Managers mesmerized by deal-making often pay dearly for the right to kiss toads, then announce massive restructuring charges when the magic doesn't happen.
- **Application**: Maintain acquisition discipline. Never let deal fever override valuation rigor. The best deals come to those who can wait, and the worst losses come from managers who feel they must always be doing something.

### Lesson 5: Extend Proven Winners Through Add-On Acquisitions
- **Context**: Berkshire's subsidiaries made five small "add-on" acquisitions in 1992 to extend product lines or distribution, including H.H. Brown's purchase of Lowell Shoe Company.
- **Lesson**: Enlarging the domain of managers you already know to be outstanding is a low-risk, high-return proposition. Small bolt-on acquisitions under proven leaders compound value with minimal integration risk.
- **Application**: When you have a great manager running a successful operation, give them more territory. Organic expansion under proven leadership is one of the safest ways to grow.

### Lesson 6: Insurance Float as a Competitive Weapon
- **Context**: Buffett detailed Berkshire's insurance float -- money held from policyholders that can be invested -- and showed that in 21 of 26 years, their cost of float was below the U.S. government bond rate.
- **Lesson**: A business that generates low-cost or negative-cost capital has an enormous structural advantage. Berkshire's insurance operations effectively gave them free money to invest, creating a compounding flywheel that competitors could not replicate.
- **Application**: Look for business models that generate "float" or prepaid revenue -- subscription models, deposits, advance payments. If you can use other people's money at below-market rates, you have a durable competitive edge.

### Lesson 7: Discipline in Saying No
- **Context**: Despite being the largest super-cat reinsurer, Berkshire rejected more than 98% of the business offered to them.
- **Lesson**: Having the capacity to do something doesn't mean you should. The ability to choose between good and bad proposals is a management strength that matches financial strength. Saying no is as important as having the resources to say yes.
- **Application**: A business's long-term survival depends on its ability to reject bad deals, bad customers, and bad opportunities, even when it has the capacity to take them on. Selectivity is a competitive advantage.

### Lesson 8: Prepare for the Unthinkable
- **Context**: Buffett discussed Hurricane Andrew's devastation of the insurance industry and the surprising historical earthquakes in Charleston, S.C. and New Madrid, Missouri -- events no one expected in those locations.
- **Lesson**: Past experience is an unreliable guide for catastrophic risk. Population growth, climate change, and insured values in vulnerable areas mean that historical loss data systematically underestimates future risk. The unthinkable does happen.
- **Application**: Every business should stress-test against scenarios that have never occurred, not just replay historical crises. Tail risks are often underpriced and under-prepared-for.

### Lesson 9: Competitive Advantage Requires Both Financial and Management Strength
- **Context**: Buffett described Berkshire's position in the super-cat reinsurance market, attributing their dominance to the combination of Berkshire's capital base and Ajit Jain's management excellence.
- **Lesson**: Financial strength alone is not enough; it must be paired with exceptional management capability. Conversely, brilliant management without adequate capital is equally insufficient. The combination creates a moat that is very hard to replicate.
- **Application**: When building a competitive advantage, invest in both your balance sheet and your talent. One without the other leaves you vulnerable.

### Lesson 10: Growth Can Destroy Value
- **Context**: Buffett discussed the airline industry as an example of profitless growth, saying investors would have been better off if Orville Wright had never gotten off the ground at Kitty Hawk.
- **Lesson**: Growth benefits investors only when the business can invest incremental capital at attractive returns. In a low-return business requiring more and more capital, growth actually hurts the investor. Growth is not inherently good -- it depends entirely on the economics of reinvestment.
- **Application**: Before pursuing growth, verify that each dollar reinvested creates more than a dollar of value. If your industry requires heavy reinvestment but earns poor returns on capital, growth is your enemy, not your friend.

### Lesson 11: Value and Growth Are Not Opposites
- **Context**: Buffett challenged the common Wall Street distinction between "value investing" and "growth investing," calling the separation "fuzzy thinking."
- **Lesson**: Growth is always a component in the calculation of value. The term "value investing" is redundant -- all true investing is the act of seeking value sufficient to justify the price paid. Paying more than calculated value in hopes of selling higher is speculation, not investing.
- **Application**: Don't categorize investment decisions as either "value" or "growth." Every business decision should be evaluated on discounted future cash flows. A high-growth company can be a value purchase, and a stagnant company can be overpriced.

### Lesson 12: Stay Within Your Circle of Competence
- **Context**: Buffett explained how Berkshire deals with the difficulty of estimating future cash flows: they stick to simple, stable businesses they can understand, and insisted that knowing the limits of your knowledge matters more than the breadth of it.
- **Lesson**: What counts for most people in investing is not how much they know, but how realistically they define what they don't know. An investor (or business operator) needs to do very few things right as long as they avoid big mistakes.
- **Application**: Don't chase opportunities in industries or domains you don't understand. Accurately defining the boundary of your competence -- and staying inside it -- prevents catastrophic errors.

### Lesson 13: Always Demand a Margin of Safety
- **Context**: Buffett described the margin-of-safety principle, attributed to Ben Graham, as the cornerstone of investment success.
- **Lesson**: If the calculated value of an investment is only slightly higher than the asking price, it's not worth buying. A wide gap between price and value protects against estimation errors, bad luck, and unforeseen problems.
- **Application**: In any business decision involving capital allocation -- acquisitions, new projects, hiring -- build in a cushion. If the deal only works under optimistic assumptions, it's not a good deal.

### Lesson 14: The Best Business Employs Large Capital at High Returns
- **Context**: Buffett defined the ideal business as one that can deploy large amounts of incremental capital at very high rates of return, while noting most high-return businesses need relatively little capital.
- **Lesson**: The best business to own combines high returns on capital with the ability to reinvest large amounts at those same high rates. The worst business consistently requires ever-greater capital at low returns. Most high-return businesses should pay out earnings rather than retain them.
- **Application**: Evaluate your business's reinvestment opportunities honestly. If you can't deploy capital at high returns, return it to shareholders rather than empire-building with low-return projects.

### Lesson 15: Secondary Markets Offer Better Bargains Than New Issues
- **Context**: Buffett compared buying in the secondary market (where mass folly periodically rules) vs. buying new issues (where sellers control timing and never offer bargains).
- **Lesson**: The secondary market is periodically ruled by irrational pricing, creating opportunities to buy assets worth X for half of X. New-issue markets are controlled by sellers who choose to sell only when prices favor them. Intelligent investors do better buying from distressed or irrational sellers than from strategic ones.
- **Application**: Look for opportunities where the seller is forced or irrational. Avoid situations where the seller has full control over timing -- they will never give you a bargain.

### Lesson 16: In Tough Industries, the Reward for Skill Is Survival, Not Prosperity
- **Context**: Buffett discussed USAir and the airline industry's "kamikaze" competitive behavior, noting that CEO Seth Schofield did an extraordinary job but could only achieve survival.
- **Lesson**: A competitively-beset business requires far more managerial skill than a business with fine economics. But even extraordinary management in a bad industry earns only survival, not prosperity. Industry structure trumps managerial talent.
- **Application**: Choose your industry carefully. No amount of operational excellence can overcome terrible industry economics. If you're in a structurally bad industry, the ceiling on your success is very low no matter how talented your team.

### Lesson 17: Hidden Liabilities Can Be Existential Threats
- **Context**: Buffett discussed post-retirement health benefits, describing how companies blithely promised open-ended healthcare coverage without booking the liability, and how one company he nearly acquired went bankrupt partly due to these obligations.
- **Lesson**: Open-ended promises create open-ended liabilities. When accounting rules allow costs to be hidden ("out of sight, out of mind"), managers will ignore them until the liabilities become existential threats. In some cases, these hidden obligations threaten the global competitiveness of entire industries.
- **Application**: Audit your business for hidden or deferred liabilities -- pension obligations, environmental costs, deferred maintenance, long-term contracts. If accounting rules let you ignore a cost, that doesn't mean the cost isn't real.

### Lesson 18: Accounting Honesty -- Calling a Tail a Leg Doesn't Make It One
- **Context**: Buffett invoked Lincoln's riddle about how many legs a dog has if you call its tail a leg, applying it to stock option accounting and other managerial attempts to hide costs.
- **Lesson**: Accounting tricks don't change economic reality. Companies incur costs when they deliver something of value to another party, not just when cash changes hands. An important cost should not be ignored simply because it can't be quantified with pinpoint precision.
- **Application**: Build your financials and internal reporting around economic reality, not accounting convenience. If something has a cost, recognize it, even if the exact number is uncertain. Imprecision is not an excuse for omission.

### Lesson 19: Stock Options Are a Real Cost
- **Context**: Buffett made a devastating argument against the practice of not expensing stock options, offering to buy any CEO's "worthless" options and challenging the logic that options aren't compensation.
- **Lesson**: If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it? And if expenses shouldn't go into the calculation of earnings, where should they go? Businesses that hide real costs from shareholders are playing a dangerous game with credibility.
- **Application**: Be honest about all forms of compensation and cost. Equity-based compensation dilutes existing owners and must be accounted for transparently. Any system that hides real costs eventually undermines trust.

### Lesson 20: Keep Corporate Overhead Ruthlessly Low
- **Context**: Berkshire's after-tax corporate overhead was under 1% of operating earnings. They had no legal, personnel, PR, investor relations, or strategic planning departments. Buffett noted no correlation between high corporate costs and good performance.
- **Lesson**: Simpler, low-cost operations are more likely to operate effectively than bureaucratic ones. Every dollar of corporate overhead directly reduces the value of the business to shareholders. The "tithing" that operations make to headquarters hurts not just earnings but capital values.
- **Application**: Regularly question every corporate overhead function. Each dollar spent on headquarters is a dollar not available for operations, investment, or shareholders. Companies like Wal-Mart, Nucor, and GEICO prove that lean headquarters correlate with superior performance.

### Lesson 21: Capital Allocation Is a Separate and Critical Skill
- **Context**: Buffett discussed how Berkshire's look-through earnings needed to grow from $604 million to $1.8 billion by 2000, requiring both excellent performance from operating businesses and skill in capital allocation.
- **Lesson**: Even with excellent operating businesses, a company cannot thrive without skillful capital allocation. How earnings are reinvested, distributed, or deployed is a distinct discipline from running operations -- and equally important.
- **Application**: Don't assume that a good operator is automatically a good capital allocator. Treat capital allocation as a separate, critical competence that deserves dedicated attention and rigorous analysis.

### Lesson 22: Long-Term Focus Must Still Produce Results
- **Context**: Buffett warned against managers who perpetually blame poor results on their "long-term focus," comparing it to Alice in Wonderland's frustration with "jam tomorrow."
- **Lesson**: If plantings made confidently are repeatedly followed by disappointing harvests, something is wrong with the farmer -- or the farm. Long-term thinking is no excuse for indefinite underperformance. For some companies and industries, there simply is no good long-term strategy.
- **Application**: Hold yourself accountable even while thinking long-term. Strategies planted years ago should be bearing fruit now. If they consistently aren't, re-examine both the strategy and whether the business itself is viable.

### Lesson 23: Attract the Right Shareholders (and Partners)
- **Context**: Buffett explained why Berkshire never split its stock, arguing that their owner-related policies assembled the best shareholder base of any widely-held American corporation, with rational long-term thinking and minimal trading friction.
- **Lesson**: The quality of your shareholders (or partners, or customers) matters enormously. Policies that attract patient, rational, long-term owners create a virtuous cycle: the stock trades near intrinsic value, trading costs are minimal, and management can focus on long-term value creation.
- **Application**: Design your business policies to attract the kind of stakeholders you want. If you optimize for short-term traders or fickle customers, you'll get them -- and the instability that comes with them.

### Lesson 24: Use Debt Sparingly and Only When Structured Properly
- **Context**: Buffett described Berkshire's general aversion to debt, especially short-term debt, while acknowledging they would incur modest amounts when properly structured and beneficial to shareholders.
- **Lesson**: Debt is a tool, not a strategy. It should be used sparingly, structured carefully, and only when it clearly benefits owners. Short-term debt is particularly dangerous because it requires refinancing at potentially unfavorable terms.
- **Application**: Maintain a conservative balance sheet. When you do use debt, ensure it is long-term, well-structured, and genuinely accretive to owner value. The businesses that survive crises are the ones that aren't over-leveraged.

### Lesson 25: The Power of Extraordinary Individuals Over Institutional Size
- **Context**: Buffett described how Scott Fetzer under Ralph Schey earned $110 million pre-tax on only $116 million of equity, distributing more than 100% of earnings while growing the business -- achieved without leverage.
- **Lesson**: An outstanding manager can produce extraordinary returns on capital by running a tight operation -- reducing inventory, minimizing fixed asset investment, and distributing excess capital. This kind of capital efficiency is rare and immensely valuable.
- **Application**: Find and empower exceptional operators. Give them autonomy, measure them on return on equity, and let them distribute excess capital rather than waste it on empire-building.

## Memorable Quotes

> "We have a firm policy about issuing shares of Berkshire, doing so only when we receive as much value as we give."
-- Context: Explaining Berkshire's disciplined approach to equity issuance and why they resist diluting shareholders.

> "We wish to increase Berkshire's size only when doing that also increases the wealth of its owners."
-- Context: Drawing the distinction between growing a company's size versus growing per-share value for owners.

> "A tolerance for short-term swings improves our long-term prospects. In baseball lingo, our performance yardstick is slugging percentage, not batting average."
-- Context: Explaining why Berkshire welcomes volatility in annual results rather than trying to smooth earnings.

> "We've long felt that the only value of stock forecasters is to make fortune tellers look good."
-- Context: Discussing why short-term market predictions are unreliable, even while making a rare long-term forecast about lower future returns.

> "Short-term market forecasts are poison and should be kept locked up in a safe place, away from children and also from grown-ups who behave in the market like children."
-- Context: Warning about the dangers of acting on short-term market predictions.

> "There is no job in the world that is more fun than running Berkshire and I count myself lucky to be where I am."
-- Context: Returning to Berkshire full-time after his stint as Interim Chairman of Salomon Inc.

> "In the search, we adopt the same attitude one might find appropriate in looking for a spouse: It pays to be active, interested and open-minded, but it does not pay to be in a hurry."
-- Context: Describing Berkshire's approach to finding acquisition targets.

> "Fanaticism consists of redoubling your effort when you've forgotten your aim."
-- Context: Quoting Santayana to describe acquisition-hungry managers who keep overpaying for bad deals.

> "In this corporate equivalent of a Head Start program, the CEO receives the education but the stockholders pay the tuition."
-- Context: Describing what happens when serial acquirers finally face reality and announce massive restructuring charges.

> "I kissed and they croaked."
-- Context: Buffett's summary of his early attempts to buy cheap, mediocre businesses.

> "Practice doesn't make perfect; practice makes permanent."
-- Context: Advice from a golf pro that inspired Buffett to shift from buying fair businesses at good prices to good businesses at fair prices.

> "I revised my strategy and tried to buy good businesses at fair prices rather than fair businesses at good prices."
-- Context: The pivotal strategic shift that defined Buffett's later career.

> "If I don't sell the company, my executor will, and I'd rather pick the home for it."
-- Context: Jack Ringwalt's reasoning for selling National Indemnity to Buffett.

> "It's only when the tide goes out that you learn who's been swimming naked."
-- Context: Discussing how Hurricane Andrew revealed that many insurers had grossly inadequate catastrophe reinsurance.

> "A fool and his money are soon invited everywhere."
-- Context: Describing the reinsurance market, where Berkshire rejects more than 98% of business offered to them.

> "Growth benefits investors only when the business in point can invest at incremental returns that are enticing -- in other words, only when each dollar used to finance the growth creates over a dollar of long-term market value."
-- Context: Explaining why growth is not inherently good and can actually destroy value.

> "For these investors, it would have been far better if Orville had failed to get off the ground at Kitty Hawk: The more the industry has grown, the worse the disaster for owners."
-- Context: Using the airline industry as the prime example of profitless growth destroying investor wealth.

> "What is 'investing' if it is not the act of seeking value at least sufficient to justify the amount paid?"
-- Context: Arguing that the term "value investing" is redundant.

> "What counts for most people in investing is not how much they know, but rather how realistically they define what they don't know."
-- Context: Explaining why staying within your circle of competence matters more than having a wide circle.

> "An investor needs to do very few things right as long as he or she avoids big mistakes."
-- Context: Arguing that avoiding catastrophic errors is more important than finding brilliant opportunities.

> "If plantings made confidently are repeatedly followed by disappointing harvests, something is wrong with the farmer."
-- Context: Challenging managers who perpetually excuse poor results by claiming a long-term focus.

> "Even Alice, after listening to the Queen lecture her about 'jam tomorrow,' finally insisted, 'It must come sometimes to jam today.'"
-- Context: Warning that long-term strategy must eventually produce tangible results.

> "For certain companies, and even for some industries, there simply is no good long-term strategy."
-- Context: A sobering reminder that some businesses are structurally doomed regardless of strategy.

> "A competitively-beset business such as USAir requires far more managerial skill than does a business with fine economics. Unfortunately, though, the near-term reward for skill in the airline business is simply survival, not prosperity."
-- Context: Discussing why industry economics trump managerial talent.

> "We have no ability to forecast the economics of the investment banking business, the airline industry, or the paper industry."
-- Context: Admitting ignorance about certain industries, a confession subsequently confirmed by poor results in USAir.

> "How many legs does a dog have if you call his tail a leg? Four, because calling a tail a leg does not make it a leg."
-- Context: Lincoln's riddle applied to accounting games, particularly the refusal to recognize stock option costs.

> "If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it? And, if expenses shouldn't go into the calculation of earnings, where in the world should they go?"
-- Context: Buffett's logical dismantling of the argument against expensing stock options.

> "So if you're a CEO and subscribe to this 'no cash-no cost' theory of accounting, I'll make you an offer you can't refuse: Give us a call at Berkshire and we will happily sell you insurance in exchange for a bundle of long-term options on your company's stock."
-- Context: A satirical offer exposing the absurdity of claiming stock options have no cost.

> "In health-care, open-ended promises have created open-ended liabilities that in a few cases loom so large as to threaten the global competitiveness of major American industries."
-- Context: Discussing the danger of uncapped post-retirement health benefit promises.

> "Charlie and I have observed no correlation between high corporate costs and good corporate performance. In fact, we see the simpler, low-cost operation as more likely to operate effectively than its bureaucratic brethren."
-- Context: Explaining why Berkshire operates with almost no corporate staff and extremely low overhead.

> "For years I argued passionately against corporate jets. But finally my dogma was run over by my karma."
-- Context: A humorous admission about his corporate jet, "The Indefensible."

> "I treasure those extraordinary Berkshire managers who are working well past normal retirement age and who concomitantly are achieving results much superior to those of their younger competitors."
-- Context: Discussing the value of experienced, dedicated managers who continue working long past typical retirement.

> "It's hard to teach a new dog old tricks."
-- Context: A characteristically inverted aphorism about why he values long-tenured managers.

> "The business elite risks losing its credibility on issues of significance to society -- about which it may have much of value to say -- when it advocates the incredible on issues of significance to itself."
-- Context: Warning that business leaders who lobby against honest accounting on stock options undermine their credibility on everything else.
