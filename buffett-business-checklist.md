# The Buffett Business Evaluation Checklist

> *"We simply attempt to be fearful when others are greedy and to be greedy only when others are fearful."* — [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md)

A practical 3-stage framework for evaluating whether a business can survive and thrive, distilled from 1,070 lessons across 49 years of Warren Buffett's shareholder letters.

**[Korean Version](ko/buffett-business-checklist.md)**

---

## How to Use This Checklist

```
Stage 1: GATHER          Stage 2: FILTER          Stage 3: SCORE
(28 questions)     ->    (7 binary gates)    ->  (moat combinations)
Collect the facts        Pass or fail?            How strong, really?
```

**Stage 1** ensures you have all the information Buffett would want before making a judgment. **Stage 2** applies his non-negotiable mental model — any single failure is a deal-breaker. **Stage 3** evaluates how the competitive advantages actually combine, because a moat is rarely one thing; it's the synergy between multiple elements.

Each section links to a **deep-dive reference file** with full evidence from the original letters. Follow the links when you need the *why* behind any question.

## Stage 1: Intelligence Gathering

*"Risk comes from not knowing what you're doing."* — [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md)

Before any judgment, collect these facts. Buffett never evaluates a business he doesn't understand deeply. Each dimension maps to a core theme from his letters.

### 1.1 Industry & Arena (Theme: Industry Economics [1977])

> Deep dive: [Cost Advantage & Scale](business-reference/03-cost-advantage-and-scale.md) — capital intensity, industry economics, structural cost positions

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | What industry is this business in? Can you explain the business model in one paragraph? | If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough |
| 2 | Is this industry growing, stable, or declining? What is the 10-year trend? | Tailwinds vs. headwinds — the most important strategic choice [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md) |
| 3 | How capital-intensive is the industry? What is the typical capex-to-revenue ratio? | Capital-hungry industries destroy returns even for winners [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |
| 4 | How fast does the industry change technologically? | Fast change precludes investment certainty [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |

### 1.2 Competitive Position (Theme: Moats [1986, 2007])

> Deep dive: [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) — moat types, durability tests, widening vs. narrowing | [Pricing Power & Brand](business-reference/02-pricing-power-and-brand.md) — the single best test of a moat

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 5 | What is the company's market share, and how has it changed over 5-10 years? | Stable or growing share signals a working moat |
| 6 | Can the company raise prices without losing customers? When was the last time it did? | Pricing power is the single best test of a moat [1983](markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |
| 7 | What is the company's cost structure relative to competitors? | Low-cost producer = strongest moat in commodity businesses [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md) |
| 8 | Does the company have a recognizable brand? Can consumers identify it without prompting? | Acme Brick: 75% brand recognition in the brick industry [2000](markdown/buffett-letter-2000.md) |

### 1.3 Financial Profile (Theme: Capital Allocation [1977, 1986])

> Deep dive: [Capital Allocation](business-reference/04-capital-allocation.md) — ROE vs. EPS, owner earnings, capital-light compounding, the allocation decision tree

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 9 | What is the return on equity (ROE) over the past 10 years? Is it consistently above 15%? | ROE, not EPS, is the real measure of performance [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md) |
| 10 | How much capital investment is required to maintain current earnings? | Owner earnings = reported earnings + depreciation - maintenance capex [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md) |
| 11 | What does the company do with its free cash flow? (Reinvest / acquire / buyback / dividend / hoard) | The allocation decision determines the company's future [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |
| 12 | What is the debt-to-equity ratio? Could the company survive 2 years of zero revenue? | "Cash is oxygen — never thought about when present, the only thing in mind when absent" [2014](markdown/buffett-letter-2014.md) |

### 1.4 Management Quality (Theme: Owner-Operator Mindset [1986, 2007])

> Deep dive: [Management Quality](business-reference/05-management-quality.md) — skin in the game, honest reporting, the institutional imperative, red flags

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 13 | Does the CEO/founder think and act like an owner? How much of their net worth is in the company? | Skin in the game is non-negotiable [2003](markdown/buffett-letter-2003.md), [2024](markdown/buffett-letter-2024.md) |
| 14 | What is management's track record on capital allocation decisions? | Operating skill and allocation skill are separate competencies [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md) |
| 15 | Does management admit mistakes openly? Search the last 5 annual reports for "mistake" or "error." | Buffett used these words 16 times in 5 years; many companies: zero [2024](markdown/buffett-letter-2024.md) |
| 16 | What is the management turnover rate? Are key people staying? | Great operators stay because they love the work, not the money [2000](markdown/buffett-letter-2000.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |

### 1.5 Customer & Product (Theme: Consumer Franchise [1983])

> Deep dive: [Pricing Power & Brand](business-reference/02-pricing-power-and-brand.md) — franchise vs. commodity, consumer habit, brand durability

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 17 | Would customers notice or care if this company disappeared tomorrow? | If the answer is no, there is no franchise [1983](markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md), [1991](markdown/buffett-letter-1991.md) |
| 18 | How do customers find this company? (Referral, habit, search, advertising, switching cost) | Habitual purchases and high switching costs create durability |
| 19 | What is the customer retention rate? | Repeat business = proof of value delivery |
| 20 | Is the product or service a "must-have" or a "nice-to-have"? | Essential services have the most resilient demand [2013](markdown/buffett-letter-2013.md) |

### 1.6 Risk Landscape (Theme: Financial Fortitude [2008, 2014])

> Deep dive: [Risk, Leverage & Survival](business-reference/06-risk-leverage-survival.md) — leverage traps, cash reserves, the "survive first" doctrine, Russian roulette math

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 21 | What would destroy this business? List 3 specific scenarios. | Reverse engineer from failure — Munger's inversion principle [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md) |
| 22 | Is this business exposed to regulatory risk? How dependent is it on government policy? | Regulatory change can eliminate a moat overnight [2023](markdown/buffett-letter-2023.md) |
| 23 | How concentrated is the revenue? (Top customer %, single product %) | Concentration = fragility |
| 24 | Does the company use significant leverage? | "Leverage amplifies returns in good times but creates existential risk in bad times" [2008](markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md) |

### 1.7 Valuation & Context (Theme: Valuation Math [1992, 2000])

> Deep dive: [Acquisition Discipline](business-reference/07-acquisition-discipline.md) — intrinsic value, margin of safety, the "buy the whole company" test, price vs. value

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 25 | What is the current price-to-owner-earnings ratio? How does it compare to 10-year average? | "Price is what you pay, value is what you get" [2008](markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md) |
| 26 | What would you pay for the entire business if you could never sell it? | The "buy the whole company" test eliminates speculative bias |
| 27 | Is the current market sentiment fearful or greedy toward this business/sector? | Contrarian opportunity arises from others' fear [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md), [2008](markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md) |
| 28 | What are comparable businesses selling for? | Relative valuation provides a sanity check |

## Stage 2: Buffett's Mental Model Filter

*"We've long felt that the only value of stock forecasters is to make fortune tellers look good."* — [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md)

These are **binary gates**. The business must pass ALL SEVEN to proceed to scoring. A single failure means stop. Buffett's four acquisition criteria [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md) plus three additional gates extracted from 49 years of evolved thinking.

### The Seven Gates

| Gate | The Question | Pass Criteria | If You Fail | Deep Dive | Source |
|------|-------------|---------------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| **G1: Understandability** | Can I explain how this business makes money in 3 sentences? | You can describe the revenue model, customer, and value proposition clearly | Stop. "Never invest in a business you cannot understand." | — | [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md), [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md), [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md) |
| **G2: Favorable Long-Term Prospects** | Will this business be stronger in 10 years? | Industry has tailwinds or stable demand; no existential tech disruption on the horizon | Stop. "Turnarounds seldom turn." | [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) | [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md), [1979](markdown/buffett-letter-1979.md), [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md) |
| **G3: Honest & Competent Management** | Do I trust these people with my money? | Management has skin in the game, admits mistakes, and has a track record of rational allocation | Stop. "We've never succeeded in making a good deal with a bad person." | [Management Quality](business-reference/05-management-quality.md) | [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md), [1989](markdown/buffett-letter-1989.md), [2024](markdown/buffett-letter-2024.md) |
| **G4: Sensible Price** | Am I paying less than intrinsic value with a meaningful margin of safety? | Price-to-owner-earnings below historical average; you'd be comfortable if markets closed for 5 years | Stop. "What is smart at one price is stupid at another." | [Acquisition Discipline](business-reference/07-acquisition-discipline.md) | [1977](markdown/buffett-letter-1977.md), [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md), [2016](markdown/buffett-letter-2016.md) |
| **G5: Demonstrated Earnings** | Has this business proven it can earn money across varied conditions? | Consistent profitability through at least one recession or downturn | Stop. "Demonstrated consistent earning power — future projections are of no interest to us." | [Capital Allocation](business-reference/04-capital-allocation.md) | [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |
| **G6: Financial Independence** | Can this business survive without external financing? | Positive free cash flow; no need to raise capital or refinance in distress | Stop. "Never depend on the kindness of strangers." | [Risk & Survival](business-reference/06-risk-leverage-survival.md) | [2008](markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md), [2014](markdown/buffett-letter-2014.md) |
| **G7: No Existential Leverage** | Could a bad year kill this business? | Debt-to-equity manageable; no single-event wipeout risk; no Russian roulette | Stop. "It is madness to risk losing what you need in pursuing what you simply desire." | [Risk & Survival](business-reference/06-risk-leverage-survival.md) | [2014](markdown/buffett-letter-2014.md), [2017](markdown/buffett-letter-2017.md) |

### Scoring the Filter

```
7/7 gates passed  ->  Proceed to Stage 3
6/7 gates passed  ->  STOP. Identify the failing gate. Can it be resolved?
                      If not structural, revisit in 6 months.
5/7 or below      ->  REJECT. Move on.
```

## Stage 3: Moat Combination Scoring

*"A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital."* — [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md)

A moat is rarely a single thing. The most durable businesses combine multiple advantages that **reinforce each other**. This scoring system evaluates 6 moat types individually, then applies multipliers for synergistic combinations.

> Deep dive: [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) — all 6 moat types with Buffett's evidence | [Cost Advantage & Scale](business-reference/03-cost-advantage-and-scale.md) — the GEICO model and scale economics

### 3.1 The Six Moat Types

Score each moat type from 0 to 10 based on the evidence gathered in Stage 1.

| Moat Type | What It Means | Score 0 | Score 5 | Score 10 | Deep Dive | Buffett Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|---------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **M1: Cost Advantage** | Structurally lower costs than any competitor | No cost advantage; commodity pricing | Some cost advantage but not structural | Structural, widening cost gap that competitors cannot close | [Cost & Scale](business-reference/03-cost-advantage-and-scale.md) | GEICO: 15+ points below competitors [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md), [2020](markdown/buffett-letter-2020.md) |
| **M2: Brand / Reputation** | Customers choose you by name, pay premium | No brand recognition; interchangeable | Recognized brand but limited pricing power | Iconic brand with strong emotional connection; pricing power proven | [Pricing & Brand](business-reference/02-pricing-power-and-brand.md) | See's Candies, Coca-Cola [1983](markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md), [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md) |
| **M3: Switching Cost / Lock-in** | Customers can't leave easily | Zero switching cost; easy to replace | Moderate inconvenience to switch | Deep integration; switching is painful and expensive | [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) | Enterprise software, railroads [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md), [2010](markdown/buffett-letter-2010.md) |
| **M4: Network Effects** | Product gets better as more people use it | No network effects | Mild network benefits | Strong, self-reinforcing network where value grows exponentially | [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) | American Express charge card network [1991](markdown/buffett-letter-1991.md) |
| **M5: Scale Economics** | Bigger is permanently cheaper | No scale advantage | Moderate scale benefit | Dominant scale that creates self-reinforcing cost/distribution advantage | [Cost & Scale](business-reference/03-cost-advantage-and-scale.md) | NFM: $44M to $132M in flat market [1986](markdown/buffett-letter-1986.md), [1989](markdown/buffett-letter-1989.md) |
| **M6: Regulatory / Structural Barrier** | Legal or structural protection from competition | No barriers; easy entry | Some regulatory requirements | Significant barriers: licenses, rights-of-way, natural monopoly | [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) | BNSF railroad, BHE utilities [2010](markdown/buffett-letter-2010.md), [2015](markdown/buffett-letter-2015.md) |

### 3.2 Individual Moat Score

```
Raw Moat Score = M1 + M2 + M3 + M4 + M5 + M6
Maximum possible: 60
```

| Raw Score | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 0-15 | **No moat.** This is a commodity business. Industry economics will determine fate. |
| 16-25 | **Narrow moat.** Some advantage exists but could erode under competitive pressure. |
| 26-40 | **Wide moat.** Strong competitive position. Multiple advantages at work. |
| 41-60 | **Castle with a moat.** Exceptional position. Think See's, GEICO, Coca-Cola. |

### 3.3 The Combination Multipliers

This is the key insight: **moats don't just add — they multiply when they reinforce each other.** Buffett's most admired businesses have combinations that create virtuous cycles.

Check which combinations are present. Each applicable combination adds a multiplier.

> Deep dive: [Competitive Moats](business-reference/01-competitive-moats.md) — combination mechanics and virtuous cycles

| Combo | Combination | Why It Multiplies | Multiplier | Buffett Reference |
|-------|------------|-------------------|------------|-------------------|
| **C1** | Cost Advantage + Scale | Lower costs attract more customers, more customers lower costs further. Virtuous cycle. | x1.3 | GEICO: direct model + growing scale = ever-widening cost gap [1996](markdown/buffett-letter-1996.md), [2008](markdown/buffett-letter-2008.md) |
| **C2** | Brand + Pricing Power | Strong brand enables price increases that fund brand investment that strengthens brand. | x1.3 | See's Candies: raised prices every year for 50 years, strengthening the franchise [1983](markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md), [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |
| **C3** | Switching Cost + Network Effects | Users can't leave AND the product improves as more join. Near-impregnable. | x1.4 | American Express: merchant acceptance + cardholder base + rewards [1991](markdown/buffett-letter-1991.md), [2022](markdown/buffett-letter-2022.md) |
| **C4** | Scale + Regulatory Barrier | Scale makes it economically impossible for new entrants even if regulation allowed it. | x1.3 | BNSF: can't build a new transcontinental railroad [2010](markdown/buffett-letter-2010.md) |
| **C5** | Cost Advantage + Brand | Lowest cost AND strongest brand = competitors have no positioning. Nowhere to attack. | x1.5 | GEICO + brand advertising: lowest price AND top-of-mind awareness [2005](markdown/buffett-letter-2005.md), [2020](markdown/buffett-letter-2020.md) |
| **C6** | Capital-Light + Any Moat | Low capital requirements mean the moat generates enormous free cash flow. Compounding machine. | x1.4 | See's: $32M invested, $1.35B returned. Capital-light amplifies any moat [2007](markdown/buffett-letter-2007.md) |

### 3.4 Final Moat Score Calculation

```
Final Score = Raw Moat Score x (Product of all applicable multipliers) x (10/6)

Cap at 100.
```

**Example: GEICO**
- M1 (Cost): 10, M2 (Brand): 8, M3 (Switching): 3, M4 (Network): 1, M5 (Scale): 9, M6 (Regulatory): 5
- Raw Score: 36
- Combos: C1 (Cost+Scale: x1.3), C5 (Cost+Brand: x1.5), C6 (Capital-Light: x1.4)
- Final: 36 x 1.3 x 1.5 x 1.4 x 1.667 = **164** -> capped at **100**

**Example: A Generic Textile Mill (Berkshire's original business)**
- M1: 2, M2: 0, M3: 0, M4: 0, M5: 2, M6: 0
- Raw Score: 4
- No combination multipliers apply
- Final: 4 x 1.667 = **7**

### 3.5 Interpreting the Final Score

| Score | Rating | What Buffett Would Say | Action |
|-------|--------|----------------------|--------|
| **80-100** | **Extraordinary** | "Buy it and never sell." This is a compounding machine. | Buy at any fair price. Hold forever. [1988](markdown/buffett-letter-1988.md) |
| **60-79** | **Excellent** | "A wonderful business at a fair price." Strong, durable position. | Buy with margin of safety. Hold for decades. [1989](markdown/buffett-letter-1989.md) |
| **40-59** | **Good** | "A good business, but not great." Returns depend on management quality. | Buy only at a clear discount. Monitor the moat annually. [1992](markdown/buffett-letter-1992.md) |
| **20-39** | **Mediocre** | "Management reputation will stay intact against these economics." Structural headwinds. | Avoid unless extraordinary management + deep discount. [1989](markdown/buffett-letter-1989.md) |
| **0-19** | **Avoid** | "About as rewarding as struggling in quicksand." No durable advantage. | Walk away. No price is low enough. [1983](markdown/buffett-letter-1983.md) |

## Appendix: The Underlying Philosophy

### Why Three Stages?

Buffett's process, distilled across 49 years, is not a single test. It's a **funnel**:

1. **You can't judge what you don't understand.** Stage 1 forces thorough understanding before any opinion forms.
2. **Some things are non-negotiable.** Stage 2 reflects Buffett's repeated insistence that certain criteria cannot be traded off. A dishonest management team cannot be compensated by a low price. A dying industry cannot be saved by a genius CEO.
3. **Competitive advantage is combinatorial.** Stage 3 captures the insight that the most durable businesses have multiple reinforcing advantages — and the interaction between advantages matters more than any single one.

### The Fractal Principle

This checklist applies at every scale:

- **Evaluating a stock** -> Full checklist
- **Evaluating a job offer** -> Does the company pass Stage 2?
- **Evaluating your own business** -> Where does your moat score fall? Which combinations are you missing?
- **Evaluating a partnership** -> Does your partner pass Gate G3?

> *"The power of Buffett's letters is not that they contain complex ideas — most can be stated in a sentence — but that they demonstrate what happens when simple truths are applied with discipline and patience for half a century."*
