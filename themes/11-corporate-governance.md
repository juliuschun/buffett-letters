# Chapter 11: Corporate Governance and Incentive Design

## Core Thesis

Buffett's governance philosophy rests on a single insight: rules cannot substitute for alignment. Statutory definitions of "independence," elaborate compliance frameworks, and thick corporate governance manuals are theater if the people in the boardroom do not have their own money at stake, the business savvy to evaluate proposals, and the courage to dissent. Real governance is achieved through three mechanisms: genuine ownership by directors and managers, compensation tied to controllable outcomes, and a culture that ruthlessly fights the institutional forces -- arrogance, bureaucracy, complacency -- that destroy great enterprises from within.

---

## 1. "Who Washes a Rental Car?" -- Director Ownership [2003, 2019, 2024]

The most memorable governance line Buffett ever wrote was a five-word question:

> "Who ever washes a rental car?" [2003]

The answer, of course, is no one -- because renters have no ownership stake in the vehicle. Buffett applied this directly to corporate boards. At the time, regulatory definitions of "independence" were, in his view, nonsensical: a director getting 100% of income from board fees was deemed independent, while one getting 3% from legal fees was not. The director living off board fees had the stronger incentive to stay silent and keep the position [2003].

At Berkshire, every director owned at least $4 million in stock purchased in the open market -- not received through grants or options. Their money was at risk alongside shareholders'. This created genuine alignment:

> "Directors with significant personal ownership will challenge foolish proposals because their own money is at stake." [2003]

Buffett returned to this theme in 2019, pointing out the irony that directors earning $250,000-$300,000 in fees were classified as "independent," while directors with large personal stakes were often deemed to lack independence. He noted directors at a lagging company who had never bought a single share with their own money [2019].

By 2024, the principle was embedded in Berkshire's DNA:

> "If you lose money, so do we." [2024]

Berkshire directors and managers held very large personal investments relative to their compensation, and the company did not use stock options or one-sided compensation instruments [2024].

## 2. GEICO's Two-Variable Compensation Plan [1996, 1997, 1998, 1999]

If the rental car analogy is Buffett's most memorable governance line, GEICO's compensation plan is his most detailed example of incentive design done right. He described it across four consecutive letters, each time adding nuance.

The plan tied bonuses for executives (and profit-sharing for all 9,000 employees) to exactly two variables:

1. **Growth in voluntary auto policies** (the metric that drives long-term franchise value)
2. **Underwriting profitability on seasoned business** (policies older than one year, measuring the quality of growth)

The design was intentional in what it excluded. New business costs were deliberately left out of the bonus calculation, because acquiring new policyholders is expensive in year one but enormously valuable over time. Penalizing managers for acquisition costs would have discouraged the very growth that built GEICO's franchise [1996, 1999].

Buffett contrasted this with "lottery ticket" compensation like broad stock options:

> "'Lottery ticket' arrangements like broad stock options, whose outcomes are beyond the employee's control, produce quixotic payoffs that may actually discourage focused behavior." [1996]

He formalized the principles underlying GEICO's plan:

- **Tailored to the economics of the specific business.** What works for an insurance company will not work for a railroad.
- **Simple enough that results can be easily measured.** Complexity in compensation breeds gaming; simplicity breeds alignment.
- **Directly related to the daily activities of participants.** Employees must be able to see how their work affects their pay.
- **Never raise the bar after good performance.** "At Berkshire, we never greet good work by raising the bar" [1996].

He also insisted on individual accountability: "If you bat .350 at Berkshire, you can be sure you will get paid commensurately even if the rest of the team bats .200" [1996]. GEICO's investment manager Lou Simpson was compensated based on his own four-year investment performance, not overall company results.

The GEICO model was extended to other subsidiaries. The principle was always the same: "merit badges, not lottery tickets" [1997]. Pay people based on what they can actually influence.

## 3. "Potted Plant" Directors and the Suppression of Dissent [2003]

Buffett listed three qualities every director needs: business savvy, shareholder orientation, and genuine interest in the company. Business savvy, he said, was the rarest and most critical:

> "The rarest of these qualities is business savvy -- and if it is lacking, the other two are of little help." [2003]

Without the ability to critically evaluate business proposals, a director adds no value regardless of fame, intelligence, or social standing:

> "I could be replaced, without loss, by a potted plant." [2003]

This was not hypothetical. Buffett had served on boards where he lacked the domain expertise to contribute meaningfully. In those settings, he kept quiet -- and recognized that his silence, however well-intentioned, enabled bad decisions to proceed unchallenged [2003].

The deeper problem was structural. Boardroom dynamics created powerful pressure toward conformity and passivity. Even good people, placed in boardroom settings, tended to go along with management rather than challenge it:

> "Despite their shortcomings, 'boardroom atmosphere' almost invariably sedates their fiduciary genes." [2003]

This was especially visible in mutual fund governance. Fund directors routinely rehired underperforming managers and approved excessive fees, despite being statutorily "independent." The social pressure of the boardroom overrode individual judgment. The institutional environment itself suppressed dissent [2003].

Buffett's solution was not more rules but more ownership. Directors with real money at stake would overcome the social pressure because the financial consequences of passivity were personal.

## 4. CEO Pay as the Acid Test of Governance Reform [2003, 2006]

Buffett identified executive compensation as the clearest signal of whether corporate governance was real or performative:

> "In judging whether Corporate America is serious about reforming itself, CEO pay remains the acid test. To date, the results aren't encouraging." [2003]

The problem was structural asymmetry: the CEO cared intensely about compensation, while the board treated it as "play money" belonging to shareholders they had never met. The outcome of such lopsided negotiations was always predictable [2003].

The mechanism of escalation was equally predictable. Compensation consultants used "peer" comparisons to ratchet pay ever higher. Each company's CEO pay became the floor for the next round of benchmarking. The consultants knew "who buttered their bread":

> "There are two classes of clients you don't want to offend -- actual and potential." [2003]

The system was self-reinforcing and structurally incapable of self-correction. In the 1890s, Buffett noted, Samuel Gompers described the goal of organized labor as "More!" -- and in the 1990s, America's CEOs adopted his battle cry [2002].

By 2006, the problem had only worsened. Buffett served as a one-man compensation committee for approximately 40 CEOs, spending virtually no time on it. Meanwhile, compensation consultants had turned executive pay into an ever-escalating arms race through cherry-picked peer comparisons [2006].

The contrast with Berkshire was sharp. Buffett's own salary remained modest, and he insisted that compensation for subsidiary CEOs be simple, fair, and tied directly to operating performance rather than benchmarked against artificial "peer groups."

## 5. Stock Options as Lottery Tickets [1992, 1996, 1998, 2024]

Buffett's opposition to stock options operated on two levels. The first was accounting honesty (covered in Chapter 10): options are a real cost that must be expensed. The second was incentive design: options are a terrible way to compensate people.

The core problem is that stock options reward factors outside the employee's control. A rising market lifts all stock prices, rewarding option holders regardless of their contribution. A falling market punishes them regardless of their excellence. The payoff is determined by forces entirely beyond the individual's influence -- making options functionally identical to lottery tickets:

> "'Lottery ticket' arrangements like broad stock options produce quixotic payoffs that may actually discourage focused behavior." [1996]

Worse, options create one-sided incentives. The holder participates in all the upside but bears none of the downside. This asymmetry encourages excessive risk-taking: if the gamble pays off, the option holder gets rich; if it fails, they lose nothing but the unrealized value of paper. In 1998, Buffett replaced General Re's stock option plan with a cash compensation plan tied to operating performance -- proving that alternatives existed [1998].

By 2024, Buffett explicitly linked options to governance failure:

> "One-sided compensation (like stock options with no downside) encourages reckless behavior." [2024]

Berkshire directors and managers had "very large personal investments" and did not use options. The principle was simple: "If you lose money, so do we" [2024].

## 6. Pete Liegl's Voluntary $100K Salary [2005, 2024]

Pete Liegl of Forest River became Buffett's ultimate example of how the best managers approach compensation. When Buffett acquired Forest River and asked Liegl to name his own pay, Liegl's response was:

> "Well, I looked at Berkshire's proxy statement and I wouldn't want to make more than my boss, so pay me $100,000 per year." [2024]

This was not false modesty. Liegl added a 10% bonus on incremental earnings -- meaning he would share in the value he created above a baseline. The structure was simple, aligned with owners, and voluntary. Over the next 19 years, Liegl "shot the lights out," massively outperforming all competitors in the RV industry [2024].

The Liegl story illustrated multiple Buffett governance principles simultaneously:

- **Compensation should share value creation, not extract it.** Liegl's bonus was on incremental earnings, not total revenue.
- **The best managers are motivated by the work itself.** Liegl chose to work for $100K not because he needed money but because he loved running Forest River.
- **Autonomy is the real currency.** When Liegl had previously sold Forest River to an LBO operator who began telling him how to run it, Liegl left, the business went bankrupt, and Liegl repurchased and rebuilt it [2005].

Buffett also contrasted the Liegl model with Japanese company executives, who are "far less aggressive in their compensation programs than their U.S. counterparts" -- and whose companies Berkshire was increasingly investing in [2024].

## 7. The ABCs of Decay: Arrogance, Bureaucracy, Complacency [2014]

In the 2014 letter, Buffett named the three forces that destroy even the strongest enterprises:

> "The ABCs of business decay -- Arrogance, Bureaucracy, and Complacency." [2014]

He cited four once-unassailable companies -- General Motors, IBM, Sears Roebuck, and U.S. Steel -- that were destroyed by these "corporate cancers." Each had enjoyed dominant market positions, enormous financial strength, and decades of historical earning power. None of it mattered once arrogance, bureaucracy, and complacency took hold [2014].

The danger was that these forces metastasize silently. They do not announce themselves. By the time they are visible, they may be terminal. The only defense is a vigilant CEO who actively fights institutional decay:

> "Tell me where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." [2014, quoting Charlie Munger]

Buffett made this one of the core criteria for his successor at Berkshire. The next CEO would need to ward off these cancers, recognizing that past success -- the very thing that makes a company feel invulnerable -- breeds the complacency that destroys it [2014].

## 8. The 25-Person Headquarters [1992, 1995, 1998, 2000, 2001, 2012, 2013, 2015]

Berkshire's absurdly lean corporate headquarters became one of Buffett's most powerful governance statements. The numbers progressed across the letters:

- **12 people** managing 47,566 employees [1998]: "Despite this alarming trend toward corporate bloat, our after-tax overhead last year was about $3.5 million, or well under one basis point (.01 of 1%) of the value of the assets we manage."
- **13.8 people** managing 112,000 employees across $40 billion in revenue [2000]: Charlie Munger quoted Ben Franklin: "A small leak can sink a great ship."
- **14.8 people** managing 110,000 employees [2001]: One person worked four days a week, hence the decimal.
- **24 people** managing 288,462 employees [2012]: Buffett quipped, "No sense going crazy."
- **25 people** managing 361,270 employees and $225 billion in equity [2013, 2015]: Revenue increased $16 billion in 2015 with zero change in headquarters staff.

The lean headquarters was not merely a cost discipline -- it was a governance philosophy. It forced decision-making to the operating level where knowledge resides. It eliminated the bureaucratic interference that talented managers despise. And it made a cultural statement about where value is created:

> "Charlie and I have observed no correlation between high corporate costs and good corporate performance. In fact, we see the simpler, low-cost operation as more likely to operate effectively than its bureaucratic brethren." [1992]

Berkshire had no legal department, no personnel department, no public relations department, no investor relations department, and no strategic planning department [1992]. The "tithing" that operations make to headquarters hurts not just earnings but capital values -- every overhead dollar reduces the per-share value of the enterprise [1992].

By 2015, the headquarters had zero turnover, and Buffett pointed out that "you have never read about 'restructuring' charges at Berkshire" [2015]. The absence of restructuring was itself proof that the lean structure worked: when you hire only when truly needed and hire people who stay, you never need to restructure.

## 9. Accountability Erodes During Bubbles [2002]

Buffett observed that ethical standards in corporate governance are counter-cyclical -- they decline precisely when markets are strongest and shareholders are happiest:

> "CEOs who traveled the high road did not encounter heavy traffic." [2002]

During the Great Bubble of the late 1990s, behavioral norms declined as stock prices rose. Rising markets created an environment where accountability withered, stakeholders stopped asking tough questions, and otherwise decent executives drifted:

> "I was Snow White but I drifted." [2002, quoting Mae West]

The predictable cycle was: bubbles breed complacency, complacency breeds misconduct, misconduct breeds collapse. The lesson for governance was that the moment to strengthen controls and question results is precisely when everything looks wonderful -- not after the crisis arrives [2002].

## 10. Skin in the Game: The Partnership Ethos [1983, 1994, 2020, 2024]

The foundation of Buffett's governance model was established in the very first principle of Berkshire's 1983 owner's manual:

> "Although our form is corporate, our attitude is partnership." [1983]

This was not a slogan. Buffett and Munger kept their personal fortunes overwhelmingly concentrated in Berkshire shares. In 1994, Buffett promised to keep at least 99% of his net worth in Berkshire for as long as he lived [1994]. The message to shareholders was unambiguous: our interests are identical.

This partnership ethos extended to operating managers. Many of Berkshire's subsidiary CEOs were independently wealthy and did not need to work. They stayed because Berkshire offered what no other company could: complete autonomy, no interference, and the knowledge that their businesses would never be sold or restructured. In 38 years, Berkshire never had a single subsidiary CEO leave to work elsewhere [2002].

The partnership model was also the ultimate governance mechanism. When directors and managers have their personal wealth at stake, they do not need external rules to force good behavior. Their own economic interest aligns them with shareholders automatically. The rental car becomes their own car, and they wash it [2024].

## 11. The Compensation Consultant Problem [2003, 2006]

Buffett identified the compensation consulting industry as a structural enabler of governance failure. Consultants were hired by management to advise on management pay -- a conflict of interest so obvious that it required willful blindness to ignore.

The mechanism was simple: consultants benchmarked each CEO's pay against a "peer group" of comparable companies. Since no CEO wanted to be paid below the median, and since the consultants knew "who buttered their bread," peer group comparisons became a one-way ratchet upward. Each round of benchmarking raised the floor for the next round [2003, 2006].

The consultants protected themselves with a simple rule:

> "There are two classes of clients you don't want to offend -- actual and potential." [2003]

This meant they would never recommend a pay cut, because doing so would cost them both the current client and all future clients who heard about it. The system was structurally incapable of self-correction [2003].

Buffett's solution at Berkshire was to eliminate the middleman entirely. He served as a one-man compensation committee for approximately 40 CEOs, keeping arrangements simple and directly tied to each unit's operating performance [2006].

## 12. Manager Retention Through Autonomy, Not Money [2000, 2002, 2005]

Buffett discovered that the most effective retention tool was not compensation but autonomy. At most large companies, talented divisional managers yearned to become CEOs elsewhere, feeling they had failed if they stayed put. At Berkshire, managers had exactly the jobs they wanted and expected to keep them for life:

> "They concentrate solely on maximizing long-term value of the businesses they 'own' and love." [2000]

The Pete Liegl story encapsulated this perfectly. When an LBO operator acquired Forest River and began telling Liegl how to run it, Liegl left. The business went bankrupt. Liegl repurchased it, rebuilt it, and then specifically sought out Berkshire as a buyer because Berkshire promised never to interfere [2005].

This was governance through culture rather than through compliance. It attracted self-motivated operators who did not need external monitoring and who viewed micromanagement as an insult. The result was a company with 361,270 employees managed by 25 people at headquarters -- and zero involuntary CEO departures in nearly four decades [2015].

---

## 13. Succession as the Board's Primary Job [2003, 2014]

Buffett believed that the single most important governance decision any board makes is choosing the right CEO successor:

> "The most important job of our board is likely to be the selection of successors to Charlie and me, and that is a matter upon which it will focus." [2003]

By 2014, Berkshire's board meetings dedicated substantial time to evaluating internal CEO candidates, preparing for Buffett's eventual replacement. The criteria were explicit: the successor must maintain culture, allocate capital wisely, keep great managers happy, and -- above all -- fight the ABCs of decay [2014].

This long-term preparation was itself a governance statement. Most companies treat succession as a crisis-mode scramble when the CEO departs. Buffett treated it as an ongoing evaluation spanning years, ensuring continuity of the partnership ethos and capital allocation discipline that defined Berkshire [2003].

## The Complete Governance Framework

Across 49 years of letters, Buffett built a governance model that rejected bureaucratic compliance in favor of economic alignment:

1. **Directors must own stock purchased with their own money.** Regulatory "independence" is meaningless without economic skin in the game [2003, 2019, 2024].

2. **Compensation must be specific, simple, and controllable.** Tie bonuses to the two or three variables employees can directly influence. Never use lottery tickets [1996, 1997].

3. **Business savvy is the most important board quality.** Famous names without domain competence can be replaced by potted plants [2003].

4. **Fight the ABCs relentlessly.** Arrogance, Bureaucracy, and Complacency are the corporate cancers that destroy even the strongest companies [2014].

5. **Keep headquarters ruthlessly lean.** A 25-person HQ for a $100B+ enterprise is proof that decentralization works when you hire well [1992-2015].

6. **CEO pay is the acid test.** If boards cannot negotiate compensation with the same intensity as the CEO, governance reform is theater [2003].

7. **Retain through autonomy, not money.** The best managers stay because they love the work and own the outcome, not because of golden handcuffs [2000, 2005].

8. **Succession is the board's primary job.** Prepare for it over years, not in panic [2003, 2014].
