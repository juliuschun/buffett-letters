# Chapter 5: Pricing Power, Cost Discipline, and the Capital-Light Ideal

> *"The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business."*
> — Warren Buffett, Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission testimony, 2010

---

## Introduction

Across 49 years of shareholder letters (1977-2025), Warren Buffett returns to three interlocking themes more than almost any other: the power to set prices, the discipline to control costs, and the dream of a business that generates enormous cash while requiring almost no capital. These are not separate ideas. They are facets of a single insight: **the best business is one that can charge more, spend less, and reinvest almost nothing to keep the machine running.**

This chapter traces those themes through every letter, extracting the frameworks, the numbers, the metaphors, and the case studies that make Buffett's thinking concrete.

---

## 1. Franchise vs. Commodity: The 1983/1991 Framework

### The Original Distinction (1983)

Buffett first articulated the franchise/commodity divide in his 1983 letter, linking it to the concept of Economic Goodwill:

> *"Consumer franchises are a prime source of economic Goodwill."* [1983]

A consumer franchise, Buffett explained, arises when a product or service (1) is needed or desired, (2) has no close substitute in the customer's mind, and (3) is not subject to price regulation. When all three conditions hold, a company can regularly raise prices without fear of volume loss — and the resulting excess returns constitute "economic Goodwill" that, unlike accounting goodwill, **grows** rather than shrinks over time.

> *"During inflation, Goodwill is the gift that keeps giving."* [1983]

This was a radical reframing. Accounting rules demanded that goodwill be amortized — written down to zero over time. But Buffett argued that the *economic* goodwill of a true franchise does the opposite: it compounds. Every price increase that sticks proves the franchise is stronger, not weaker.

### The Full Framework (1991)

By 1991, Buffett had sharpened the distinction into a complete analytical framework. He separated the world into two categories:

**A franchise** exists when a product or service:
1. Is needed or desired
2. Is thought by its customers to have no close substitute
3. Is not subject to price regulation

The presence of all three allows the franchise holder to price aggressively without risking unit volume. The franchise can tolerate mismanagement and still survive — it is, in Buffett's formulation, almost idiot-proof.

**A business** (i.e., a commodity enterprise), by contrast, earns exceptional returns only through being the low-cost operator or by operating in a tight supply environment. In a commodity business, "weights" constantly pull returns toward mediocrity: competitors can match your product, customers shop on price, and any operational edge is temporary.

> *"A franchise can tolerate mismanagement. Inept managers may diminish a franchise's profitability, but they cannot inflict mortal damage. In contrast, a 'business' can be killed by poor management."* [1991]

### The Three Categories (2007)

Buffett refined the framework further in 2007, sorting businesses into three types:

- **The Great** — require little capital, earn high returns, and grow. See's Candies is the archetype.
- **The Good** — earn decent returns and need capital to grow, but the capital is productively deployed. FlightSafety and Scott Fetzer exemplified this.
- **The Gruesome** — require significant capital, earn poor returns, and occasionally need more capital just to survive. Airlines are the canonical example.

> *"The worst sort of business is one that grows rapidly, requires significant capital to engender the growth, and then earns little or no money."* [2007]

### The "Buy Commodities, Sell Brands" Formula (2011)

In 2011, Buffett distilled his investment philosophy into a memorable formula:

> *"Buy commodities, sell brands."* [2011]

This encapsulates the franchise ideal: your inputs are interchangeable raw materials (sugar, cocoa, peanuts for See's; paper and ink for newspapers), purchased at market prices. But your output carries a brand premium that customers willingly pay. The spread between commodity input costs and branded output prices is the franchise's moat, and it widens every time the franchise raises prices while input costs stay flat or rise more slowly.

## 2. See's Candies: Fifty Years of Pricing Power

See's Candies is not merely an example in Buffett's letters — it is **the** example, the case study he returns to more than any other to illustrate what a franchise looks like from the inside. The numbers tell a story of compounding that borders on the absurd.

### The Purchase (1972)

Berkshire acquired See's Candies in 1972 for $25 million. At the time, See's had about $8 million in tangible net assets and was earning approximately $4.2 million pre-tax. Buffett later admitted he almost walked away from the deal because the price seemed high relative to tangible assets — a lesson that taught him the difference between accounting value and economic value.

### The Compounding Machine

From that $25 million purchase, See's became one of the most extraordinary cash generators in American business history. Buffett tracked the cumulative returns across decades:

| Year | Cumulative Pre-Tax Earnings | Capital Invested | Notable Detail |
|------|---------------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1991 | $410 million | $18 million | Profits grew from $4.2M to $42.4M annually |
| 1999 | $857 million | ~$25 million | Approaching $1B on original $25M purchase |
| 2007 | $1.35 billion | $32 million | "Adam and Eve" metaphor introduced |
| 2011 | $1.65 billion | ~$32 million | Carrying value "below zero" |
| 2014 | $1.9 billion | $40 million | 76x cumulative return on purchase price |

By 2014, See's had returned **$1.9 billion in pre-tax earnings** on a $25 million purchase, requiring only $40 million of additional capital investment over 42 years. The business needed almost nothing to grow — it simply raised prices, maintained quality, and mailed the profits to Omaha.

> *"Just as Adam and Eve kick-started an activity that led to six billion humans, See's has given birth to multiple new streams of cash for us."* [2007]

Buffett used this metaphor to make a crucial point: the cash that See's generated didn't just sit in a vault. It funded the purchase of other businesses, which generated their own cash, which funded still more purchases. See's was the seed capital for an empire.

> *"There's no rule that you have to invest money where you've earned it."* [2007]

### The Albuquerque Test (1990)

How do you know if you have a franchise? Buffett offered a vivid empirical test from 1990. When See's briefly considered closing its Albuquerque store:

> *"263 customers wrote or called to protest, many of them saying they would drive to the nearest remaining store — 50 miles away — rather than switch brands."* [1990]

Two hundred and sixty-three letters from a single store in a mid-sized city. This was not brand "awareness" — it was brand *devotion*. No commodity business could generate that response. If your customers will drive 50 miles past your competitors, you have pricing power.

### Quality as Sacred

Buffett repeatedly emphasized that pricing power depends on product quality — and that quality must never be compromised for short-term savings:

> *"See's uses only the finest ingredients. We use no artificial flavors or preservatives."* [multiple years]

> *"Don't mess with my candy."* [2020, describing the customer verdict]

The logic is circular but robust: quality creates loyalty, loyalty creates pricing power, pricing power creates profits, and profits fund continued quality. Break any link in the chain and the franchise erodes.

Chuck Huggins, who managed See's for 34 years, delivered "more than a tenfold increase" in profits [2005] — not through financial engineering or cost-cutting on ingredients, but through disciplined pricing, operational excellence, and an absolute refusal to dilute the product.

### Price Increases as Franchise Evidence

Buffett raised prices at See's virtually every year for five decades. Each successful increase was empirical evidence that the franchise remained intact. The ability to raise prices is not just a source of profit — it is a **diagnostic tool**. If customers absorb the increase without switching, you have a franchise. If they defect, you have a commodity.

## 3. GEICO: The Low-Cost Producer's Structural Moat

If See's illustrates pricing power, GEICO illustrates the other side of the coin: **cost discipline as moat**. In a commodity business like auto insurance — where the product is a government-mandated contract and policies are largely interchangeable — the low-cost producer holds the only durable advantage.

### The Founding Insight (1936)

Leo Goodwin founded GEICO in 1936 on a single insight: selling auto insurance directly to customers, bypassing the agent, could cut costs dramatically. This structural advantage — baked into the business model from day one — proved to be one of the most durable moats in American business:

> *"In a commodity business where products are largely interchangeable, the low-cost producer holds the only durable competitive advantage."* [2004]

GEICO grew from **$238,288 in first-year premiums** to over **$35 billion** [2020]. The founding insight never changed. What changed was scale.

### The Expense Ratio Advantage

Buffett tracked GEICO's cost advantage obsessively across decades:

| Year | GEICO Expense Ratio | Industry Average / Competitors | Gap |
|------|--------------------|-----------------------------|-----|
| 1986 | 23.5% | ~30-35% (industry) | 7-12 points |
| 2015 | 14.7% | ~25% (State Farm/Allstate) | ~10 points |
| 2015 | 14.7% | ~40% (agency-based insurers) | ~25 points |

By 2015, GEICO's expense ratio of 14.7% was roughly **half** that of direct competitors like State Farm and Allstate, and barely **one-third** that of traditional agency-based insurers. This gap is enormous. In a business where combined ratios hover near 100%, a 10-25 point expense advantage means GEICO can price below competitors, earn underwriting profits, *and* grow market share simultaneously.

> *"The difference between GEICO's costs and those of its competitors is a kind of moat — one that is widening every year."* [1986]

> *"GEICO's cost advantage is the factor that has enabled the company to gobble up market share year after year. Its low costs create a moat — an enduring one — that competitors are unable to cross."* [2013]

### The Virtuous Circle

Buffett described GEICO's competitive dynamics as a self-reinforcing cycle:

> Low costs → low prices → more policyholders → greater scale → even lower costs per policy → even lower prices...

This virtuous circle, first described explicitly in the 1996 letter, is the commodity-business equivalent of a franchise's pricing power. Each turn of the cycle widens the moat.

### Market Share Progression

The virtuous circle is visible in the numbers:

| Year | Market Share | Policies/Employee | Notable |
|------|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| 1995 | 2.5% | — | Berkshire acquires remaining 50% |
| 2005 | 6.1% | — | 32% productivity improvement in two years |
| 2008 | 7.7% | 299→439 | Policies per employee up 47% |
| 2009 | 8.1% | — | Third-largest U.S. auto insurer |
| 2010 | 8.8% | — | Structural cost advantages emphasized |
| 2012 | 9.7% | — | Approaching 10% |
| 2014 | 10.8% | — | Double-digit share |
| 2015 | 11.4% | — | Expense ratio data published |
| 2016 | 12.0% | — | Continued growth |

From 2.5% to 12% in two decades — GEICO roughly quintupled its market share through nothing more exotic than being cheaper, year after year after year.

### Productivity as Cost Weapon

GEICO didn't just inherit low costs from its direct model — it actively drove costs lower through productivity gains:

- **2005**: 32% productivity improvement in two years [2005]
- **2006**: 47% productivity gain [2006]
- **2008**: Policies per employee rose from 299 to 439 [2008]
- **2018**: From 1.9 million policies in 1993 to becoming the #2 insurer, with sales 1,200% higher and $15.5 billion in cumulative underwriting profits. Tony Nicely, GEICO's long-time CEO, added approximately $50 billion to Berkshire's intrinsic value [2018]

### Pricing Discipline: The Refusal to Compete on Stupidity

Buffett drew a critical distinction: being the low-cost producer does NOT mean being the lowest-price seller at all times. GEICO maintained pricing discipline even when competitors did not:

> *"We must never write inadequately-priced policies in order to stay in the game. That policy is corporate suicide."* [2024]

At NICO (National Indemnity), Berkshire's other major insurer, this discipline was even more dramatic: NICO **let premiums fall 85% over 14 years** rather than match foolish pricing by competitors [2004]. The willingness to shrink rather than write bad business is the ultimate expression of cost discipline.

### Todd Combs and GEICO's Renewal (2024)

By 2024, Todd Combs had reshaped GEICO's operations to produce what Buffett called a "spectacular" improvement — proof that even a structurally advantaged business requires continuous management attention:

> *"Todd Combs reshaped GEICO — 'spectacular' improvement."* [2024]

## 4. Nebraska Furniture Mart: Mrs. B's Cost Formula

Nebraska Furniture Mart (NFM) provides Buffett's most vivid illustration of how a cost advantage translates into competitive dominance in retail — a notoriously difficult business.

### The Numbers

Buffett published NFM's cost advantage with unusual specificity:

| Year | NFM Operating Expenses (% of revenue) | Competitor Benchmark | Gap |
|------|---------------------------------------|---------------------|-----|
| 1984 | 16.5% | 35.6% (Levitz Furniture) | 19.1 points |
| 1990 | 15% | 40% (competitors generally) | 25 points |

In 1984, NFM's operating expenses were **16.5%** of revenue versus **35.6%** at Levitz, then the nation's largest furniture retailer — less than half. By 1990, the gap had widened further: NFM at **15%** versus competitors averaging **40%**.

This meant NFM could sell furniture at prices competitors could not match and still earn healthy margins. The arithmetic is devastating: a competitor with 40% operating costs cannot profitably match the prices of a retailer with 15% costs. They can try, but they will bleed cash while NFM earns money.

### Mrs. B's Formula

Rose Blumkin — "Mrs. B" — arrived in America from Russia, unable to speak English, with $50 in cash. She built NFM into the largest furniture store in America by following a formula of radical simplicity:

> *"Sell cheap and tell the truth."* [1984]

Mrs. B worked until age 103 [2020]. Her formula was not a marketing slogan — it was a cost philosophy. "Sell cheap" meant keeping prices so low that customers had no reason to shop anywhere else. "Tell the truth" meant never deceiving customers about quality or value. The combination created a flywheel: low prices attracted volume, volume drove purchasing power, purchasing power drove costs lower, lower costs enabled even lower prices.

The formula produced results that seemed impossible. A single store in Omaha, Nebraska generated more revenue than many multi-location chains. Competitors opened and closed around NFM like waves breaking on a rock.

## 5. Buffalo News: Quality and Cost Coexisting

The Buffalo News demonstrates that cost discipline and product quality are not opposed — they can reinforce each other.

### The 50% News Hole

Under Buffett's ownership, the Buffalo News maintained a **50% news hole** — meaning half the newspaper's pages were devoted to editorial content rather than advertising. This was among the highest ratios in American journalism. Most newspapers ran 35-40% news holes to maximize advertising revenue per page.

Yet the Buffalo News achieved this while maintaining **the lowest production costs** among comparable newspapers. Quality content attracted readers, readers attracted advertisers, advertising revenue funded the newsroom, and the cycle continued.

### The Economics Crumble

By 2006, however, even this virtuous combination could not overcome secular decline:

> *"Buffalo News profits declining despite excellent management — industry economics crumbling."* [2006]

Buffett used the Buffalo News to illustrate a painful truth: no amount of cost discipline or product quality can save a business whose fundamental economics are being destroyed by technological disruption. The newspaper's monopoly economics — which Buffett had celebrated for decades — evaporated as the internet disaggregated classified advertising, display advertising, and news distribution.

This is a crucial caveat to the pricing-power thesis: **a franchise can be destroyed by a change in the environment, not just by competitive attack.** The Buffalo News had pricing power, cost discipline, and product quality. It still declined because the medium itself became obsolete.

## 6. The Tax Test: Franchise vs. Commodity Revealed

In his 1986 letter, Buffett offered one of his most elegant diagnostic tools for distinguishing franchises from commodities — the tax test:

> *"When a franchise business gets a tax cut, the franchise holder captures the benefit. When a commodity business gets a tax cut, the benefit flows through to customers in the form of lower prices."* [1986]

The logic is precise. A franchise has pricing power — it sets prices based on what customers will pay, not based on costs. So when costs fall (including taxes), the franchise pockets the savings. A commodity business, by contrast, prices at cost-plus or at market. When costs fall, competition forces prices down, and the benefit passes to customers.

This test is remarkably practical. After any tax reform, observe which companies' margins expand and which companies' prices fall. The margin expanders are franchises. The price cutters are commodities. It works in reverse too: when costs rise, franchises pass them to customers (preserving margins), while commodity businesses absorb them (compressing margins).

### Insurance as Special Case

Insurance offers an interesting wrinkle on this framework. Buffett noted that insurance — technically a commodity — can behave like a franchise under certain conditions:

> *"Insurance can thrive as costs rise — IF you can reprice."* [2015]

Because insurance policies are repriced annually, a disciplined insurer can adjust premiums to reflect changing costs. This repricing ability gives insurance a quasi-franchise characteristic that other commodities lack. A steel producer cannot reprice yesterday's steel. An insurer reprices next year's policy.

BHE (Berkshire Hathaway Energy) in Iowa demonstrated the opposite approach: **16 years with no rate increase** while the industry raised rates 44% [2015], extended to **nearly 20 years flat** while competitors charged 61% more [2019]. This wasn't franchise pricing — it was cost discipline so extreme that rate increases became unnecessary.

## 7. The EBITDA Wars: "Tooth Fairy," "Polygraph Test," and "Banned Measurement"

No topic in Buffett's letters draws sharper language than EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Across 25 years, Buffett attacked this metric with escalating ferocity, producing some of the most quotable lines in his entire correspondence.

### The Core Objection

Buffett's argument is simple: depreciation represents real economic wear on real assets that must eventually be replaced with real cash. Excluding it from earnings is not "conservative" or "analytical" — it is dishonest.

> *"Though dentists correctly claim that if you ignore your teeth they'll go away, the same is not true for depreciation charges."* [1986]

> *"At 95% of American businesses, capital expenditures that over time roughly approximate depreciation are a necessity and are every bit as real an expense as labor or utility costs."* [1989]

### The Escalation

Buffett's language grew progressively sharper over the decades:

**2000 — The Tooth Fairy:**
> *"References to EBITDA make us shudder — does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?"* [2000]

**2001 — Dangerously Flawed:**
> *"Bad terminology is the enemy of good thinking. When companies use terms such as 'EBITDA' and 'pro forma,' they want you to unthinkingly accept concepts that are dangerously flawed."* [2001]

> *"Those who believe that EBITDA is in any way equivalent to true earnings are welcome to pick up the tab."* [2001]

**2002 — Pernicious Practice:**
> *"Trumpeting EBITDA is a particularly pernicious practice. Doing so implies that depreciation is not truly an expense, given that it is a 'non-cash' charge. That's nonsense."* [2002]

**2013 — Button Your Wallet:**
> *"When Wall Streeters tout EBITDA as a valuation guide, button your wallet."* [2013]

**2014 — The Polygraph Test:**
> *"When CEOs tout EBITDA as a valuation guide, wire them up for a polygraph test."* [2014]

**2015 — Pinocchio:**
> *"When CEOs or investment bankers tout pre-depreciation figures such as EBITDA as a valuation guide, watch their noses lengthen."* [2015]

**2021 — Bloviation:**
> *"Bull markets breed bloviated bull..."* [2021, on adjusted earnings and similar metrics]

**2023 — Banned:**
> *"EBITDA is a banned measurement at Berkshire."* [2023]

> Maintenance CapEx at BNSF exceeded depreciation by **$22 billion** over 14 years [2023] — proof that depreciation, far from being a "non-cash" abstraction, actually *understates* the true capital needs of asset-heavy businesses.

**2024 — Flawed Favorite:**
> *"EBITDA, a flawed favorite of Wall Street, is not for us."* [2024]

### The BNSF Proof

The 2023 letter provided the most devastating empirical refutation of EBITDA. At BNSF Railroad, actual maintenance capital expenditures exceeded GAAP depreciation charges by **$22 billion over 14 years**. This means that even *including* depreciation in earnings would have overstated true economic profit. EBITDA, which excludes depreciation entirely, would have overstated it by an additional tens of billions.

This single data point demolishes the EBITDA thesis. Depreciation is not merely "real" — it is often **insufficient** to capture true capital consumption.

### Owner Earnings: Buffett's Alternative

Rather than EBITDA, Buffett has long advocated "owner earnings" as the correct measure of business profitability:

**Owner Earnings = Net Income + Depreciation/Amortization − Required Capital Expenditures**

The key difference from EBITDA: owner earnings *subtract* the capital expenditures needed to maintain competitive position. This captures the cash actually available to the owner — the cash that could be withdrawn without degrading the business.

## 8. The Capital-Light Dream

The ultimate business, in Buffett's framework, combines pricing power with minimal capital requirements. Such a business generates enormous free cash flow because it earns high margins and needs almost nothing plowed back to maintain operations.

### See's as Archetype

See's Candies remains the purest expression of this ideal:

> *"We purchased See's for $25 million when its sales were $30 million and pre-tax earnings were less than $5 million. The capital then required to conduct the business was $8 million... We have since then spent only an additional $32 million for capital improvements, bringing total investment to approximately $40 million. Pre-tax earnings have totaled $1.35 billion."* [2007]

The math: $40 million of cumulative investment produced $1.35 billion (by 2007) to $1.9 billion (by 2014) of cumulative pre-tax earnings. A dollar invested in See's generated roughly **$35-48 of pre-tax earnings** over four decades. No financial engineering. No leverage. No acquisitions. Just chocolate, pricing power, and capital efficiency.

By 2011, Buffett noted that See's "carrying value" was effectively **below zero** — the business had returned so much more cash than had ever been invested that, in an economic sense, Berkshire was playing with house money many times over.

### The Contrast: Capital-Intensive Nightmares

Buffett sharpened the capital-light ideal by contrasting it with capital-intensive businesses:

> *"The worst sort of business is one that grows rapidly, requires significant capital to engender the growth, and then earns little or no money. Think airlines."* [2007]

Airlines require billions in aircraft, maintenance facilities, and working capital. They grow by adding routes and planes — each requiring more capital. And after all that investment, they earn returns that barely cover their cost of capital, if that.

> *"The best results occur at companies that require minimal assets to conduct high-margin businesses."* [2020]

### BNSF: Structural Cost Advantage Through Capital

Not all capital-intensive businesses are bad. BNSF Railroad demonstrates that massive capital investment can create structural advantages — if the economics are right:

> *"BNSF moves freight with 3x the fuel efficiency of trucking."* [2010]

The railroad's capital intensity creates a barrier to entry (no one is building a new transcontinental railroad) and a cost advantage (fuel efficiency vs. trucking). But Buffett was clear-eyed about the trade-off: BNSF requires continuous heavy investment. It is a "good" business, not a "great" one in his three-category framework.

### CTB International: The Quiet Compounder

CTB International, purchased for $140 million, generated $89 million in pre-tax earnings — illustrating the "blocking and tackling" approach to capital-light value creation [2008]. No dramatic pricing power, no famous brand. Just operational excellence in an unglamorous niche.

## 9. Product Quality as the Foundation

Pricing power is not self-sustaining. It rests on a foundation of product quality that customers recognize and value. Buffett returned to this theme repeatedly, treating quality as a non-negotiable constraint.

### See's: "The Finest Ingredients"

See's maintained its franchise by using the finest available ingredients — real butter, premium chocolate, no artificial flavors or preservatives. This was not a marketing claim but an operational commitment that constrained costs upward. The willingness to accept higher input costs in exchange for customer loyalty is the franchise trade-off in action.

> *"Don't mess with my candy."* [2020, the customer verdict]

### The Moat Must Be Widened Daily

> *"Every day, in countless ways, the competitive position of each of our businesses grows either weaker or stronger."* [2005]

> *"When short-term and long-term conflict, widening the moat must take precedence."* [2005]

Quality investment is moat-widening. Cost-cutting that degrades quality is moat-narrowing. Buffett was explicit: if you must choose between this quarter's earnings and the moat's long-term width, choose the moat. Every time.

### The ABCs of Decay (2014)

Buffett warned that even great franchises can erode through internal rot:

> *"The ABCs of business decay: Arrogance, Bureaucracy, Complacency."* [2014]

Arrogance leads to price increases beyond what quality justifies. Bureaucracy adds costs without adding value. Complacency allows quality to drift downward. Together, they can destroy even the strongest franchise — not overnight, but slowly, almost imperceptibly, until the 263 letters from Albuquerque stop arriving.

## Summary: The Unified Framework

Buffett's 49 years of letters reveal a single, coherent framework for evaluating business quality:

1. **Identify the business type.** Is it a franchise (pricing power, no close substitute, unregulated) or a commodity (interchangeable product, price competition)?

2. **If franchise:** Can it raise prices annually without losing volume? Does it pass the tax test — capturing cost reductions rather than passing them through? Does it pass the Albuquerque test — generating customer devotion, not just satisfaction?

3. **If commodity:** Is it the low-cost producer? Is its cost advantage structural (like GEICO's direct model) or temporary (like a lucky contract)? Does it have the discipline to shrink rather than write bad business?

4. **Measure capital intensity.** How much capital must be reinvested to maintain competitive position? The dream is See's: $40 million invested, $1.9 billion returned. The nightmare is airlines: billions invested, returns near zero.

5. **Verify quality commitment.** Is the moat being widened daily? Is management resisting the ABCs of decay? Is short-term profit being sacrificed for long-term position when necessary?

6. **Ignore EBITDA.** Focus on owner earnings — the cash actually available after maintaining the business. Depreciation is real. The tooth fairy is not.

7. **Remember the formula.** Buy commodities, sell brands. Require minimal capital. Earn high returns. Let time compound the result.

> *"It is not necessary to do extraordinary things to get extraordinary results."* [1987]

The extraordinary result comes from finding a business with pricing power or structural cost advantage, buying it at a fair price, letting great managers run it, and never — never — messing with the formula that works.
