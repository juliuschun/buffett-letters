# Chapter 12: Mistakes, Humility, and Learning from Error

> "I'm the fellow, remember, who thought he understood the future economics of trading stamps, textiles, shoes and second-tier department stores." -- [2000]

---

## The Core Principle

Warren Buffett has built something almost unheard of in corporate America: a culture where the chairman publicly catalogs, quantifies, and even jokes about his own mistakes. From the earliest shareholder letters, Buffett treats error not as something to bury in a footnote but as something to drag into daylight, dissect, and learn from. The practice is not mere self-flagellation. It rests on a specific insight about asymmetry: a few great decisions can carry an entire enterprise forever, but only if you survive your mistakes long enough for the winners to compound. The cardinal sin is not making errors -- it is refusing to acknowledge them, or worse, delaying their correction.

In the 2024 letter, Buffett noted he had used the words "mistake" or "error" sixteen times across his 2019-23 letters, while many huge companies had never used either word in theirs. Board meetings and analyst calls where "mistake" or "wrong" are forbidden words create a dangerous illusion of perfection. As he put it: "If you start fooling your shareholders, you will soon believe your own baloney and be fooling yourself as well" [2024].

## The Catalog of Confessions

### Waumbec Mills -- The Bargain That Wasn't [1979]

Buffett's earliest significant public admission of error involves a textile company. He bought Waumbec Mills at a price below working capital -- getting the machinery and real estate "for less than nothing." It looked like an extraordinary statistical bargain. It was a mistake.

> "Both our operating and investment experience cause us to conclude that 'turnarounds' seldom turn, and that the same energies and talent are much better employed in a good business purchased at a fair price than in a poor business purchased at a bargain price." -- [1979]

The purchase produced endless new problems. The economics of the textile industry were so poor that no price -- not even a negative one -- could make the investment work. Waumbec became Exhibit A in Buffett's lifelong argument that the economics of the industry matter far more than the price paid. A structurally disadvantaged business will produce poor returns regardless of the purchase price or management quality.

### The "First 25 Years of Mistakes" Review [1989]

The 1989 letter is the magna carta of Buffett's mistake-acknowledgment philosophy. Marking a quarter-century of running Berkshire, he reviewed his errors systematically, starting with the purchase of Berkshire Hathaway itself -- a cheap textile business that turned out to be cheap for good reason.

He chronicled the Hochschild Kohn department store acquisition (bought at a bargain, sold at a bargain, producing no returns in between) and his habit of buying "cigar butts" -- businesses with one puff left:

> "A cigar butt found on the street that has only one puff left in it may not offer much of a smoke, but the 'bargain purchase' will make that puff all profit." -- [1989]

The fatal lesson: time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre. In a difficult business, no sooner is one problem solved than another surfaces. This led directly to the most important lesson of those 25 years:

> "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." -- [1989]

Charlie Munger grasped this early. Buffett admitted he was "a slow learner." His self-deprecating summary of what those 25 years taught him about solving difficult business problems:

> "What we have learned is to avoid them. To the extent we have been successful, it is because we concentrated on identifying one-foot hurdles that we could step over rather than because we acquired any ability to clear seven-footers." -- [1989]

And the simplest version:

> "We've done better by avoiding dragons than by slaying them." -- [1989]

### The Cockroach Principle [1989, 2002, 2014]

Out of those 25 years of mistakes came one of Buffett's most quoted principles. In a difficult business, problems multiply:

> "There is seldom just one cockroach in the kitchen." -- [2002]

This principle first crystallized in the 1989 review and was reinforced dramatically in 2014 with the Tesco investment. It applies equally to business operations and to accounting: when management takes the low road in visible aspects of reporting, they are almost certainly following a similar path behind the scenes. Visible corner-cutting is a reliable signal of hidden problems.

The cockroach theory served as a warning system. When you see the first sign of serious trouble, expect more. The correct response is decisive action, not dawdling.

### USAir -- A Running Comedy of Errors [1989-1997]

No mistake in the Buffett letters gets more sustained, self-lacerating comic treatment than USAir. In 1989, Buffett invested $358 million in USAir preferred stock. He was "beguiled by the company's long history of profitable operations, and by the protection that ownership of a senior security seemingly offered me" -- and overlooked the crucial point that revenues would face fierce unregulated competition while costs remained bloated from the regulatory era [1996].

The saga unfolded across nearly a decade of letters:

- **1990**: "I plunged into the business at almost the exact moment that it ran into severe problems. (No one pushed me; in tennis parlance, I committed an 'unforced error.')"
- **1991**: Bankrupt airlines were destroying industry economics with "burn-the-furniture-to-provide-firewood" fare-setting.
- **1992**: "A competitively-beset business such as USAir requires far more managerial skill than does a business with fine economics. Unfortunately, though, the near-term reward for skill in the airline business is simply survival, not prosperity."
- **1994**: Buffett wrote down the investment to 25 cents on the dollar. Herb Kelleher's quip: "Bankruptcy court for airlines has become a health spa."
- **1995**: Churchill rescued him: "Eating my words has never given me indigestion."
- **1996**: Richard Branson's classic on how to become a millionaire: "There's really nothing to it. Start as a billionaire and then buy an airline." Buffett noted he had tested this proposition with USAir. He tried to sell at a loss -- and failed: "I tried once more to unload our holdings... You're lucky: I again failed in my attempt to snatch defeat from the jaws of victory."
- **1997**: "I have compiled a record that is unblemished by success." And: "I was wrong in originally purchasing the stock, and I was wrong later, in repeatedly trying to unload our holdings at 50 cents on the dollar." The turnaround? "Two changes at the company coincided with its remarkable rebound: 1) Charlie and I left the board of directors and 2) Stephen Wolf became CEO."

A friend's quip summarized the whole episode: "If you're so rich, why aren't you smart?" [1996]. Buffett noted this was the lesson that taught him the circle of competence -- "The USAir mistake happened precisely because Buffett ventured outside his -- making a superficial analysis of a business he didn't deeply understand" [1996]. If history supplied all the answers, the Forbes 400 would consist of librarians.

### Dexter Shoes -- The Triple Compounding Error [2000, 2007]

Of all Buffett's confessions, the Dexter Shoes disaster gets the most precise quantification -- because the mistake compounded in three distinct ways.

**Mistake one**: He bought a business whose competitive advantage he had assessed as durable, but which vanished within a few years as low-cost foreign manufacturers undercut domestic production.

> "What I had assessed as durable competitive advantage vanished within a few years." -- [2007]

**Mistake two**: He paid with Berkshire stock instead of cash. This turned a bad investment into a catastrophic one, because the stock he gave away kept appreciating.

**Mistake three**: Berkshire's stock went up enormously after the deal, magnifying the error geometrically.

> "In essence, I gave away 1.6% of a wonderful business -- one now valued at $220 billion -- to buy a worthless business." -- [2007]

The lesson: using stock to make acquisitions can compound errors enormously. The true cost is not the dollar amount at the time but the future value of the shares given away. He had compounded a bad business judgment with a catastrophic currency decision, and time had turned a mistake into a disaster.

In the 2000 letter, Buffett delivered the broader confession:

> "I'm the fellow, remember, who thought he understood the future economics of trading stamps, textiles, shoes and second-tier department stores." -- [2000]

### Tesco and "Thumb-Sucking" [2014]

Berkshire's investment in Tesco, the British supermarket chain, demonstrated the cockroach principle in real time. Problems worsened month by month -- market share fell, margins contracted, and accounting problems surfaced.

> "You see a cockroach in your kitchen; as the days go by, you meet his relatives." -- [2014]

Buffett lost $444 million on Tesco, but the real lesson was about delay. He had soured on management but was too slow to sell:

> "Charlie calls this sort of behavior 'thumb-sucking.'" -- [2014]

This became a central teaching of the 2024 letter: "The cardinal sin is delaying the correction of mistakes or what Charlie Munger called 'thumb-sucking.' Problems, he would tell me, cannot be wished away. They require action, however uncomfortable that may be" [2024].

### Precision Castparts -- The $11 Billion Write-Down [2020]

The Precision Castparts mistake was Buffett's largest in dollar terms. He overpaid for the company in 2016, resulting in an $11 billion write-down four years later. What makes the confession remarkable is its absolute refusal to externalize blame:

> "No one misled me in any way -- I was simply too optimistic about PCC's normalized profit potential." -- [2020]

And with characteristic understatement:

> "PCC is far from my first error of that sort. But it's a big one." -- [2020]

The lesson: even when buying "a fine company -- the best in its business" with excellent management, paying too high a price can turn a good business into a mediocre investment. Valuation discipline matters as much as business quality. A wonderful company at the wrong price is not a wonderful investment.

## Errors of Omission -- The Invisible Mistakes

Buffett has argued repeatedly that errors of omission -- the great investments you pass on -- can be just as costly as errors of commission, and are far more common:

> "Typically, our most egregious mistakes fall in the omission, rather than the commission, category." -- [1991]

> "Some of my worst mistakes were not publicly visible. These were stock and business purchases whose virtues I understood and yet didn't make. ... the cost of this thumb-sucking has been huge." -- [1989]

**The Dallas TV Station**: Tom Murphy offered Buffett a Dallas-Fort Worth NBC TV station for $35 million. Buffett knew TV stations were capital-light, cash-generating businesses, and the offer came from a trusted friend and expert. He said no anyway. The station went on to earn over $1 billion in cumulative profits. Buffett called it "a brain vacation" [2007].

**Nearly Missing See's Candies**: Buffett almost passed on See's Candies over a $5 million price disagreement. See's went on to become one of the most important investments in Berkshire's history -- "Adam and Eve kick-started an activity that led to six billion humans" -- generating streams of cash that funded dozens of subsequent acquisitions [2007].

**Fannie Mae**: In 1988, Buffett was building a position in Fannie Mae when the price rose during his buying program. He stopped buying, then sold his partial position. The cost: approximately $1.4 billion in foregone gains [1991].

## The Practice of Post-Mortems

Buffett has been a persistent critic of corporate America's refusal to examine its own failures:

> "Agonizing over errors is a mistake. But acknowledging and analyzing them can be useful, though that practice is rare in corporate boardrooms." -- [2000]

> "Post-mortems prove useful for hospitals and football teams; why not for businesses and investors?" -- [1991]

In most companies, triumphs are trumpeted while dumb decisions get no follow-up. Failed acquisitions are buried in "nonrecurring" restructuring charges:

> "The financial consequences of these boners are regularly dumped into massive restructuring charges or write-offs that are casually waved off as 'nonrecurring.' ... When it comes to corporate blunders, CEOs invoke the concept of the Virgin Birth." -- [2000]

Buffett held up The Washington Post Company as a rare example of honest practice: they systematically reviewed acquisitions three years after completion, comparing actual results to original projections. He argued this should be standard practice everywhere: "Post mortems of acquisitions, in which reality is honestly compared to the original projections, are rare in American boardrooms. They should instead be standard practice" [2014].

## The Self-Deprecating Tradition

A distinctive feature of the Buffett letters is the sheer density of self-deprecating confession. No major CEO in American corporate history has produced anything comparable. A selection:

> "Your Chairman, always a quick study, required only 20 years to recognize how important it was to buy good businesses. In the interim, I searched for 'bargains' -- and had the misfortune to find some. My punishment was an education in the economics of short-line farm implement manufacturers, third-place department stores, and New England textile manufacturers." -- [1987]

> "It is necessary to dig deep into our history to find illustrations of such mistakes -- sometimes as deep as two or three months back." -- [1981]

> "Several minor adjustments to that opinion -- now aggregating approximately 180 degrees -- have since been required." -- [1981]

On the GEICO credit card fiasco, where he overrode his own managers' warnings:

> "GEICO's managers, it should be emphasized, were never enthusiastic about my idea. They warned me that instead of getting the cream of GEICO's customers we would get the -- -- -- -- -- well, let's call it the non-cream. I subtly indicated that I was older and wiser. I was just older." -- [2009]

On the Energy Future Holdings bonds:

> "In tennis parlance, this was a major unforced error by your chairman." -- [2011]

And the broader confession that binds them all:

> "I was dead wrong." -- [2011]

## The Asymmetry: Mistakes Fade, Winners Blossom

The 2024 letter introduces the ultimate frame for understanding mistakes in the context of a long business life:

> "Mistakes fade away; winners can forever blossom." -- [2024]

A single great decision -- acquiring GEICO, hiring Ajit Jain, partnering with Charlie Munger -- can generate returns that dwarf all accumulated mistakes. The upside of getting one thing spectacularly right is effectively unlimited, while the downside of any individual mistake is finite. This asymmetry means that leaders should not become so risk-averse from past errors that they miss transformative opportunities.

But the asymmetry only works if you survive the errors. And surviving requires three things Buffett practiced relentlessly:

1. **Admit mistakes quickly and publicly.** Name the error, explain what went wrong, quantify the damage. Sixteen times in five years.
2. **Act on problems immediately.** Never thumb-suck. The cost of delay almost always exceeds the discomfort of immediate action.
3. **Conduct honest post-mortems.** Compare reality to projections. Study what went wrong, not just what went right. As Charlie Munger put it: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."

The entire tradition rests on a conviction Buffett stated in the 1985 letter, channeling Munger: studying errors is more valuable than studying successes. Knowing what destroys value helps you avoid catastrophe, and avoiding catastrophe matters more than finding brilliance. The most important business lessons come from understanding what went wrong, not what went right.

> "An investor needs to do very few things right as long as he or she avoids big mistakes." -- [1992]
