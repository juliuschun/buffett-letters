# Chapter 8: The Insurance Model and the Power of Float

## The Engine Inside the Engine

If Berkshire Hathaway were a body, insurance would be its circulatory system. Every major expansion, every landmark acquisition, every patient accumulation of wealth traces back to a simple business idea that Buffett recognized in 1967 and spent the next six decades refining: collect premiums today, pay claims tomorrow, and invest the difference. That difference -- the "float" -- is the single most important concept in understanding how Berkshire became what it is.

This chapter traces the float model from a $17.3 million curiosity in 1967 to a $171 billion fortress in 2024, examining the disciplines that made it work, the people who built it, the disasters that tested it, and the metaphors Buffett invented to explain it.

---

## What Float Is -- and Why It Matters

Insurance float is money held but not owned. Policyholders pay premiums upfront; claims arrive later -- sometimes months later, sometimes thirty years later. In the interim, the insurer holds and invests that cash. When underwriting breaks even or better, the float is free capital. When underwriting produces a profit, the insurer is actually being paid to hold other people's money.

> "If our premiums exceed the total of our expenses and eventual losses, we register an underwriting profit that adds to the investment income our float produces. When such a profit is earned, we enjoy the use of free money -- and, better yet, get paid for holding it." [2007]

The concept is simple. The execution is not.

## The Float Growth Trajectory: From $17.3 Million to $171 Billion

Buffett began tracking float growth in his very first shareholder letter [1977], where insurance was already Berkshire's primary engine. The numbers tell a story of patient, relentless compounding:

| Year | Float | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1967 | $17.3 million | National Indemnity acquisition [1996] |
| 1970 | $39 million | [2014] |
| 1977 | Insurance already the "primary engine" | [1977] |
| 1996 | $6.7 billion (22.3% annual compounding since 1967) | [1996] |
| 1997 | Growing at 21.7% annually since 1967 | [1997] |
| 2003 | $44.2 billion | [2003] |
| 2006 | $50.9 billion | [2006] |
| 2007 | $58.7 billion | [2007] |
| 2014 | $84 billion (from $39M in 1970) | [2014] |
| 2015 | $87.7 billion | [2015] |
| 2017 | $114.5 billion | [2017] |
| 2020 | $138 billion | [2020] |
| 2024 | $171 billion | [2024] |

The growth rate is extraordinary. From 1967 to 1996, float compounded at 22.3% annually [1996]. From 1967 to 1997, at 21.7% annually [1997]. But the truly remarkable fact is not the size of the float but its cost. In 21 of 26 years through 1992, Berkshire's cost of float was below the U.S. government bond rate [1992]. In 18 of 37 years through 2003, Berkshire was actually paid to hold the float -- the cost was negative [2003]. Over two decades ending in 2024, Berkshire generated $32 billion in cumulative after-tax underwriting profits while the float grew from $46 billion to $171 billion [2024].

Float is, in Buffett's framework, an invisible asset. It does not appear as equity on the balance sheet, yet it functions exactly like equity -- better, in fact, because its cost is often zero or negative.

## The Four Disciplines of Sound Insurance

The 2014 letter formalized what Buffett had been teaching for decades. Tad Montross at General Re articulated the four commandments of insurance underwriting [2014]:

1. **Understand all exposures that might cause losses.** Know what you are insuring.
2. **Conservatively assess the probability and cost of losses.** Estimate honestly, not hopefully.
3. **Set prices that deliver a profit after losses and expenses.** The math must work.
4. **Be willing to walk away if the right price cannot be obtained.** This is the hardest one.

> "'The other guy is doing it, so we must as well' spells trouble in any business, but in none more so than insurance." [2014]

Many insurers pass the first three tests. Most flunk the fourth. The willingness to let competitors take business away at unprofitable prices requires what Buffett called "unusual managerial discipline" as far back as 1977 -- a discipline that "runs counter to normal institutional behavior" [1977]. The 2015 letter elevated this to religious language:

> "Disciplined risk evaluation is the daily focus of all of our insurance managers, who know that while float is valuable, its benefits can be drowned by poor underwriting results. All insurers give that message lip service. At Berkshire it is a religion, Old Testament style." [2015]

The 2001 letter presented an earlier three-principle version: (1) accept only risks you can properly evaluate, (2) limit business so no single event threatens solvency, and (3) avoid moral risk -- "No matter what the rate, trying to write good contracts with bad people doesn't work" [2001].

## "Corporate Suicide" -- The Pricing Discipline

The most emphatic statement on pricing discipline came in the 2024 letter:

> "We must never write inadequately-priced policies in order to stay in the game. That policy is corporate suicide." [2024]

This was not a new idea. It was the culmination of nearly fifty years of warnings:

- **1977**: "Unusual managerial discipline will be required, as it runs counter to normal institutional behavior to let the other fellow take away business -- even at foolish prices." [1977]
- **1979**: Phil Liesche at National Indemnity produced an $8.4 million underwriting profit on $82 million of earned premiums. His premiums were down from the prior year because he refused to write unprofitable business [1979].
- **1986**: Berkshire went from the slowest-growing insurer to the fastest-growing among the top 100 -- not because Berkshire came and went, but because competitors did [1986].
- **1995**: Berkshire's California workers' compensation business lost many renewals when it refused to accept inadequate rates. Despite volume dropping materially, it produced excellent underwriting profit [1995].
- **2006**: After calm hurricane seasons flooded capital into super-cat reinsurance, Berkshire sharply reduced wind exposures rather than chase volume at inadequate prices [2006].
- **2007**: Buffett warned that insurance profit margins would fall as prices dropped, but Berkshire's managers maintained underwriting discipline even when it meant slower growth [2007].
- **2024**: Berkshire stated plainly that it "needs to shrink when prices are inadequate" [2024].

The key insight is that pricing discipline is not about any single year. It is about permanent readiness:

> "Be steadfast -- stay ready when others flee. The winning strategy in cyclical industries is permanent readiness combined with price discipline. Be available when competitors retreat, patient when they return with irrational prices." [1986, 1989]

And Berkshire protected this discipline structurally by refusing to lay off insurance employees during cyclical slowdowns. Employees who fear layoffs "will understandably write business at any price to maintain volume -- through thick and thin (mostly thin)" [1986]. A no-layoff practice during downturns is in the company's self-interest because it allows employees to maintain pricing discipline without fearing for their jobs.

## "Self-Graded Exams" -- The Treachery of Loss Reserves

One of Buffett's most vivid warnings concerns the inherent dishonesty risk in insurance accounting. Loss reserves -- the estimate of what an insurer will eventually pay on policies already written -- are fundamentally subjective:

> "The loss reserves of an insurer are similar to a self-graded exam. Ignorance, wishful thinking or, occasionally, downright fraud can deliver inaccurate figures about an insurer's financial condition for a very long time." [2017]

This metaphor first appeared in the 2001 letter, where Buffett noted that insurers provide their own reserve estimates to auditors and "generally don't get an argument." Companies in financial distress are "unlikely to be tough graders on themselves" [2001]:

> "Insurance accounting is a self-graded exam." [2001]

The consequences are severe because surprises in insurance are almost always unpleasant:

> "As for Berkshire, Charlie and I attempt to be conservative in presenting its underwriting results to you, because we have found that virtually all surprises in insurance are unpleasant ones." [1997]

The 1986 letter captured the industry's euphemism for bad reserving with devastating precision:

> "When insurance executives belatedly establish proper reserves, they often speak of 'reserve strengthening,' a term that has a rather noble ring to it... the term is a euphemism for what should more properly be called 'correction of previous untruths.'" [1986]

And Buffett confessed to his own failures in this area:

> "If the physiological rules that applied to Pinocchio were to apply to me, my nose would now draw crowds." [1986]

-- admitting that his loss reserve estimates had been wrong three years in a row.

The 2024 letter emphasized that the float model punishes optimists:

> "This model is dangerous for optimists and crooks because losses can be hidden for years or decades. Discipline in pricing and reserving is essential." [2024]

And the 1978 letter warned about the industry-wide consequences:

> "It is very easy to fool yourself regarding underwriting results in reinsurance (particularly in casualty lines involving long delays in settlement), and we believe this situation prevails with many of our competitors." [1978]

General Re's near-destruction illustrated this perfectly. The company "severely miscalculated the cost of the product it was selling" because years of underreserving masked the true cost, leading to chronic underpricing [2001].

## Ajit Jain: The Extraordinary Underwriter

If the float model is the engine, Ajit Jain is the engineer who built much of it from scratch. Jain joined Berkshire in 1986 and created the reinsurance operation that became one of the company's most valuable assets. The 1999 letter contained Buffett's most complete tribute:

> "In Ajit, we have an underwriter equipped with the intelligence to properly rate most risks; the realism to forget about those he can't evaluate; the courage to write huge policies when the premium is appropriate; and the discipline to reject even the smallest risk when the premium is inadequate. It is rare to find a person possessing any one of these talents. For one person to have them all is remarkable." [1999]

Note the four qualities: intelligence, realism, courage, and discipline. These mirror the four disciplines of sound insurance but are expressed as personal traits rather than institutional rules. Jain embodied the disciplines before they were codified.

The 1999 letter also quantified his contribution:

> "It is simply impossible to overstate Ajit's value to Berkshire." [1999]

He built the reinsurance operation "from scratch into a $6.3 billion float business" [1999].

The 2001 letter praised his extraordinary discipline over 15+ years:

> "His discipline didn't eliminate losses but prevented foolish ones." [2001]

In more than 15 years, Jain had "never broken any of the three underwriting rules" [2001]. This consistency -- avoiding catastrophic errors over long periods -- is what produces extraordinary long-term results.

The 2024 letter placed Jain alongside GEICO and Charlie Munger as examples of how "mistakes fade away; winners can forever blossom" -- a single great decision (hiring Jain) that generates returns dwarfing all accumulated mistakes [2024].

By 2017, Jain was elevated to Vice Chairman alongside Greg Abel, with Buffett noting that "the character of each man matches his talents. And that says it all" [2017].

Mike Goldberg, the executive who recruited Jain, captured the ideal insurance mindset:

> "We want our underwriters to daily come to work nervous, but not paralyzed." [2024]

## National Indemnity: Fifteen Minutes, One and a Half Pages, $111 Billion

The origin story of Berkshire's insurance empire is itself a lesson in simplicity and decisiveness. The 2014 letter tells the tale:

> "The acquisition of National Indemnity in 1967 -- a 15-minute conversation, a 1.5-page homemade purchase agreement, no lawyers, no audit. That $8.6 million purchase now has $111 billion in net worth." [2014]

Jack Ringwalt, the founder, was "a bit quirky and likely to walk away if the deal became at all complicated" [2014]. Simplicity preserved the deal. As Ringwalt told Buffett:

> "If I don't sell the company, my executor will, and I'd rather pick the home for it." [1992]

The contrast between the $8.6 million price and the $111 billion result is perhaps the single most powerful illustration of what patient capital allocation can produce. And it happened because Buffett moved fast, kept things simple, and trusted his counterparty.

National Indemnity's culture of pricing discipline -- epitomized by Phil Liesche and later by its successors -- became the template for all of Berkshire's insurance operations. The 1978 letter called Liesche's operation "the best portion of the insurance business by a large margin" [1978], and the 1979 letter showed him cutting business volume rather than accepting inadequate rates [1979].

## Insurance Cycle Dynamics: "Not More Statesmen, but Less Corn"

Buffett rejected the popular notion of a predictable "underwriting cycle." Instead, he saw insurance as a commodity business whose profitability depends entirely on capacity psychology:

> "In a commodity business with hundreds of participants and independently-set prices, profitability will be subnormal in all circumstances except one: a shortage of usable capacity." [1989]

The 1986 letter drew the memorable analogy:

> "What's needed is not more statesmen, but less corn." [1986]

The logic is simple: when insurance profits surge, new capital floods in, capacity expands, competitive juices flow, and prices fade. Calls for "statesmanlike" pricing discipline are as futile as asking all corn growers worldwide to limit production. Only genuine fear -- not desire, not need, not statesmanship -- produces adequate pricing.

> "Pricing behavior in the insurance industry continues to be exactly what can be expected in a commodity-type business. Only under shortage conditions are high profits achieved, and such conditions don't last long." [1986]

Berkshire's strategy was to be the steadfast participant: always ready to do business at rational prices, available when competitors flee, patient when they return with irrational prices [1986]. The 1986 letter showed this in action -- Berkshire went from the slowest-growing insurer to the fastest-growing among the top 100 in a single year, not because Berkshire changed its behavior but because competitors did.

The 1989 letter made the deeper point that capacity in insurance is "ultimately a function of the mental state of managers" [1989]. When managers are fearful, they pull back, creating genuine shortages. When they are confident, they expand, destroying pricing. The industry's emotional state -- not its financial state -- drives the cycle.

## Super-Cat Reinsurance: Preparing for the Unthinkable

Buffett entered the super-catastrophe reinsurance business with eyes wide open about its nature:

> "What you must understand, however, is that a truly terrible year in the super-cat business is not a possibility -- it's a certainty. The only question is when it will come." [1996, 1997]

The business required three competitive advantages that Berkshire possessed and few others could match [1996]:

1. **Guaranteed ability to pay claims** even in worst-case scenarios.
2. **Continued availability of capacity** after a disaster, when others might withdraw.
3. **Ability to provide coverage sizes** unmatched in the industry.

After Hurricane Hugo and the California earthquake in 1989, Berkshire "instantly offered $250 million of catastrophe coverage while competitors were licking their wounds" [1989]. The willingness to write large policies that most competitors avoided -- and to accept the quarterly volatility that came with it -- was a structural advantage:

> "We are willing to _look_ foolish as long as we don't feel we have _acted_ foolishly." [1989]

The 1992 letter described Hurricane Andrew revealing that "many insurers had grossly inadequate catastrophe reinsurance" -- or as Buffett put it: "It's only when the tide goes out that you learn who's been swimming naked" [1992]. Berkshire's financial strength meant customers knew that "when the big one came, many reinsurers who found it easy to write policies would find it difficult to write checks" [1991]:

> "Some reinsurers can say what Jackie Mason does: 'I'm fixed for life -- as long as I don't buy anything.'" [1991]

The 1992 letter also warned about the unreliability of historical data for catastrophic risk. Buffett cited the surprising historical earthquakes in Charleston, South Carolina, and New Madrid, Missouri -- events no one expected in those locations. Past experience is an unreliable guide; population growth, climate change, and insured values in vulnerable areas mean historical loss data systematically underestimates future risk [1992].

Berkshire accepted the lumpy results that super-cat insurance produces:

> "Charlie and I would much rather earn a lumpy 15% over time than a smooth 12%." [1995, 1996]

And because most managers optimize for smooth quarterly results, Berkshire had a "competitive advantage that we try to maximize" [1995].

By 2017, Berkshire could absorb a $400 billion mega-catastrophe that no other private insurer could contemplate [2017]. And by 2024, Buffett was warning about the growing unpredictability of losses:

> "Someday, any day, a truly staggering insurance loss will occur -- and there is no guarantee that there will be only one per annum." [2024]

> "It would be foolish -- make that madness -- to write ten-year policies for these coverages." [2024]

Climate change, he noted, "may have been announcing its arrival" through increased convective storm damage [2024].

## "A Bad Reinsurance Contract Is Like Hell"

The 1995 letter delivered one of Buffett's most memorable warnings about the insurance business:

> "A bad reinsurance contract is like hell: easy to enter and impossible to exit." [1995]

He was reflecting on mistakes made in reinsurance in the early 1970s, with bills still arriving more than twenty years later. Long-duration commitments made on bad terms create liabilities that haunt a business for decades. Unlike bad inventory that can be marked down and cleared, bad contractual obligations cannot be escaped [1995].

This lesson was reinforced by Lloyd's of London's near-collapse. The 2017 letter warned that even Lloyd's, after three centuries of success, nearly collapsed in the 1980s due to "latent long-tail insurance problems" [2017]. What appears predictable in insurance can harbor enormous hidden risks. Conservative and careful underwriting, embedded in company culture, is the only defense.

And the 1991 letter used dark humor to illustrate insurance companies that under-reserved to disguise deteriorating results:

> A patient tells his doctor he cannot afford an operation and asks him to "touch up the x-rays." [1991]

## GEICO: The Low-Cost Moat

While National Indemnity and the reinsurance operations demonstrate float discipline, GEICO demonstrates cost advantage. Buffett recognized GEICO's structural advantage in 1951, when he was twenty years old:

> "I was blown away by the huge cost advantage the company enjoyed compared to the expenses borne by the giants of the industry. It was clear to me that GEICO would succeed because it deserved to succeed." [2015]

GEICO's direct-marketing model gave it operating costs of about 14.7% of premiums versus about 25% for State Farm and Allstate, and roughly 40% for traditional agency-based insurers [2015]. The cost advantage was structural -- embedded in the business model itself -- and competitors could see it clearly but could not abandon their own distribution model because it was too deeply ingrained [1995].

> "GEICO's low costs create a moat -- an enduring one -- that competitors are unable to cross." [2015]

> "The difference between GEICO's costs and those of its competitors is a kind of moat that protects a valuable and much-sought-after business castle." [1986]

GEICO's market share trajectory tells the story: 2.5% in 1995, 7.2% in 2007, 10.8% in 2014, 11.4% in 2015 [various years]. Annual advertising expenditures grew from $31 million to $751 million over the same period [2007]. When you have a genuine cost advantage, aggressive marketing investment is rational because every new customer is profitable.

The 2024 letter noted that Todd Combs had reshaped GEICO over five years, "bringing underwriting practices up to date" and producing a "spectacular" improvement [2024]. Even great businesses can become operationally stale and need "major repolishing" [2024].

## The Float Model Beyond Insurance

Buffett repeatedly emphasized that the float concept extends far beyond insurance:

> "Any business that can structure itself to collect revenue before delivering costs has a structural financing advantage. Subscription models, prepaid services, and deposits all create mini-floats that can be deployed productively." [2014]

The collect-now, pay-later model is one of the most powerful structural advantages in business. But it only works when the core business is disciplined enough that the float is genuinely free:

> "Float is wonderful only when it is free or better. The cost of float is underwriting losses. When the cost is below the government borrowing rate, float is a powerful competitive advantage. When the cost is high, it is an albatross." [1992, 1996, 2003]

## Every Insurance Quote and Metaphor

The letters are rich with insurance-specific language that Buffett invented or perfected:

**On the nature of insurance:**
> "Insurance companies offer standardized policies which can be copied by anyone. Their only products are promises. It is not difficult to be licensed, and rates are an open book. There are no important advantages from trademarks, patents, location, corporate longevity, raw material sources, etc., and very little consumer differentiation to produce insulation from competition." [1977]

**On the treachery of the business:**
> "While the lamb may lie down with the lion, the lamb shouldn't count on getting a whole lot of sleep." [1986]

**On competitors' false precision:**
> "The precision they project is a chimera. In fact, such models can lull decision-makers into a false sense of security and thereby increase their chances of making a really huge mistake." [1996]

**On the inevitable surprise:**
> "The super-cat business is just like the investment business in that it often takes a long time to find out whether you knew what you were doing." [1996]

**On counterparty risk:**
> "A fool and his money are soon invited everywhere." [1992]

**On buying from the wrong people:**
> "In insurance, it is essential to remember that virtually all surprises are unpleasant." [1996]

**On the cost of long-tail errors:**
> "We buried Dad in a rented suit." [2007]
-- A joke illustrating how costs keep coming long after the original event, like a rented suit on a corpse generating monthly bills.

**On competitive advantage in insurance:**
> "This is a business in which there are no trade secrets, patents, or locational advantages. What counts are brains and capital." [2017]

**On the ideal underwriter mindset:**
> "We want our underwriters to daily come to work nervous, but not paralyzed." [2024]

**On the unique scale advantage:**
> "No private insurer has the willingness to take on the amount of risk that Berkshire can provide." [2024]

## Summary: The Float Flywheel

The insurance model is not a single idea but a flywheel of reinforcing principles:

1. **Collect premiums before paying claims** -- creating investable float.
2. **Maintain underwriting discipline** -- keeping the cost of float at zero or below.
3. **Accept volatility** -- taking lumpy results that competitors avoid.
4. **Build financial strength** -- becoming the counterparty of last resort.
5. **Invest the float wisely** -- compounding the advantage over decades.
6. **Attract the best people** -- Ajit Jain, Phil Liesche, Tony Nicely, Todd Combs.
7. **Stay steadfast** -- always available at rational prices, never chasing volume.

Each element strengthens the others. Financial strength attracts better reinsurance customers. Better customers produce better underwriting results. Better results grow the float. Larger float funds larger investments. Larger investments produce greater financial strength. And so the flywheel turns, year after year, decade after decade, from $17.3 million to $171 billion.

> "If our premiums exceed the total of our expenses and eventual losses, we register an underwriting profit that adds to the investment income our float produces. When such a profit is earned, we enjoy the use of free money -- and, better yet, get paid for holding it." [2007]

That is the insurance model. That is the power of float. That is the engine inside the engine.
