# Chapter 9: Acquisition Discipline -- Buying Right

## The Art of Saying No

Warren Buffett has spent more time explaining why he did not buy something than why he did. In a corporate world where deal-making is celebrated, where CEOs are rewarded for "doing things," and where investment bankers are paid to close transactions regardless of merit, Buffett built Berkshire Hathaway's acquisition record on a foundation of refusal. The discipline to say no -- to bad businesses, to bad prices, to bad people, to the institutional momentum that pushes every deal toward "yes" -- is the thread that runs through nearly fifty years of shareholder letters.

This chapter traces the evolution of Buffett's acquisition framework from its first appearance in 1977 through its most sophisticated expressions in the 2020s, examining the deals that worked, the disasters that taught, and the metaphors that made the lessons memorable.

---

## The Four-Criteria Framework: 1977 to Forever

The framework appeared in the very first shareholder letter and never changed. In 1977, Buffett described how Berkshire evaluates businesses:

> "We want the business to be (1) one that we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) available at a very attractive price." [1977]

What evolved was not the framework but its expression. Each restating added nuance:

**1978** -- The same four criteria, now applied explicitly to stock selection for insurance company portfolios:
> "We get excited enough to commit a big percentage of insurance company net worth to equities only when we find (1) businesses we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) priced very attractively." [1978]

**1986** -- The criteria expanded to include specific operational requirements: "demonstrated consistent earning power (future projections are of little interest to us, nor are 'turn-around' situations)," good returns on equity with little or no debt, management in place, and simple businesses [1986]. Buffett added his famous speed promise:
> "We can promise complete confidentiality and a very fast answer -- customarily within five minutes -- as to whether we're interested." [1986]

**1989** -- The criteria were restated with precise minimums: large purchases ($10M+ after-tax earnings), demonstrated consistent earning power, good returns on equity with little debt, management in place, simple businesses, and an offering price [1989]. And a new negative list: no turnarounds, no auctions, no unfriendly takeovers.

**2003** -- The framework distilled to three essential requirements: "(1) favorable and enduring economic characteristics, (2) talented and honest managers, and (3) a sensible price. All three conditions must be met -- missing even one makes the deal unwise" [2003].

**2019** -- Another restatement: "(1) it earns good returns on the net tangible capital required for operations, (2) it is run by able and honest managers, and (3) it is available at a sensible price" [2019].

The framework's power lies not in its sophistication but in its permanence. Forty-seven years of consistent criteria created a filter that kept Berkshire out of disasters that consumed its competitors.

## The "Cigar Butt" Metaphor: One Puff and Nothing

The 1989 letter contained Buffett's most famous confession about his early investing mistakes:

> "I call this the 'cigar butt' approach to investing. A cigar butt found on the street that has only one puff left in it may not offer much of a smoke, but the 'bargain purchase' will make that puff all profit." [1989]

The context was a quarter-century of mistakes. Buffett had purchased Berkshire Hathaway itself -- a dying textile business -- because it was statistically cheap. He had purchased Hochschild Kohn, a Baltimore department store, because it appeared to be a bargain. Both had able, honest managers who could not overcome the poor economics of their industries.

The cigar butt metaphor captured a deeper truth about bad businesses:

> "In a difficult business, no sooner is one problem solved than another surfaces -- never is there just one cockroach in the kitchen." [1989]

And the decisive principle:

> "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre." [1989]

Charlie Munger understood this years before Buffett did. The strategic pivot that defined Berkshire's entire trajectory was:

> "I revised my strategy and tried to buy good businesses at fair prices rather than fair businesses at good prices." [1992]

> "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." [1989]

A golf pro's advice crystallized the danger of repeating the old strategy:

> "Practice doesn't make perfect; practice makes permanent." [1992]

Buffett's self-assessment of his cigar-butt years was characteristically blunt:

> "I kissed and they croaked." [1992]

---

## The Spousal Analogy: "Not in a Hurry"

The 1992 letter introduced one of Buffett's most enduring analogies for acquisition discipline:

> "In the search for acquisitions, we adopt the same attitude one might find appropriate in looking for a spouse: It pays to be active, interested and open-minded, but it does not pay to be in a hurry." [1992]

The analogy served a dual purpose. First, it warned against the deal-making fever that infects CEOs. Second, it acknowledged that great acquisitions, like great marriages, require both opportunity and timing.

The 2019 letter extended the marriage metaphor further:

> "Acquisitions are similar to marriage: They start, of course, with a joyful wedding -- but then reality tends to diverge from pre-nuptial expectations. Sometimes, wonderfully, the new union delivers bliss beyond either party's hopes. In other cases, disillusionment is swift." [2019]

And the crucial asymmetry:

> "It is usually the buyer who encounters unpleasant surprises because it is easy to get dreamy-eyed during corporate courtships." [2019]

The 1980 letter had already compared the post-acquisition period to marriage: "sellers have numerous opportunities to mislead, and attitudes can shift after the deal closes" [1980]. The courtship phase is always better than reality. The question is how much worse.

---

## Toad Kissing: The Princess Illusion

The 1981 letter introduced the fairy-tale metaphor that Buffett would return to repeatedly:

> "Investors can always buy toads at the going price for toads. If investors instead bankroll princesses who wish to pay double for the right to kiss the toad, those kisses had better pack some real dynamite. We've observed many kisses but very few miracles." [1981]

The image is devastating: CEOs as princesses, convinced their managerial kiss will transform a mediocre business into a prince. The fairy tale never works:

> "Many managerial princesses remain serenely confident about the future potency of their kisses -- even after their corporate backyards are knee-deep in unresponsive toads." [1981]

And Buffett's honest assessment of his own record:

> "We have done well with a couple of princes -- but they were princes when purchased. At least our kisses didn't turn them into toads." [1981]

The key insight is not just that transformations fail but that the psychology of deal-making prevents learning from failure. The 1992 letter quoted Santayana on this point:

> "Fanaticism consists of redoubling your effort when you've forgotten your aim." [1992]

And described the inevitable outcome:

> "In this corporate equivalent of a Head Start program, the CEO receives the education but the stockholders pay the tuition." [1992]

## The Dexter Shoes Disaster: $433 Million Becomes $3.5 Billion+

If the cigar butt metaphor is the theoretical lesson, Dexter Shoes is the empirical proof. The 2007 letter laid out the catastrophe:

> "I gave away 1.6% of a wonderful business -- one now valued at $220 billion -- to buy a worthless business." [2007]

The original error was paying $433 million for Dexter Shoes. But the compounding error -- the truly catastrophic mistake -- was paying with Berkshire stock rather than cash. As Berkshire's value grew, the true cost of those shares exploded. What seemed like a $433 million mistake became a $3.5 billion+ disaster [2007].

The 2014 letter elevated the Dexter mistake to its fullest horror, calling it "a candidate for the Guinness Book of World Records as a financial disaster." The shares given for Dexter eventually became worth $5.7 billion [2014].

The lesson was not just about Dexter but about the irreversible nature of paying with stock:

> "Using stock to make acquisitions can compound errors enormously. When you pay with shares of a wonderful business for a worthless one, the true cost is not the dollar amount at the time but the future value of the shares you gave away." [2007]

Buffett drew the broader rule:

> "The intrinsic value of shares given in an acquisition must not exceed the intrinsic value of the business received. This error is irreversible -- you cannot undo the dilution." [2014]

And he added:

> "I've yet to see an investment banker quantify this all-important math when he is presenting a stock-for-stock deal to the board of a potential acquirer." [2014]

> "You can't get rich trading a hundred-dollar bill for eight tens (even if your advisor has handed you an expensive 'fairness' opinion endorsing that swap)." [2014]

## The See's Near-Miss: Almost Walking Away Over $5 Million

If Dexter Shoes is the worst deal Buffett ever made, See's Candies may be the best. The near-miss makes the story even more instructive. The 2007 letter told it plainly:

> "I almost blew the See's purchase. The seller was asking $30 million, and I was adamant about not going above $25 million. Fortunately, he caved. Otherwise I would have balked, and that $1.35 billion would have gone to somebody else." [2007]

See's was purchased in 1972 for $25 million. By 2007, it had generated $1.35 billion in cumulative pre-tax earnings while requiring only $32 million of reinvestment [2007]. By 2014, cumulative pre-tax earnings reached $1.9 billion [2014]. The 1999 letter showed $857 million in pre-tax earnings from a $25 million purchase, with "very little additional capital" required [1999].

The near-miss over $5 million illustrates the tension at the heart of acquisition discipline. The discipline to say no -- to walk away from overpriced deals -- is essential. But taken to an extreme, it becomes rigidity that prevents you from buying extraordinary businesses. See's taught Buffett that wonderful businesses at fair prices are vastly more valuable than fair businesses at wonderful prices.

The 2014 letter explained why:

> "A wonderful business with durable competitive advantages, pricing power, and low capital requirements will compound value for decades. Selecting a marriage partner clearly requires more demanding criteria than does dating." [2014]

And Charlie Munger delivered the principle in its cleanest form:

> "Forget what you know about buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices." [2014]

## The McLane 29-Day Acquisition

Speed and simplicity in deal-making are Berkshire trademarks, and the McLane acquisition from Wal-Mart in 2003 is the clearest example:

> The McLane acquisition "was completed in 29 days with a handshake and no due diligence, because Buffett trusted Wal-Mart's word completely." [2003]

Buffett "knew everything would be exactly as Wal-Mart said it would be" [2003]. No lawyers. No audits. No protracted negotiations. When both parties have impeccable reputations for honesty, transactions happen fast and cheap.

McLane itself operated on paper-thin margins -- about 1% pre-tax on $23 billion in sales -- a business most would dismiss as unattractive [2003]. But Buffett saw consistent execution, a durable competitive position, and massive scale. A reliable 1% on billions is worth far more than a volatile 20% on millions.

## "Buyer of Choice" -- The Reputation Moat

Over the decades, Berkshire's behavior in acquisitions created a self-reinforcing deal-sourcing advantage:

> "When sellers care about these matters, Berkshire does not have a lot of competition." [2000, 2014, 2024]

What matters to sellers? Permanence, autonomy, and respect. Buffett articulated the value proposition:

- Berkshire "would almost never sell a subsidiary" [2014].
- Competitors fire employees for "synergies." Private equity loads companies with debt and flips them [2014].
- Berkshire's commitment to permanence attracts "the best businesses and the best managers, creating a virtuous circle" [2014].

The 2024 letter showed this philosophy continuing: Pete Liegl specifically wanted to sell Forest River to Berkshire because of its "steadfast behavior" [2024]. The 2017 letter showed the Pilot Flying J deal facilitated by Kevin Clayton's personal recommendation -- a Berkshire subsidiary CEO voluntarily advocating for joining the organization [2017].

The 1999 letter captured the strategy with characteristic simplicity:

> "At Berkshire, our carefully-crafted acquisition strategy is simply to wait for the phone to ring." [1999]

This is not passivity -- it is the harvest of decades of consistent behavior.

## Mrs. B and the Handshake Deal

The 1984 letter tells the story of Rose Blumkin, founder of Nebraska Furniture Mart, and one of Buffett's most beloved acquisitions:

> "We gave Mrs. B a check for $55 million and she gave us her word. That made for an even exchange." [1984]

No audit. No inventory check. No verification of receivables. No property title checks. Just a handshake with a 91-year-old woman who had never attended a day of school.

Mrs. B's formula was the essence of business simplicity:

> "Sell cheap and tell the truth." [1984]

The Blumkin formula, as Buffett distilled it, had four elements: "(1) apply extraordinary enthusiasm and energy; (2) define with extraordinary realism your area of special competence; (3) ignore even the most enticing propositions outside that area; and (4) unfailingly behave with high-grade integrity toward everyone" [1984].

The 1986 letter described Mrs. B at 93, at the store seven days a week from opening to close:

> "Competing with her represents a triumph of courage over judgment." [1986]

The lesson is that trust, built on demonstrated character, can make transactions faster, cheaper, and more pleasant for both parties. Extensive due diligence becomes less important when you are dealing with people of unquestionable integrity [1984].

Gene Abegg, founder of Illinois National Bank, exhibited the same quality. The 1980 letter described how Abegg "disclosed all negative factors upfront during acquisition negotiations, then continued to reveal previously undiscussed items of value for years afterward. He worked harder as an employee than he had as an owner" [1980]. This is the courtship-in-reverse that Buffett prizes: the seller who surprises you with good news, not bad.

## The Institutional Bias Toward Deal-Making

The 2017 letter contained Buffett's most complete articulation of why corporations systematically overpay for acquisitions:

> "The CEO job self-selects for 'can-do' types. Subordinates cheer for bigger domains and bigger pay. And investment bankers applaud because they smell fees." [2017]

The entire ecosystem is rigged to say yes:

> "Once a CEO hungers for a deal, he or she will never lack for forecasts that justify the purchase." [2017]

> "Spreadsheets never disappoint." [2017]

The 1982 letter had laid the foundation decades earlier:

> "Managerial intellect wilts in competition with managerial adrenaline." [1982]

> "If the CEO is visibly panting over a prospective acquisition, subordinates and consultants will supply the requisite projections to rationalize any price." [1982]

Buffett's reaction to the merger wave of 1982 was telling:

> "Not envy, but relief that Berkshire did not participate." [1982]

And his greatest accomplishment that year? A large acquisition that fell through:

> "Our greatest accomplishment was not what we bought but what we didn't buy." [1982]

The 2019 letter extended the warning:

> "Don't ask the barber whether you need a haircut." [2017, 2019]

CEOs never bring in articulate critics to argue against deals they want. The entire corporate deal-making ecosystem -- investment bankers, lawyers, consultants -- is financially incentivized to encourage transactions regardless of merit [2019].

Peter Drucker, quoted in the 1995 letter, captured the psychological root:

> "Dealmaking is exciting and fun, and working is grubby. Running anything is primarily an enormous amount of grubby detail work... dealmaking is romantic, sexy. That's why you have deals that make no sense." [1995]

## Speed, Simplicity, and Decisiveness

Berkshire's deal-making style is the opposite of the investment-banking-driven auction process:

- **National Indemnity (1967)**: 15-minute conversation, 1.5-page agreement, no lawyers, no audit. $8.6M purchase became $111B in net worth [2014].
- **Nebraska Furniture Mart (1983)**: $55 million check, Mrs. B's word. No audit, no inventory, no title checks [1984].
- **Scott & Fetzer (1985)**: From first letter to signed contract in 17 days [1985]. When Charlie Munger was offered the investment banking book on the company, he declined: the deal was already agreed.
- **McLane (2003)**: Completed in 29 days with a handshake, no due diligence [2003].
- **Marmon/Pritzker (2007)**: No advisors, no nit-picking, using only financial statements. "With the Pritzkers, as with Berkshire, a deal is a deal" [2007].

The pattern is clear: when you trust the counterparty and understand the business, elaborate process adds cost without adding value. As Buffett noted about Jack Ringwalt, "he was also a bit quirky and likely to walk away if the deal became at all complicated. Simplicity preserved the deal" [2014].

## Turnarounds Seldom Turn

The anti-turnaround philosophy runs through the letters like a bass line:

> "Turnarounds seldom turn." [1979, 1986]

The 1979 letter provided the empirical evidence: the Waumbec Mills acquisition looked like an extraordinary statistical bargain -- machinery and real estate for "less than nothing" after subtracting working capital value. But it was a mistake. The same energy and talent applied to a good business at a fair price would have produced far better results [1979].

The 1989 letter broadened the point:

> "Good jockeys will do well on good horses, but not on broken-down nags." [1989]

> "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact." [1989]

And the 2024 letter, sixty years later, still anchored in the same truth: Buffett called his 1965 purchase of Berkshire itself "a mistake that plagued the company for two decades" because "no amount of bargain pricing compensates for an industry headed to zero" [2024].

## Every Acquisition Quote and Metaphor

**On the search process:**
> "At Berkshire, our carefully-crafted acquisition strategy is simply to wait for the phone to ring." [1999]

> "We've found that if you advertise an interest in buying collies, a lot of people will call hoping to sell you their cocker spaniels." [1989, 2014]

> "When the phone don't ring, you'll know it's me." [2014]
-- A country song lyric expressing Berkshire's disinterest in turnarounds, new ventures, and auction-like sales.

**On overpaying:**
> "The empire would have been larger, but the citizenry would have been poorer." [1981]

> "We wish to increase Berkshire's size only when doing that also increases the wealth of its owners." [1992]

> "Increasing a company's size is not the same as increasing owner wealth." [1992]

**On projections and synergies:**
> "Things are seldom what they seem, skim milk masquerades as cream." [1995]
-- From HMS Pinafore, applied to sellers' projections.

> "In the production of rosy scenarios, Wall Street can hold its own against Washington." [1995]

> "Also, a marvelous business purchased at a fair price becomes a terrible buy when paid for with undervalued currency." [1985]
-- Quoting himself on the Waumbec Mills disaster.

> "We also made a major acquisition, Waumbec Mills, with the expectation of important synergy (a term widely used in business to explain an acquisition that otherwise makes no sense)." [1985]

**On the thrill of the chase:**
> "All of man's troubles come from his inability to sit quietly in a room alone." [1982]
-- Quoting Pascal on acquisition-hungry CEOs.

> "I've never gone to bed with an ugly woman, but I've sure woke up with a few." [2007]
-- A country song lyric on how acquisitions look at signing versus reality.

**On the information asymmetry:**
> "Can you help me? Sometimes my horse walks just fine and sometimes he limps." The vet replied: "No problem -- when he's walking fine, sell him." In the world of mergers and acquisitions, that horse would be peddled as Secretariat. [1995]

**On the importance of demonstrated results:**
> "Future projections are of little interest to us, nor are 'turn-around' situations." [1986]

> "A track record across varied conditions is worth far more than any hockey-stick forecast." [1986]

**On paying with stock:**
> "We have a firm policy about issuing shares of Berkshire, doing so only when we receive as much value as we give." [1992]

**On buying talent:**
> "Buying a retailer without good management is like buying the Eiffel Tower without an elevator." [1995]

**On bolt-on acquisitions:**
> "Enlarging the domain of managers you already know to be outstanding is a low-risk, high-return proposition." [1992, 2007]

**On extending proven winners:**
> "Just as Adam and Eve kick-started an activity that led to six billion humans, See's has given birth to multiple new streams of cash for us." [2007]

**On the lessons of failure:**
> "Post mortems of acquisitions, in which reality is honestly compared to the original projections, are rare in American boardrooms. They should instead be standard practice." [2014]

> "If plantings made confidently are repeatedly followed by disappointing harvests, something is wrong with the farmer." [1992]

## The Anti-Checklist: What Berkshire Will Not Do

As revealing as Buffett's criteria for what he will buy is his list of what he refuses to do:

- **No turnarounds**: "Turnarounds seldom turn" [1979, 1986].
- **No auctions**: Berkshire will not participate in competitive bidding [1986, 1989].
- **No unfriendly takeovers**: "None of these attracts us in the least" [1986].
- **No stock-funded deals** (unless receiving equal or greater intrinsic value): The Dexter lesson [2007, 2014].
- **No projections-based valuations**: "Demonstrated consistent earning power" is the requirement [1986].
- **No deals with bad people**: "We've never succeeded in making a good deal with a bad person" [1989].
- **No "get-to-know-each-other" proposals**: "Please include me out" [1989].

The negative list is as important as the positive criteria. It eliminates entire categories of deals that consume competitors' time and capital.

## Summary: The Acquisition Flywheel

Like the insurance model, Berkshire's acquisition discipline creates a self-reinforcing flywheel:

1. **Clear, permanent criteria** attract the right deals and repel the wrong ones.
2. **Speed and simplicity** close deals that elaborate processes would kill.
3. **Trust and reputation** bring sellers who value permanence over price.
4. **Autonomy for managers** retains the talent that makes businesses valuable.
5. **Honest post-mortems** prevent repeated errors.
6. **Patience** ensures that no deal is done out of urgency or boredom.
7. **Cash over stock** protects existing shareholders from dilution.

The result is a compounding advantage: each successful acquisition strengthens Berkshire's reputation, which attracts better future acquisitions, which produces better results, which further strengthens the reputation. The flywheel turns.

> "In the search for acquisitions, we adopt the same attitude one might find appropriate in looking for a spouse: It pays to be active, interested and open-minded, but it does not pay to be in a hurry." [1992]

That patience, sustained over half a century, is the acquisition discipline. It is not a strategy so much as a temperament -- one that has proven more valuable than any spreadsheet, any synergy model, or any investment banker's fairness opinion.
