# Chapter 10: Honest Accounting, Transparency, and Shareholder Communication

## Core Thesis

Accounting is the language of business -- indispensable, but dangerous when mistaken for truth itself. Buffett spent nearly four decades waging war on the gap between what companies report and what they actually earn, arguing that honest disclosure is both a moral obligation and a competitive advantage. The trajectory runs from gentle education (teaching shareholders what "owner earnings" really means) to full-throated denunciation (calling EBITDA users candidates for the polygraph). Across every letter, one principle never wavers: give shareholders the information you would wish to receive if positions were reversed.

---

## 1. Owner Earnings: The Only Number That Matters [1986]

The concept Buffett introduced in his 1986 letter remains the foundation of everything he believes about accounting. In a lengthy appendix comparing "Company O" and "Company N" -- both actually Scott Fetzer, before and after purchase-price accounting adjustments -- he demonstrated that GAAP earnings can wildly misrepresent the true economics of a business.

**Owner earnings** are defined as:

> Reported earnings + depreciation/amortization - required capital expenditures to maintain competitive position

This is the cash a business generates for its owners after maintaining itself as a going concern. Buffett attacked the popular "cash flow" metric (earnings + depreciation, full stop) as fatally misleading because it ignores the money a business must spend simply to stay in place:

> "Though dentists correctly claim that if you ignore your teeth they'll go away, the same is not true for [capital expenditures]." [1986]

The cash-flow metric, Buffett warned, was "frequently used by marketers of businesses and securities in attempts to justify the unjustifiable (and thereby to sell what should be the unsalable)" [1986].

He acknowledged that owner earnings require estimation -- you cannot precisely know future maintenance capex. But imprecision is not an excuse for omission. Here he deployed the Keynes quote that would become a recurring touchstone:

> "I would rather be vaguely right than precisely wrong." [1986]

The letter closed with a line that encapsulates his entire philosophy of accounting:

> "Accounting numbers, of course, are the language of business and as such are of enormous help to anyone evaluating the worth of a business and tracking its progress... Managers and owners need to remember, however, that accounting is but an aid to business thinking, never a substitute for it." [1986]

This framework -- use accounting as a starting point, then translate to economic reality -- informed every subsequent letter. By 2010, Buffett restated it as: "We would rather be approximately right than precisely wrong" [2010], applying it to the absurdity of Black-Scholes pricing on long-dated options. By 2020, he was still teaching the same lesson: "Operating earnings are what count most, even during periods when they are not the largest item in our GAAP total" [2020].

## 2. Stock Options: If Compensation Isn't an Expense, What Is It? [1992, 1998, 2007]

Few campaigns consumed Buffett as much as the fight to expense stock options. In the 1992 letter, he produced one of the most devastating logical sequences in business writing:

> "If options aren't a form of compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is it? And, if expenses shouldn't go into the calculation of earnings, where in the world should they go?" [1992]

He invoked Lincoln's famous riddle to drive the point home:

> "How many legs does a dog have if you call his tail a leg? Four, because calling a tail a leg does not make it a leg." [1992]

The argument was not abstract. Buffett offered to buy any CEO's "worthless" options for cash, exposing the absurdity of claiming options had no cost:

> "So if you're a CEO and subscribe to this 'no cash-no cost' theory of accounting, I'll make you an offer you can't refuse: Give us a call at Berkshire and we will happily sell you insurance in exchange for a bundle of long-term options on your company's stock." [1992]

He also warned that the credibility of the entire business establishment was at stake:

> "The business elite risks losing its credibility on issues of significance to society -- about which it may have much of value to say -- when it advocates the incredible on issues of significance to itself." [1992]

By 1998, Buffett put his money where his mouth was. He replaced General Re's stock option plan with a cash compensation plan tied to operating performance. The economics were identical, but GAAP accounting treated options as free while charging the cash plan as an expense. The failure to account for options, he estimated, inflated reported earnings by 5-10% at many companies [1998].

The 2007 letter revealed the staggering scale of corporate resistance: only 2 of 500 S&P companies voluntarily expensed stock options when given the choice. CEOs systematically chose accounting methods that maximized reported earnings rather than reflected economic reality [2007].

## 3. The War on EBITDA: Tooth Fairies and Polygraphs [2000, 2002, 2014, 2024]

Buffett's attacks on EBITDA escalated in ferocity across the decades. The 2000 letter opened the assault with a question that has become one of his most quoted lines:

> "References to EBITDA make us shudder -- does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" [2000]

He used FlightSafety's $272 million annual simulator spending to illustrate the point: every year the company spent amounts equal to its depreciation charge simply to maintain its competitive position, then spent additional sums to grow. Depreciation was every bit as real a cost as payroll or raw materials [2000].

By 2002, the attack broadened. Buffett called the practice of trumpeting EBITDA "particularly pernicious":

> "Trumpeting EBITDA is a particularly pernicious practice. Doing so implies that depreciation is not truly an expense, given that it is a 'non-cash' charge. That's nonsense." [2002]

He added that depreciation was actually a "particularly unattractive" expense because the cash outlay occurs upfront, before the asset delivers any benefit.

The 2014 letter formalized the distinction between real depreciation (a genuine economic cost that must be replaced) and non-real amortization (often a mere accounting artifact from purchase-price adjustments). Then came the devastating test:

> "When CEOs tout EBITDA as a valuation guide, wire them up for a polygraph test." [2014]

By 2024, the dismissal was complete -- and delivered with the brevity of someone who had been saying it for a quarter century:

> "EBITDA, a flawed favorite of Wall Street, is not for us." [2024]

## 4. More Money Stolen with a Pen Than a Gun [1988, 2000]

Buffett first raised the alarm about accounting fraud in 1988, noting that some managers use GAAP "to deceive, smoothing earnings or taking 'big bath' quarters, while others commit outright fraud." He quoted a principle that would recur across multiple letters:

> "It has been far safer to steal large sums with a pen than small sums with a gun." [1988]

The 2000 letter expanded this into a formal warning:

> "More money, it has been noted, has been stolen with the point of a pen than at the point of a gun." [2000]

This was not hyperbole. Buffett laid out the mechanics of how pen-based theft works: CEOs publicly predict growth rates they cannot honestly deliver, then engage in accounting games to "make the numbers." The manipulation starts small -- shifting revenue between quarters, adjusting reserves -- but it compounds because each period's fudging creates a larger hole to fill the next period.

---

## 5. The Prediction Culture: Fudging Turns to Fraud [2000, 2002]

Buffett identified CEO prediction-making as the root cause of corporate dishonesty. The chain of causation runs: prediction leads to pressure, pressure leads to manipulation, and manipulation leads to fraud.

> "Charlie and I tend to be leery of companies run by CEOs who woo investors with fancy predictions. A few of these managers will prove prophetic -- but others will turn out to be congenital optimists, or even charlatans. Unfortunately, it's not easy for investors to know in advance which species they are dealing with." [2000]

The problem is arithmetic. Predicting 15% annual earnings growth is "courting trouble because only a tiny percentage of large businesses can sustain it." When the growth inevitably stalls, the CEO faces a choice: admit failure or manipulate the numbers. The culture of Wall Street rewards the latter:

> "Only in the sales presentations of investment banks do earnings move forever upward." [2000]

The manipulation is addictive. Once earnings are moved from one period to another, future shortfalls require ever more "heroic" accounting maneuvers. This is the slippery slope from fudging to fraud [2000].

By 2002, Buffett distilled this into a single, devastating prediction:

> "Managers that always promise to 'make the numbers' will at some point be tempted to make up the numbers." [2002]

The cultural decay was pervasive. CEOs who maintained honest standards found that the ethical high road was sparsely traveled:

> "CEOs who traveled the high road did not encounter heavy traffic." [2002]

And those who drifted into dishonesty found themselves in Mae West's position:

> "I was Snow White but I drifted." [2002]

## 6. Restructuring Charges: The Golf Analogy [1992, 1998, 2000, 2015]

Buffett saw restructuring charges as one of the most common tools for earnings manipulation. The mechanism is simple: dump as many costs as possible into a single "restructuring" quarter, creating an artificially low baseline from which future quarters show impressive improvement.

The 1998 letter produced the perfect analogy:

Companies take massive one-time charges to create future earnings "reserves" -- like a golfer turning in a 140 score in round one to create a handicap that guarantees future 80s [1998].

In 1998 alone, companies took $72.1 billion in special charges. The practice was spreading because it worked: Wall Street rewarded smooth earnings trajectories and forgave "one-time" charges, no matter how frequently they recurred [1998].

The consequences of failed acquisitions were routinely buried in these charges:

> "The financial consequences of these boners are regularly dumped into massive restructuring charges or write-offs that are casually waved off as 'nonrecurring.' ... When it comes to corporate blunders, CEOs invoke the concept of the Virgin Birth." [2000]

Buffett's alternative was simple: don't create the problems in the first place. Berkshire's 25-person headquarters team had zero turnover in 2015, and Buffett noted with pride: "You have never read about 'restructuring' charges at Berkshire" [2015].

Serial acquirers who overpaid for deals faced a grimmer outcome:

> "In this corporate equivalent of a Head Start program, the CEO receives the education but the stockholders pay the tuition." [1992]

## 7. Mark-to-Myth and the Derivatives Danger [2002]

The 2002 letter contained Buffett's most extensive warning about financial instruments whose values were determined by models rather than markets. Complex derivatives that allowed earnings to be recorded before cash changed hands, and that depended on assumptions rather than transactions for valuation, were inherently dangerous:

> "In extreme cases, mark-to-model degenerates into what I would call mark-to-myth." [2002]

This was not merely an accounting concern -- it was a systemic risk. Derivatives were "financial weapons of mass destruction" whose dangers were latent but potentially lethal [2002]. Five years later, the 2008 financial crisis proved him right.

The underlying problem was incentive corruption: traders and managers who booked profits today based on models whose accuracy would not be tested for years. The auditing failure was equally profound -- complexity provided cover for manipulation. As Buffett noted:

> "If you can't understand a footnote or other managerial explanation, it's usually because the CEO doesn't want you to." [2002]

## 8. The Cockroach Theory of Accounting [1989, 2002, 2014]

When Buffett saw one accounting problem in a company, he expected more:

> "There is seldom just one cockroach in the kitchen." [2002]

He warned investors to treat visible accounting shortcuts -- refusing to expense options, using fanciful pension assumptions, trumpeting EBITDA -- as signals of deeper hidden problems. "When management takes the low road in visible aspects of accounting, they are almost certainly following a similar path behind the scenes" [2002].

The cockroach metaphor first appeared in 1989 ("In a difficult business, no sooner is one problem solved than another surfaces -- never is there just one cockroach in the kitchen") and recurred in the 2014 Tesco investment, where problems surfaced serially: "You see a cockroach in your kitchen; as the days go by, you meet his relatives" [2014].

---

## 9. Insurance Accounting: The Self-Graded Exam [1986, 2001, 2017]

Buffett saw insurance as the industry where accounting dishonesty was most dangerous and most tempting. Loss reserves are estimates made by the very people whose compensation depends on those estimates being low:

> "The loss reserves of an insurer are similar to a self-graded exam. Ignorance, wishful thinking or, occasionally, downright fraud can deliver inaccurate figures about an insurer's financial condition for a very long time." [2017]

He attacked the euphemisms the industry used to disguise its failures:

> "When insurance executives belatedly establish proper reserves, they often speak of 'reserve strengthening,' a term that has a rather noble ring to it... the term is a euphemism for what should more properly be called 'correction of previous untruths.'" [1986]

In 2001, he expanded the critique: "Insurance accounting is a self-graded exam," and auditors "generally don't get an argument" when companies submit their own reserve estimates [2001]. Bad terminology enabled bad thinking -- terms like "loss development" and "reserve strengthening" were designed to make people "unthinkingly accept concepts that are dangerously flawed" [2001].

---

## 10. Pension Return Assumptions: The Time-Delayed Lie [2007]

Corporate America's use of inflated pension return assumptions (8%+) to boost reported earnings was, in Buffett's view, one of the most insidious forms of accounting manipulation. CEOs chose high return assumptions because "the consequences of being wrong will only appear after they retire" [2007].

This was a perfect example of the accounting culture Buffett despised: decisions that flattered current earnings while pushing the consequences to the future. The 2007 letter noted that the same mentality drove the resistance to stock option expensing -- only 2 of 500 S&P companies voluntarily expensed options when given the choice [2007].

Senator Alan Simpson's quote, which Buffett used to frame his critique, applied equally to Wall Street:

> "Those who travel the high road in Washington need not fear heavy traffic." [2007]

---

## 11. Equal and Simultaneous Disclosure [1998, 2000]

Buffett demanded that all shareholders receive the same information at the same time -- what he called the golden rule of corporate disclosure:

> "At Berkshire, full reporting means giving you the information that we would wish you to give to us if our positions were reversed." [2000]

He attacked the common practice of "selective disclosure" -- giving analysts and institutional investors hints, winks, and nods about earnings before the general public knew. The fact that reform required SEC coercion (Regulation FD) rather than corporate conscience was, he said, "a matter of shame" [2000].

Every shareholder, from the holder of one B share to the largest institution, deserved equal access [1998].

---

## 12. Honest Error Reporting: The "Mistake" Count [1984, 2024]

Buffett's most distinctive transparency practice was his willingness to publicly name, quantify, and take responsibility for his mistakes. This was not false modesty -- it was a deliberate institutional practice.

In 1984, he presented shareholders with a table showing how reported underwriting results compared to corrected figures one year later, admitting consistent errors that made results look better than they were. He committed to continuing to report these errors annually [1984].

By 2024, he formalized the contrast with the rest of Corporate America:

Buffett used the words "mistake" or "error" 16 times in his 2019-2023 letters. Many huge companies never used either word. He criticized board meetings and analyst calls where "mistake" and "wrong" were forbidden words [2024].

The danger of this silence was not just external -- it was internal:

> "If you start fooling your shareholders, you will soon believe your own baloney and be fooling yourself as well." [2024]

---

## 13. Pro-Forma Honesty: Adjusting in Both Directions [2002]

Buffett noticed that while companies routinely excluded bad news from their "pro-forma" or "adjusted" earnings, no one ever adjusted downward when results benefited from favorable breaks. He broke this pattern in 2002 by voluntarily noting that Berkshire's results were aided by the absence of megacatastrophes and favorable bond market conditions [2002].

He parodied the corporate practice: "In these presentations, the CEO tells his owners 'don't count this, don't count that -- just count what makes earnings fat'" [2002].

True "normalized" earnings, Buffett insisted, must account for deviations in both directions. Management that only adjusts favorably is not providing transparency -- it is providing propaganda [2002].

---

## 14. Unintelligible Disclosure as a Red Flag [2002]

Clarity of communication became, in Buffett's framework, a direct test of management integrity:

> "If you can't understand a footnote or other managerial explanation, it's usually because the CEO doesn't want you to." [2002]

Honest leaders explain complex situations in plain language. Those who hide behind jargon and complexity are usually hiding something substantive. This principle applied equally to derivatives disclosures, pension assumptions, and restructuring charges [2002].

---

## The Complete Accounting Honesty Framework

Across 49 years of letters, Buffett built a unified theory of honest accounting and transparent communication:

1. **Translate accounting to economics.** GAAP records; the investor's job is to evaluate. Owner earnings, not reported earnings, reveal reality [1986].

2. **Recognize all real costs.** Stock options are compensation. Depreciation is a real expense. Ignoring either one inflates reported earnings and misleads capital allocation [1992, 2000, 2014].

3. **Never predict.** Public growth predictions create pressure to manipulate, and manipulation becomes addiction [2000, 2002].

4. **Disclose equally and simultaneously.** The golden rule of disclosure: treat all shareholders as you would wish to be treated [2000].

5. **Admit mistakes by name.** The word "mistake" appeared 16 times in five years of Buffett's letters; at many large companies, it appeared zero times [2024].

6. **Watch for cockroaches.** One visible accounting shortcut signals hidden ones. Weak accounting is a proxy for weak integrity [2002].

7. **Be skeptical of precision.** It is better to be vaguely right than precisely wrong -- whether valuing a business, pricing an option, or estimating reserves [1986, 1993, 2010].
